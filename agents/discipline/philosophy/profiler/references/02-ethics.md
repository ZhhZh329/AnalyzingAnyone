# Branch 2: Ethics — What Should We Do?

## Overview

This branch maps the landscape of **normative ethics**: frameworks for determining what actions are right, what character is virtuous, and how we ought to live. The major schools differ fundamentally in what they take as foundational (character, rules, consequences, relationships, contracts, or moral facts), but most people integrate insights from multiple frameworks without consciously realizing it.

**SEP Critical Note**: "Any plausible normative ethical theory will have something to say about virtues, consequences, AND rules. The three major frameworks are distinguished by which concept is FOUNDATIONAL, not by exclusive concern." Most people operate with intuitive hybrids, and ethical profiling must distinguish between shallow eclecticism and genuine integration.

## 1. Virtue Ethics

**SEP Reference**: `ethics-virtue` (Anscombe, Hursthouse, Foot, MacIntyre, Nussbaum)

### Core Thesis
Morality is fundamentally about **developing good character and achieving human excellence (arete)**. Right action flows from virtuous character; virtue is cultivated through habit, practice, and practical wisdom (phronesis). The question "What should I do?" is secondary to "What kind of person should I be?" Eudaimonia (flourishing/living well) is the ultimate good, not happiness in a hedonic sense.

### Key Thinkers
- **Aristotle**: Virtues as mean between extremes; habituation; phronesis as practical reasoning; eudaimonia as telos.
- **Elizabeth Anscombe**: Criticized act-centered ethics; revived virtue language; emphasized intentionality.
- **Philippa Foot**: Virtues as traits that benefit their possessor and others; "natural goodness"; virtues as correctives to natural human defects.
- **Alasdair MacIntyre**: Virtues embedded in social practices and traditions; critique of modern moral fragmentation; internal goods vs. external rewards.
- **Rosalind Hursthouse**: Contemporary virtue ethics; virtues in moral education; virtue theory in applied contexts.

### Main Arguments
1. **Character-based approach**: Morality is about what kind of person you are becoming, not just isolated acts. A virtuous person will reliably do the right thing because their character is reliable.
2. **Phronesis**: Right action requires practical wisdom—the ability to perceive what matters in particular situations and respond appropriately. This cannot be reduced to rules or formulas.
3. **Natural human goods**: Virtues are excellences relative to human nature and human flourishing. They perfect capacities that are distinctively human.
4. **Integration of emotion and reason**: Virtue ethics rejects sharp divisions between rational and emotional capacities. A truly virtuous person *feels* correctly, not just thinks correctly.
5. **Teleology**: Human beings have a function or telos; virtue is fulfilling that function excellently.

### Key Objections
1. **The problem of circular definition**: "What is virtue?" → "What a virtuous person would do." → "What is a virtuous person?" This appears circular until phronesis is properly understood.
2. **Cultural variation objection**: If virtue is culturally embedded (per MacIntyre), how can we adjudicate between different virtue traditions? Does virtue ethics collapse into relativism?
3. **Motivational gap**: Does knowing the virtuous character traits tell us what to do in a novel situation? The theory can seem actionable only post-hoc.
4. **The demandingness question**: If virtue requires constant self-cultivation, is it too demanding for ordinary moral agents?
5. **Applied ethics problem**: Virtue ethics seems strongest in character development but weaker in resolving specific dilemmas (e.g., abortion, resource allocation) where competing virtues clash.
6. **Eudaimonia ambiguity**: Is flourishing a moral concept or prudential? Can a flourishing life be immoral?

### Diagnostic Markers

| Signal | Strength | Plain Language |
|--------|----------|---|
| "I'm trying to be a good person" | WEAK–MODERATE | Moral orientation present but not philosophically grounded |
| "What would a wise person do here?" | MODERATE–STRONG | Phronesis reasoning; appeals to exemplar |
| "What does this say about the kind of person I'd be?" | STRONG | Character-centered reasoning; self-understanding |
| "Different situations call for different responses; you need judgment" | MODERATE–STRONG | Recognizes particularity; implies phronesis |
| "The goal is to live a good life, not just follow rules" | MODERATE–STRONG | Telos-oriented; rejects rule-centrism |
| "I admire how [person] handles these things" | MODERATE | Model-based learning; exemplar reliance |
| "Virtue is cultivated through practice" | STRONG | Explicitly Aristotelian habituation view |
| "You can't teach virtue through rules alone" | MODERATE–STRONG | Skepticism of codification; emphasis on practice |
| "It depends on your character and intentions" | WEAK–MODERATE | Character focus but may lack philosophical depth |
| "Excellence in human functioning" | STRONG | Explicit arete/eudaimonia language |

### Common Confusions
1. **Virtue ≠ Victorianism or Puritan morality**: Virtue ethics is not about restrictive social conformity. Aristotle values courage, honor, and even appropriate anger.
2. **Virtue ≠ being "nice"**: Virtues include justice, which can demand uncomfortable confrontation. Foot's "natural goodness" rejects sentimentality.
3. **Phronesis ≠ intuition or mere feeling**: Phronesis is learned, reason-informed judgment, not gut instinct.
4. **Virtue ≠ consequentialist agent**: A virtue ethicist cares about outcomes, but right action isn't defined by optimal consequences—it flows from character.
5. **Eudaimonia ≠ happiness** (in modern psychological sense): It's closer to "fulfillment" or "living well" in the sense of achieving one's potential.

### Cross-Branch Correlations
- **Virtue Ethics + Deontology**: Many deontologists (esp. Ross, contemporary contractarians) incorporate virtue language. Duty and virtue are compatible; both emphasize what we are obligated to do.
- **Virtue Ethics + Consequentialism**: Virtue consequentialists argue that cultivating virtuous character has the best consequences. Virtues are instrumentally valuable.
- **Virtue Ethics + Care Ethics**: Both emphasize particularity, relationships, and emotional attunement. Care ethics can be read as a virtue account focused on attentiveness and responsiveness.
- **Virtue Ethics + Natural Law**: Both appeal to human nature and telos, though they differ on the role of divine will and universal human goods.
- **Virtue Ethics ↔ Contractarianism**: Tension here; contractarians often see virtue as instrumentally useful within a contract framework, not foundational.

### Internal Variants
1. **Aristotelian virtue ethics**: Telos, phronesis, eudaimonia as an activity of soul in accordance with virtue (Aristotle, Foot, Hursthouse).
2. **Neo-Aristotelian natural law**: Finnis, Grisez; virtues as means to basic human goods within a natural law framework.
3. **MacIntyrean virtue ethics**: Virtues as embedded in social practices and traditions; rejects modern individualism; skeptical of universal virtue lists.
4. **Particularist virtue ethics**: Challenges the idea of stable virtue traits; emphasizes context and responsiveness (some readings of Dancy overlap here).
5. **Virtue reliabilism**: Virtue as an intellectual or moral capacity that reliably produces good judgment or right action.

## 2. Deontology (Duty-Based Ethics)

**SEP Reference**: `ethics-deontological` (Kant, Ross, contemporary meta-ethics)

### Core Thesis
Morality is fundamentally about **duties, obligations, and rights**. An action is right if it conforms to the correct moral rules or principles, regardless of consequences. Certain acts are categorically forbidden (e.g., lying, breaking promises, killing innocents) not because of bad outcomes but because they violate duties. The agent's will, intention, and respect for moral law are central.

### Key Thinkers
- **Immanuel Kant**: The categorical imperative; duty as the ground of morality; the agent's maxim (subjective principle); treating humanity as an end, never merely as a means; autonomy as foundational.
- **W.D. Ross**: Prima facie duties (promise-keeping, beneficence, justice, reparation, gratitude, non-maleficence, self-improvement); duties can conflict, requiring judgment to determine actual duty in a case.
- **Thomas Scanlon**: Contractualism; an action is wrong if it violates principles that could not be reasonably rejected by anyone who is aiming for principles that could be justified to all.
- **Christine Korsgaard**: Kantian constructivism; self-constitution through moral choice; the source of normativity in practical identity.

### Core Structures
1. **Agent-centered deontology**: Emphasizes the agent's duties (what I must and must not do). Kantian.
2. **Patient-centered deontology**: Emphasizes rights of patients/recipients (what others can demand). Can ground strong rights protections.
3. **Contractualist deontology**: Duties derive from what would be justified to all reasonable agents under fair conditions (Rawls, Scanlon).
4. **Rule-based deontology**: Rules (don't lie, keep promises) are foundational; consequences matter only in rule-selection, not in act evaluation.

### Main Arguments
1. **Categorical imperatives**: Some acts are intrinsically wrong, not merely imprudent. Lying is wrong even if it produces good outcomes.
2. **Respect for persons**: Deontology treats people as ends in themselves with inviolable rights, not as mere means to maximizing welfare.
3. **Agent responsibility**: Deontology focuses on what the agent does and intends, distinguishing between doing harm and allowing harm; between intended and foreseen consequences.
4. **Moral constraints**: Deontology posits "constraints" on what agents may do, even for excellent outcomes (e.g., "You must not kill an innocent person to save five others").
5. **Autonomy**: Moral agents have dignity and autonomy; they cannot be overridden for the greater good.
6. **Universalizability**: If an action is right for one agent in a situation, it must be right for any agent in relevantly similar circumstances.

### Key Objections
1. **The demandingness of duty**: If deontology is strictly agent-centered, doesn't it demand constant moral excellence? But humans are limited, and ethics should be achievable.
2. **The problem of conflicting duties**: Ross introduced prima facie duties to allow for conflict, but how do we adjudicate between them? The theory seems to lack a decision procedure.
3. **Integrity vs. compliance**: Does deontology demand that agents become moral automatons, purely committed to rule-following? Does it value integrity or just conformity?
4. **Consequences matter (surely)**: The objection that deontology ignores consequences seems obvious. A theory that says "keep your promise" even if the consequences are catastrophic appears divorced from reality.
5. **The problem of moral realism**: How do we know what duties are? Are they objective, divinely ordained, rationally constructed, or what? Deontology seems to presuppose moral realism but provides no clear ontology.
6. **Rights inflation**: Patient-centered deontology can lead to a proliferation of supposed rights with unclear foundations.

### Diagnostic Markers

| Signal | Strength | Plain Language |
|--------|----------|---|
| "Some things are just wrong, period" | STRONG | Categorical prohibition; no outcome-justification |
| "Could everyone do that?" (Kantian test) | STRONG | Categorical imperative reasoning; universality test |
| "You can't use people as mere means" | STRONG | Respect for persons; Kantian dignity |
| "I have a duty to keep my word" | MODERATE–STRONG | Agent-centered obligation |
| "Some rights are inviolable" | MODERATE–STRONG | Rights-based; patient-centered focus |
| "It's about doing what's right, not what works" | MODERATE–STRONG | Duty > consequences |
| "The principle is the thing" | MODERATE | Rule-centrism; abstract principles |
| "What were your intentions?" | MODERATE | Focus on agency and intention |
| "Rules exist for a reason" | WEAK–MODERATE | Rule-respect but not necessarily deontological |
| "You're entitled to do that, even if it's harmful" | MODERATE | Rights-based; patient focus |
| "Fairness demands equal treatment" | MODERATE | Contractualist deontology |
| "That violates my integrity" | WEAK–MODERATE | Autonomy concern; could be deontological or virtue-based |

### Common Confusions
1. **Deontology ≠ rule worship or moral rigidity**: Ross's account explicitly allows for prima facie conflicts and judgment. Modern deontologists reject inflexible rule-following.
2. **Deontology ≠ indifference to consequences**: Deontologists care about consequences; they just don't define rightness by optimal outcomes.
3. **Duty ≠ burden or unpleasantness**: A Kantian duty can be joyful and self-fulfilling. Duty and inclination are not opposed.
4. **Deontology ≠ legalism or legalistic thinking**: A deontological morality is not the same as legal positivism. Moral duties can diverge from laws.
5. **Rights ≠ absolute**: Many deontologists allow for limited rights violations in extreme circumstances (Scanlon's "reasonable rejection" test permits some flexibility).

### Cross-Branch Correlations
- **Deontology + Virtue Ethics**: Virtues can be understood deontologically (as excellences in following duty) or vice versa (duty as an expression of virtue). Ross and contemporary virtue ethicists often see complementarity.
- **Deontology + Consequentialism**: Deontological constraints can be justified consequentially (rule consequentialism argues that following certain rules has best outcomes).
- **Deontology + Care Ethics**: Tension here; care ethics emphasizes particularity and relationships, while deontology emphasizes universal principles. Some feminists argue care ethics reveals deontological reliance on impartial rules.
- **Deontology + Contractarianism**: Contractarian deontology (Scanlon, Rawls) is a major contemporary variant. Duties are grounded in hypothetical agreement.
- **Deontology + Moral Realism**: Most traditional deontologists (Kant, Ross) posit objective moral facts. But constructivist deontologists (Korsgaard) reject metaphysical realism.

### Internal Variants
1. **Kantian deontology**: Categorical imperative; autonomy; universality; agent-centered; focus on maxims and will.
2. **Rossian deontology**: Prima facie duties; pluralistic principles; intuitionism about duty; more modest metaphysical claims.
3. **Contractualist deontology**: Scanlon, Rawls; duties derive from principles no one could reasonably reject; emphasizes fairness and justification to all.
4. **Rights-based deontology**: Natural rights tradition; patients' rights are foundational (Locke, Nozick, contemporary libertarians).
5. **Constructivist deontology**: Korsgaard; moral principles are constructed through practical reasoning and self-constitution, not discovered.

## 3. Consequentialism & Utilitarianism

**SEP Reference**: `consequentialism` (Bentham, Mill, Sidgwick, Singer, Parfit)

### Core Thesis
The morality of an action is determined entirely by its **consequences**. An action is right if and only if it produces the best outcomes (or among the best outcomes). The good is defined independently of the right (e.g., happiness, preference satisfaction, objective goods), and the right is what produces the most good. Intentions, duties, virtues, and character are morally relevant only insofar as they affect outcomes.

### Key Thinkers
- **Jeremy Bentham**: Utilitarianism; the principle of utility; the greatest happiness of the greatest number; hedonic calculus; animal sentience matters.
- **John Stuart Mill**: Refined utilitarianism; qualitative vs. quantitative pleasures; higher pleasures (intellectual, moral); rule utilitarianism; liberal commitments.
- **Henry Sidgwick**: The methods of ethics (egoism, utilitarianism, intuitionism); dualism of practical reason; rational egoism as a fundamental problem.
- **Peter Singer**: Contemporary utilitarianism; sentience as the basis for moral consideration; animal liberation; effective altruism; demanding nature of impartial consequentialism.
- **Derek Parfit**: Consequentialism and metaphysics; personal identity and impersonal value; reductionism; the non-identity problem.

### Core Structures
1. **Act consequentialism**: An act is right if it produces the best consequences among the available acts. Evaluate each act individually.
2. **Rule consequentialism**: An act is right if it conforms to a rule whose general acceptance would produce the best consequences.
3. **Hedonistic consequentialism**: The good is pleasure/happiness (Bentham, early Mill).
4. **Preference satisfaction**: The good is the satisfaction of agents' preferences (modern utilitarians).
5. **Objective list theories**: The good consists in objectively valuable states (knowledge, virtue, relationships, etc.), not just subjective experiences.
6. **Maximizing vs. satisficing**: Maximize the good (produce the absolute best outcome) vs. satisfice (produce an outcome good enough).

### Main Arguments
1. **Impartiality and impersonal value**: Morality is impartial; my happiness counts no more than anyone else's. What matters is the total good, not whose good it is.
2. **Consequentialism is common sense**: We ordinarily ask, "What are the consequences?" before acting. Morality should be continuous with practical reasoning.
3. **Only consequences matter morally**: Intentions can be virtuous but lead to bad outcomes. Rules can be well-intentioned but produce suffering. Only outcomes make moral truth.
4. **Rationality of benevolence**: If reason requires impartial concern for all sentient beings' welfare, then consequentialism follows.
5. **Aggregation principle**: The right action is the one that maximizes the total or average good, even if some are sacrificed.
6. **Efficiency and effectiveness**: Consequentialism demands that we pursue moral goals effectively; inefficient rule-following is irrational.

### Key Objections
1. **The demandingness objection**: Consequentialism requires constant self-sacrifice to maximize good. You should donate almost all your income to effective charities. This seems psychologically unrealistic and morally excessive.
2. **Integrity and separateness of persons**: Consequentialism can justify using innocent people as means (e.g., harvesting organs to save five others). It violates the separateness of persons and individual integrity.
3. **Partiality and special relationships**: Morality seems to recognize special duties to family, friends, and community. Strict consequentialism treats all beings as fungible.
4. **The problem of moral knowledge**: How do we predict consequences accurately? Consequentialism can seem paralyzing in practice.
5. **The calculation problem**: In complex situations, consequences branch infinitely. There's no decision procedure for determining which act is right.
6. **Happiness as a measure**: Is happiness/pleasure really all that matters? What of virtue, knowledge, accomplishment, relationships valued for their own sake?
7. **Rights violations**: Consequentialism can justify slavery, torture, or killing if the aggregate good is large enough. This seems to abandon fundamental commitments to justice and rights.
8. **The sorites problem and measurement**: How do we measure and compare pleasures or preferences? A utilitarian calculus seems impossible in practice.

### Diagnostic Markers

| Signal | Strength | Plain Language |
|--------|----------|---|
| "What matters is the outcome" | STRONG | Consequences foundational |
| "The greatest good for the greatest number" | STRONG | Classic utilitarian formulation |
| "It's about reducing suffering" | MODERATE–STRONG | Consequentialist concern; focus on welfare |
| "We should do what produces the best results" | MODERATE–STRONG | Outcome-oriented |
| "Intentions don't matter; results do" | STRONG | Explicit consequentialism |
| "If it produces more happiness overall, it's right" | STRONG | Utilitarian reasoning |
| "We have to think about the bigger picture" | WEAK–MODERATE | Aggregative thinking; could be consequentialist or prudential |
| "The greatest happiness of the greatest number" | VERY STRONG | Direct Benthamite language |
| "What's the long-term effect?" | WEAK–MODERATE | Consequentialist concern but not philosophically distinctive |
| "Effective altruism demands we maximize good per dollar" | STRONG | Modern consequentialist reasoning |
| "Everyone's welfare counts equally" | MODERATE–STRONG | Impartiality; utilitarian principle |
| "Rules are just tools for better outcomes" | STRONG | Consequentialist instrumentalism |

### Common Confusions
1. **Consequentialism ≠ pragmatism**: Pragmatism asks what works; consequentialism asks what produces the best outcomes, which is more specific.
2. **Consequentialism ≠ egoism**: A consequentialist treats their own good as one good among others. An egoist prioritizes their own good.
3. **Utilitarianism ≠ happiness**: Modern utilitarians often focus on preference satisfaction or objective goods, not subjective happiness.
4. **Consequentialism ≠ indifference to rights**: Some consequentialists argue that protecting rights has best consequences; they can accommodate strong rights-protections.
5. **Rule consequentialism ≠ deontology**: Rule consequentialism is still consequentialist (rules are justified by outcomes), not deontological (duties are foundational).
6. **Consequentialism ≠ cost-benefit analysis**: Moral consequentialism is impartial and agent-neutral; pragmatic cost-benefit analysis may be partial and self-interested.

### Cross-Branch Correlations
- **Consequentialism + Virtue Ethics**: Virtue consequentialists argue that virtuous character produces best outcomes. Virtues are instrumentally valuable for good consequences.
- **Consequentialism + Deontology**: Act consequentialism vs. rule consequentialism creates a bridge; rule consequentialists say "follow these rules" which resembles deontological thinking.
- **Consequentialism + Care Ethics**: Tension here; consequentialism is impartial, while care ethics emphasizes particular relationships. Some argue care ethics is consequentialist about relational goods.
- **Consequentialism + Contractarianism**: Both can be combined; social contracts are justified if they produce best consequences. Rawls is often read as a contractarian consequentialist.
- **Consequentialism ↔ Moral Realism**: Most classical consequentialists posit objective facts about welfare or good. But non-cognitivist consequentialists also exist.

### Internal Variants
1. **Classical utilitarianism**: Hedonistic; maximizing; Bentham's version emphasizes pleasure as the ultimate good.
2. **Mill's higher utilitarianism**: Qualitative pleasures; cultivation of faculties; less demanding than Bentham.
3. **Preference satisfaction**: Welfare is preference satisfaction, not subjective pleasure (Hare, modern economists).
4. **Objective list theories**: Welfare includes knowledge, virtue, relationships (Parfit, Sen, Nussbaum).
5. **Rule consequentialism**: Rules are justified by their consequences; an act is right if it conforms to optimal rules (Hooker, Railton).
6. **Two-level consequentialism**: Hare's theory: use intuitive thinking and deontological rules in practice; use consequentialism in critical reflection.
7. **Effective altruism**: Modern movement applying utilitarian reasoning to cause prioritization and resource allocation; focuses on tractability, scale, and neglectedness.

## 4. Care Ethics

**SEP Reference**: `feminism-ethics` (Gilligan, Noddings, Held, Ruddick)

### Core Thesis
Morality is fundamentally about **relationships, responsiveness, and attentiveness** to particular others' needs and contexts. The care perspective rejects abstraction from relationships and emphasizes the moral significance of interdependence, emotion, and contextual judgment. Right action flows from caring relationship and attentiveness to what matters in a particular situation, not from universal principles or impartial calculation.

### Key Thinkers
- **Carol Gilligan**: "In a Different Voice"; distinguished care ethic from justice/rights ethic in moral development; challenged Kohlberg's stage theory; context-sensitivity and relationship-focus.
- **Nel Noddings**: Caring relation as the moral primitive; caring-about and caring-for; displacement of other; fidelity and responsiveness; natural caring and ethical caring.
- **Virginia Held**: Care as a moral value, not just virtue; care work in families and communities; relational autonomy; interdependence and dependency.
- **Sara Ruddick**: Maternal thinking; preservative love, growth, and social acceptance; practical reasoning in parenting.
- **Alison Jaggar, Eva Feder Kittay**: Feminist epistemology and emotion; emotion as moral epistemically significant; the ethically dependent and vulnerability.

### Main Arguments
1. **Relationships are primary**: Morality emerges from and is grounded in caring relationships, not in abstract individuals considering universal principles.
2. **Contextuality and particularity**: Moral situations are embedded in particular relationships and contexts. Abstract principles miss morally relevant details.
3. **Emotion and responsiveness**: Emotions (compassion, empathy, concern) are morally significant and epistemically important. They attune us to others' needs.
4. **Interdependence and care work**: Human beings are fundamentally interdependent; care work (raising children, nursing the sick) is central to morality, not peripheral.
5. **Attentiveness and nonviolence**: Caring requires attentiveness to others and a commitment to nonviolence and growth.
6. **Rejecting impartiality**: The care ethicist rejects the ideal of impartial, agent-neutral morality. I owe more to my children than to strangers—and this is appropriate, not a moral failing.
7. **Reconstructing autonomy**: Rather than atomistic independence, autonomy is relational; we are constituted through relationships.

### Key Objections
1. **Parochialism and nepotism**: If care ethics privileges particular relationships, doesn't it license partiality that contradicts justice? Can care ethics ground obligations to strangers and distant others?
2. **Integration with justice**: How do we integrate care and justice? Some worry care ethics becomes a vehicle for women's expected self-sacrifice.
3. **Universalization and fairness**: How can care ethics adjudicate between competing caring relationships? If care is particularistic, it seems to lack the impartiality that fairness requires.
4. **Emotional unreliability**: Emotions can mislead. Why privilege emotional attunement over principled reasoning?
5. **Applied ethics in large-scale societies**: Care ethics seems best suited to small-scale relationships (families, communities). How does it scale to policy, law, or global justice?
6. **Gender concerns**: Is care ethics reinforcing traditional gender roles by valorizing caring as women's work? Or does it liberate by recognizing care's moral importance?
7. **The moral status of the uncared-for**: If morality is grounded in caring relationships, what about those no one cares for? They seem to have no moral status.

### Diagnostic Markers

| Signal | Strength | Plain Language |
|--------|----------|---|
| "It depends on the relationship" | MODERATE–STRONG | Particularism; relational focus |
| "What does this person need?" | MODERATE | Attentiveness and responsiveness |
| "Rules don't capture what matters in this situation" | MODERATE–STRONG | Skepticism of universalism; contextuality |
| "You have to listen and be responsive" | MODERATE | Attunement and care focus |
| "I care about what happens to them" | WEAK–MODERATE | Affective concern; not philosophically distinctive |
| "Relationships are what matter most" | MODERATE | Relational primacy |
| "It's about maintaining connection and growth" | MODERATE–STRONG | Care ethic language; relational focus |
| "You can't reduce this to a principle" | MODERATE | Anti-principle stance; contextuality |
| "Empathy and understanding are key" | WEAK–MODERATE | Emotional attunement; could be care-based or virtue-based |
| "The system is ignoring vulnerable people" | MODERATE | Care concern for dependency and vulnerability |
| "Justice and fairness aren't enough" | MODERATE–STRONG | Critique of impartial principles; care priority |
| "Caring requires attentiveness" | STRONG | Core care ethics concept |

### Common Confusions
1. **Care ≠ sentimentality or niceness**: Care ethics is not about being agreeable; caring can involve confrontation and refusal when growth requires it.
2. **Particularism ≠ relativism**: Care ethicists are not moral relativists. They argue particular relationships have objective moral significance.
3. **Care ≠ maternal sentimentality**: While Noddings uses maternal caring as a model, care ethics is not reducible to sentimentalism or traditional motherhood.
4. **Care ≠ emotion over reason**: Care ethicists argue emotion is reason-informed; emotions can be epistemically and morally reliable.
5. **Contextuality ≠ no principles**: Care ethicists can appeal to principles (e.g., "minimize harm," "preserve relationship") without reducing morality to abstract principles.

### Cross-Branch Correlations
- **Care Ethics + Virtue Ethics**: Both emphasize particularism, character traits, and emotional-rational integration. Some care ethicists (e.g., Held) are also virtue ethicists. Care can be understood as a virtue.
- **Care Ethics + Deontology**: Tension here. Care is particularistic; deontology universal. But some deontologists argue caring relationships generate agent-centered duties.
- **Care Ethics + Consequentialism**: Care consequentialists argue that maintaining caring relationships has best outcomes. But traditional care ethics resists outcome-oriented reasoning.
- **Care Ethics + Contractarianism**: Tension; contracts assume independent individuals. Care ethicists critique this assumption, but contractarians might ground contracts in interdependence.
- **Care Ethics + Moral Realism**: Care ethicists can be moral realists (objective moral facts about relationships) or anti-realists (moral claims express attitudes toward relationships).

### Internal Variants
1. **Noddings's caring-relations ethics**: Caring relationship as moral primitive; caring-about and caring-for distinguished; ideal of caring.
2. **Held's care as a value**: Care as comparable to justice and autonomy; not just an ethic for private life but a framework for social policy.
3. **Maternal ethics**: Ruddick; morality grounded in practices of care (mothering, nursing); preservative love, fostering growth, social training.
4. **Relational autonomy ethics**: Autonomy as constituted through relationships, not opposed to care (Meyers, Oshana).
5. **Intersectional care ethics**: Attends to how care is distributed differently across race, class, gender; critiques white, middle-class assumptions in care ethics.

## 5. Contractarianism & Contractualism

**SEP Reference**: `contractarianism` (Hobbes, Rawls, Scanlon, Gauthier, contemporary political philosophy)

### Core Thesis
Morality and justice are grounded in a **hypothetical or actual agreement** among self-interested or reasonable agents. An action is right if it conforms to principles that would be agreed to under fair conditions (or that could not be reasonably rejected). The foundation of obligation is mutual advantage, fair reciprocity, or reasonable justifiability to all, not external moral facts or human nature.

### Key Thinkers
- **Thomas Hobbes**: State of nature; mutual advantage; the sovereign; psychological egoism; peace as the rational good.
- **John Rawls**: The original position; veil of ignorance; the difference principle; justice as fairness; liberal egalitarianism.
- **David Gauthier**: Contractarianism as moral theory; rational bargaining; morals by agreement; constraints on rational pursuit of advantage.
- **Thomas Scanlon**: Contractualism; an action is wrong if it violates principles that no one could reasonably reject; focuses on justifiability to all; reasonable rejectability test.
- **Jean Hampton**: Contractarianism and the sensible knave; mutual advantage and fairness; critique and defense of contractarianism.

### Core Structures
1. **Mutual advantage contractarianism**: Hobbes, Gauthier; morality is justified because it benefits all parties compared to a state of nature or non-cooperation.
2. **Fair bargaining contractarianism**: Rawls, Scanlon; morality is grounded in principles that could be agreed to or justified to all under fair conditions.
3. **Constructivism**: Principles are constructed through hypothetical reasoning, not discovered as pre-existing facts.
4. **Hobbesian social contract**: Sovereign authority; escape from state of nature; strong version: individuals agree to be bound unconditionally.
5. **Lockean social contract**: Individuals retain natural rights; government justified if it protects pre-political rights; limited sovereignty.
6. **Rawlsian original position**: Hypothetical choice situation behind a veil of ignorance; principles chosen from ignorance of one's place in society.

### Main Arguments
1. **Mutual advantage and rationality**: It is rational to cooperate with others on mutually advantageous terms. Morality is the rational solution to coordination problems.
2. **Legitimacy through agreement**: Principles that all would agree to (or could not reasonably reject) are legitimate and binding.
3. **Constructivism over moral realism**: We don't discover moral truths; we construct principles through hypothetical reasoning. This avoids metaphysical commitments.
4. **Impartiality through procedure**: The fairness of the choice situation (veil of ignorance, reasonableness criterion) ensures impartial principles.
5. **Reconciling self-interest and morality**: Contractarianism shows how self-interested agents can rationally commit to moral principles.
6. **Consensus and pluralism**: Contractarianism accommodates reasonable pluralism; it asks what all could agree to, not what is objectively true.

### Key Objections
1. **The non-identity problem**: Those not actually party to the contract (children, future generations, animals) may not be bound by it. How do their moral status follow?
2. **Moral realism challenge**: Does contractarianism really avoid objective moral facts? Fairness, reasonableness, and natural rights are themselves substantive moral concepts.
3. **Circularity**: Contractarians must specify what counts as "fair" choice conditions or "reasonable" rejection. But this seems to presuppose the very moral principles that are supposed to be justified by the contract.
4. **Contingency and moral truth**: If morality is grounded in hypothetical agreement, isn't it contingent on what parties would agree to? This seems to make morality radically conditional.
5. **Hobbesian violence**: Hobbes's theory seems to justify absolute sovereignty and obedience, which many find abhorrent.
6. **Justice as fairness concerns**: Some argue Rawls's difference principle inadequately addresses exploitation; others worry about justice to future generations and non-parties.
7. **Stability and motivation**: Why should anyone be motivated to uphold a contract if they are not bound by actual agreement? What ensures stability?

### Diagnostic Markers

| Signal | Strength | Plain Language |
|--------|----------|---|
| "We all have to agree on principles" | MODERATE | Consensus-seeking; contractarian concern |
| "Fairness means what everyone could agree to" | MODERATE–STRONG | Contractualist reasoning; fair bargaining |
| "If you wouldn't want it done to you, don't do it" (mutual advantage) | WEAK–MODERATE | Golden rule; could be contractarian or deontological |
| "Justice requires a social contract" | MODERATE–STRONG | Explicit contractarian language |
| "We're all better off under these rules" | MODERATE | Mutual advantage reasoning |
| "Rules that no one could reasonably reject" | STRONG | Scanlon's contractualism |
| "What would rational people agree to?" | MODERATE–STRONG | Contractarian reasoning about rational bargaining |
| "Justice as fairness" | STRONG | Rawlsian language |
| "Everyone deserves a fair chance" | WEAK–MODERATE | Fair opportunity; could be egalitarian or contractarian |
| "We need legitimate authority" | MODERATE | Contractarian concern for legitimacy |
| "What's the social contract here?" | MODERATE | Explicitly contractarian framing |

### Common Confusions
1. **Contractarianism ≠ actual historical contracts**: The social contract is hypothetical; there was no moment when all agreed. It's a normative device, not a historical claim.
2. **Contractarianism ≠ contractualism**: Contractarians ground morality in mutual advantage; contractualists ground it in reasonable rejection or justifiability to all. Different theories.
3. **Fairness ≠ egalitarianism**: Rawls's difference principle allows inequalities if they benefit the worst-off. This is fair but not egalitarian in a strict sense.
4. **Veil of ignorance ≠ actual ignorance**: The veil is a device for generating impartial principles, not a claim that we're actually ignorant of our place.
5. **Mutual advantage ≠ egoism**: Contractarians seek principles all can agree to; egoists pursue only their own interest. Contractarians constrain egoism through agreement.

### Cross-Branch Correlations
- **Contractarianism + Deontology**: Contractarian deontology (Scanlon, Rawls) grounds duties in what could be justified to all. This bridges contractarianism and deontological language.
- **Contractarianism + Consequentialism**: Some argue social contracts are justified consequentially; if following rules agents all agree to has best outcomes, contractarianism is instrumentally justified.
- **Contractarianism + Virtue Ethics**: Contractarians can ask what virtues support just cooperation. Virtues are instrumentally valuable for maintaining social contracts.
- **Contractarianism + Care Ethics**: Tension; contracts assume independent agents. Care ethicists critique this and ground morality in interdependence.
- **Contractarianism + Moral Realism**: Most contractarians reject moral realism; they are constructivists. But some (e.g., Hampton) argue for a realist contractarianism.

### Internal Variants
1. **Hobbesian contractarianism**: State of nature as war; sovereign authority; peace as the good; strong version demands absolute obedience.
2. **Lockean social contract**: Natural rights; limited government; individuals retain right to resist unjust authority.
3. **Rawlsian justice as fairness**: Original position; veil of ignorance; two principles of justice (equal basic liberties, difference principle); ideal theory.
4. **Scanlon's contractualism**: Wrong-making features are those that violate principles no one could reasonably reject; focuses on reasonable rejection test.
5. **Gauthier's bargaining theory**: Rational bargaining; morality as a constrained maximization of advantage; morals by agreement.
6. **Non-ideal contractualism**: Applied to real, imperfect institutions (Estlund, Carole Pateman, indigenous critique).

## 6. Moral Relativism

**SEP Reference**: `moral-relativism` (Harman, Wong, Shafer-Landau critique)

### Core Thesis
Moral truths are **not absolute or objective**; they are relative to cultures, individuals, historical periods, or conceptual schemes. What is right in one culture or for one person may be wrong in another. Morality is constituted by cultural norms, individual commitments, or frameworks—not discovered as mind-independent facts.

### Key Variants
1. **Cultural relativism**: Morality varies by culture; there is no objective standard above culture by which to judge.
2. **Individual relativism**: Morality is personal preference; what is right for you may differ from what is right for me.
3. **Meta-ethical relativism**: Moral truth is relative (not absolute or objective) but this is compatible with objectivity within a framework.
4. **Historical relativism**: Moral concepts and truths evolve over history; past moral views were neither absolutely right nor wrong, just different.

### Main Arguments
1. **Diversity of moral views**: Cultures have radically different moral codes. This suggests morality is not objective.
2. **No neutral standpoint**: We cannot step outside our cultural or conceptual framework to judge morality from a view-from-nowhere.
3. **Tolerance and humility**: Relativism counsels humility about our moral views and openness to other cultures.
4. **Coherence**: If morality is objective, how do we explain persistent moral disagreement?

### Key Objections
1. **Tolerance paradox**: Relativism seems to forbid judging other cultures as morally wrong—but doesn't relativism itself make a moral judgment?
2. **Moral progress problem**: If morality is relative, how can we speak of moral progress? Abolition of slavery was progress, not just a change.
3. **Contradiction**: If morality is relative and "X is right in culture A" and "X is wrong in culture B," then X is both right and wrong. This seems incoherent.
4. **The adequacy problem**: Doesn't relativism fail to condemn slavery, genocide, or extreme suffering? We want to say these are wrong, period.
5. **Internal contradiction within cultures**: Even within a culture, there are dissenters. Whose culture-relative morality wins?

### Diagnostic Markers

| Signal | Strength | Plain Language |
|--------|----------|---|
| "Morality is just cultural norms" | MODERATE–STRONG | Cultural relativism |
| "Right and wrong depend on perspective" | MODERATE | Relativist claim |
| "What's true for you may not be true for me" (morally) | MODERATE–STRONG | Individual or meta-ethical relativism |
| "There's no absolute standard" | MODERATE | Anti-realism; could be relativist or expressivist |
| "All cultures are equally valid" | WEAK–MODERATE | Tolerance; not necessarily relativist (could be universalist) |
| "Morality has evolved over time" | WEAK | Historical observation; not necessarily relativist |
| "Who are we to judge other cultures?" | WEAK–MODERATE | Tolerance; could reflect relativism or humility |

### Common Confusions
1. **Relativism ≠ tolerance**: You can be universalist and tolerant; tolerance doesn't require moral relativism.
2. **Diversity ≠ relativism**: That cultures differ morally doesn't entail relativism; universalists explain diversity as error or misapplication of universal principles.
3. **Historical change ≠ relativism**: That morality evolves doesn't mean it's relative; moral progress is compatible with universalism.
4. **Subjectivity ≠ relativism**: Subjectivity (morality is about attitudes, not facts) doesn't entail relativism (it could be universal attitudes).

### Cross-Branch Correlations
- **Moral Relativism ↔ Moral Realism**: These are opposed. Relativism denies objective moral facts; realism affirms them.
- **Moral Relativism + Emotivism**: Both deny that moral claims express objective truths, though they differ on what morality expresses.
- **Moral Relativism + Virtue Ethics**: Tension; virtue ethics appeals to human nature or telos (which seem universal). But MacIntyre's social-practice relativism integrates with virtue ethics.
- **Moral Relativism + Care Ethics**: Care ethics's emphasis on cultural practices and relationships can accommodate relativism, though not necessarily.

## 7. Moral Realism

**SEP Reference**: `moral-realism` (Shafer-Landau, Huemer, FitzPatrick, Boyd)

### Core Thesis
There are **objective moral facts** that are independent of what anyone believes, feels, or agrees to. Moral truths are discoverable through reason, intuition, perception, or other reliable methods. Murder is objectively wrong, not wrong because we believe it, feel disgusted by it, or have agreed it's wrong.

### Key Thinkers
- **Plato**: Moral forms; objective ideas of justice, goodness; dialectical reasoning to moral truth.
- **Derek Shafer-Landau**: Non-naturalist moral realism; moral facts are objective but not reducible to natural facts; rational intuition.
- **Michael Huemer**: Moral phenomenology; moral truths are self-evident to moral perception; foundational moral axioms.
- **Russ Shafer-Landau, Richard Boyd**: Moral naturalism; moral facts are natural facts about human flourishing, preferences, or well-being.
- **G.E. Moore**: Non-naturalism; goodness is a simple, non-natural property; known through intuition; open question argument against naturalism.

### Core Structures
1. **Moral naturalism**: Moral facts are natural facts (about flourishing, preferences, well-being). Morality is part of the natural world.
2. **Moral non-naturalism**: Moral facts are objective but not natural; they are irreducibly moral properties known through reason or intuition.
3. **Robust realism**: There are moral truths independent of what anyone thinks, feels, or would agree to.
4. **Quietist realism**: Moral realism is true, but we cannot know much about moral facts or derive much from realism.

### Main Arguments
1. **Moral knowledge**: We do seem to know that torture is wrong, that we have obligations to help others. Realism explains this knowledge.
2. **Objectivity**: Morality feels objective; we don't feel we're merely expressing preferences when we condemn genocide.
3. **Disagreement and truth**: Persistent moral disagreement doesn't entail relativism; disputes about science persist, but science aims at objective truth.
4. **Accommodation**: Realism can accommodate our sense that morality constrains us and places demands independent of our will.
5. **Explanatory power**: Moral realism provides a unified framework for understanding moral discourse, knowledge, and motivation.

### Key Objections
1. **The epistemic problem**: How do we access non-natural moral facts? If they're not observable, how do we know them?
2. **The metaphysical problem**: What is the ontological status of moral properties? Are they natural or non-natural? Naturalism seems to reduce morality to facts about welfare; non-naturalism seems mysterious.
3. **Queerness objection**: Non-natural moral properties seem metaphysically queer; they don't fit with our scientific worldview.
4. **Frege-Geach problem**: How can moral predicates appear in logical arguments (conditionals, negations) if they express non-cognitive attitudes?
5. **Moral disagreement**: If morality is objective, why is there so much disagreement? This seems to undermine realism.
6. **Motivational internalism**: Do moral truths, if objective, necessarily motivate? If not, how can objective facts guide action?

### Diagnostic Markers

| Signal | Strength | Plain Language |
|--------|----------|---|
| "Some things are objectively wrong" | MODERATE–STRONG | Realist claim |
| "Murder isn't wrong just because we say so" | STRONG | Mind-independent moral facts |
| "There are real moral truths to discover" | MODERATE–STRONG | Realism about moral knowledge |
| "What's right is right, regardless of opinion" | MODERATE–STRONG | Objective morality |
| "Moral facts exist" | STRONG | Explicit realism |
| "We can know moral truths" | MODERATE | Moral knowledge claim |
| "Disagreement doesn't make something not real" | MODERATE | Realist response to disagreement |

### Common Confusions
1. **Realism ≠ universalism**: Universalism is about the scope of moral principles; realism is about their objectivity. Both can be true, but they're distinct claims.
2. **Realism ≠ any particular moral view**: Realism is compatible with utilitarianism, deontology, virtue ethics, etc. It's a meta-ethical claim about moral truth.
3. **Realism ≠ rationalism**: Realism doesn't require that reason alone determines moral truth; empiricism about morality is also realist.
4. **Non-naturalism ≠ mysticism**: Non-natural moral facts needn't be magical or mysterious; they might be knowable through moral intuition, reason, or perception.

### Cross-Branch Correlations
- **Moral Realism + Deontology/Virtue Ethics/Consequentialism**: Most traditional versions of these normative theories are realist about moral facts.
- **Moral Realism ↔ Moral Relativism**: Opposed; realism affirms objective facts, relativism denies them.
- **Moral Realism + Natural Law Theory**: Natural law theory typically affirms moral realism; human rights and flourishing are objective facts.
- **Moral Realism ↔ Emotivism/Expressivism**: Opposed; non-cognitivism denies that moral claims express factual truths.

### Internal Variants
1. **Moral naturalism**: Moral facts are natural facts about human welfare, preferences, or flourishing (Boyd, Brink, Arnhart).
2. **Moral non-naturalism**: Moral facts are objective but irreducibly moral (Moore, Shafer-Landau, Huemer).
3. **Cornell realism**: Robust realism about moral facts; moral properties supervene on natural properties but are not reducible (Boyd).
4. **Quietist realism**: Moral realism is true, but we shouldn't invest much in ontological theorizing about moral facts (Enoch).

## 8. Emotivism & Expressivism (Non-Cognitivism)

**SEP Reference**: `moral-cognitivism` (Ayer, Stevenson, Blackburn, Gibbard)

### Core Thesis
Moral claims do **not express factual beliefs or truths**; they express **emotions, attitudes, prescriptions, or plans**. "Torture is wrong" does not state a fact; it expresses disapproval, a commitment to avoiding torture, or a plan to discourage it. Moral "knowledge" is not knowledge of facts but mastery of how to use moral language appropriately.

### Key Thinkers
- **A.J. Ayer**: Emotivism; moral statements are emotive utterances, not propositions with truth conditions; "wrong" expresses disapproval.
- **Charles Stevenson**: Emotivism refined; moral terms have both emotive and descriptive meaning; moral disagreement is often disagreement in attitude, not belief.
- **Simon Blackburn**: Quasi-realism and expressivism; moral claims express attitudes but can be evaluated for consistency, coherence; expressivism with quasi-realistic surface.
- **Allan Gibbard**: Norm-expressivism; moral claims express commitments to norms; they can be true or false (in a robust sense) relative to a system of norms.

### Core Structures
1. **Simple emotivism**: Moral claims express emotions (Ayer); no truth conditions; moral disagreement is attitudinal.
2. **Refined emotivism**: Moral claims have both emotive force and descriptive content (Stevenson); moral disagreement is complex.
3. **Expressivism**: Moral claims express commitments, attitudes, or plans in a logically sophisticated way (Blackburn, Gibbard); they have internal logic.
4. **Norm-expressivism**: Moral claims express acceptance of or commitments to norms; normative systems can be evaluated for consistency and rationality.
5. **Quasi-realism**: Expressivism can accommodate the surface features of moral realism (moral truth, moral knowledge, moral facts) without ontological commitment.

### Main Arguments
1. **Moral claims lack truth conditions**: What would make "torture is wrong" true? No observable facts determine it; this suggests it expresses attitude, not belief.
2. **Frege-Geach problem solved**: Expressivism explains how moral claims can appear in logical arguments without expressing propositions (they express attitudes in a truth-apt way).
3. **Linguistic continuity**: Moral language functions similarly to other attitude-expressing language (imperative, evaluative).
4. **Parsimony**: Expressivism avoids postulating mysterious moral facts or properties; it explains moral discourse with only attitudes and language.
5. **Moral motivations**: Expressivism naturally explains why moral claims motivate action; they express attitudes that guide behavior.

### Key Objections
1. **The truth-value problem**: Do moral claims have truth conditions or not? If "yes," expressivism collapses into realism. If "no," how do we make sense of moral knowledge and disagreement?
2. **Embedded clauses**: In a conditional like "If torture is wrong, then we shouldn't torture," the moral claim is embedded. How does expressivism handle truth-conditions for embedded clauses?
3. **Logical form**: Can expressivism accommodate logical inferences involving moral claims without smuggling in propositionality?
4. **Objectivity and disagreement**: If morality is attitude-expression, how do we distinguish genuine moral disagreement from mere difference in attitudes?
5. **Moral knowledge**: How can we have moral knowledge if moral claims don't express truths? The expressivist must explain what "moral knowledge" amounts to.
6. **Moral progress and error**: Can expressivism accommodate the idea that we can be morally mistaken or that moral views progress over time?

### Diagnostic Markers

| Signal | Strength | Plain Language |
|--------|----------|---|
| "Morality is just expressing our feelings/attitudes" | MODERATE–STRONG | Emotivism/expressivism |
| "Saying 'that's wrong' is like saying 'boo, torture!'" | STRONG | Emotivist metaphor |
| "Moral claims don't state facts" | STRONG | Non-cognitivism; expressivism |
| "What matters is what we care about, not objective facts" | MODERATE | Expressivism; attitude-focused |
| "Morality is about committing to principles" | MODERATE | Norm-expressivism |
| "Right and wrong aren't real properties" | STRONG | Anti-realism; expressivism |
| "Moral disagreement is disagreement in attitude" | MODERATE–STRONG | Emotivist framing |

### Common Confusions
1. **Expressivism ≠ amoralism**: An expressivist can be deeply morally committed; they just deny that commitments are factual beliefs.
2. **Expressivism ≠ skepticism**: Non-cognitivism is not skeptical about morality; it's skeptical about moral facts but not moral discourse.
3. **Emotivism ≠ morality = mere feeling**: Refined emotivism and expressivism involve sophisticated attitudes, norms, and commitments, not primitive emotions.
4. **Quasi-realism ≠ realism**: Blackburn's quasi-realism mimics realist language while maintaining an expressivist foundation; it's not metaphysical realism.

### Cross-Branch Correlations
- **Expressivism + Deontology/Virtue/Consequentialism**: These normative theories are typically cognitivist (they assume moral claims express truths). But expressivist versions exist.
- **Expressivism + Moral Realism**: Opposed; expressivism denies moral facts, realism affirms them.
- **Expressivism + Moral Relativism**: Both deny objective moral facts, but for different reasons. Relativism says facts are relative; expressivism says there are no moral facts.
- **Expressivism + Contractualism**: Scanlon's contractualism is sometimes read expressively; principles express commitments rather than stating truths.

### Internal Variants
1. **Simple emotivism**: Moral claims are exclamations of emotion (Ayer).
2. **Descriptive emotivism**: Moral terms have both descriptive and emotive content (Stevenson).
3. **Blackburn's quasi-realism**: Expressivism with a realist surface; moral claims can be "true" or "false" within an expressivist framework.
4. **Gibbard's norm-expressivism**: Moral claims express acceptance of norms; normative systems can be rationally evaluated.
5. **Minimalist expressivism**: Moral claims express attitudes without substantial philosophical theorizing.

## 9. Natural Law Theory

**SEP Reference**: `natural-law-ethics` (Aquinas, Finnis, Grisez, Arnhart)

### Core Thesis
Morality is grounded in **human nature and its proper functioning**. Actions are right if they perfect human nature or contribute to human basic goods; they are wrong if they violate natural human purposes or goods. Moral law flows from understanding what humans are naturally inclined toward and ordered toward by reason. Right action realizes the natural human goods.

### Key Thinkers
- **Thomas Aquinas**: Natural law as participation in eternal law; human nature has a telos; basic goods (life, knowledge, friendship, play, practical reasonableness, religion, beauty).
- **John Finnis**: Basic human goods (life, knowledge, friendship, play, practical reasonableness, religion, beauty); incommensurable and foundational; natural law theory without metaphysics.
- **Germain Grisez**: Natural law grounded in human nature; basic goods; collateral damage doctrine (double effect).
- **Daryl Cole, Larry Arnhart**: Evolutionary natural law; human nature as evolved; virtues as excellences relative to natural human inclinations.

### Main Arguments
1. **Natural teleology**: Humans have a nature and a telos (purpose or proper functioning). Flourishing requires living in accordance with that nature.
2. **Basic goods**: Certain fundamental goods (life, knowledge, friendship, practical reasonableness) are objectively valuable for human flourishing.
3. **Rationality and inclination**: Natural law theory is not "brutish" nature-following; it is rational insight into how to achieve authentic human goods.
4. **Universality from nature**: Because human nature is universal, basic human goods and natural law are universal.
5. **Integration of emotion and reason**: Natural law respects human emotional and rational capacities as naturally ordered toward goods.
6. **Human dignity**: Humans have dignity because we possess reason and are ordered toward genuine goods, not mere survival.

### Key Objections
1. **The is-ought gap**: How do we derive moral oughts from facts about human nature? What humans are naturally inclined toward is not necessarily what they ought to do.
2. **Naturalness and normativity**: Is "natural" always morally good? Natural inclinations can be selfish or violent.
3. **Evolutionary debunking**: If human nature is evolved, and evolved traits are selected for reproduction, not moral truth, why think natural law reflects morality?
4. **Diversity of goods**: Who decides which goods are basic? Different traditions recognize different human goods (Finnis's list seems Western and Catholic).
5. **Double effect and proportionalism**: Debates within natural law theory about collateral damage doctrine show internal disagreements about how natural law applies.
6. **Religious foundation**: Natural law theory is often criticized for smuggling in religious commitments (e.g., Aquinas grounds natural law in divine reason).

### Diagnostic Markers

| Signal | Strength | Plain Language |
|--------|----------|---|
| "Human nature has a purpose" | MODERATE–STRONG | Teleology; natural law language |
| "We should fulfill our natural potential" | MODERATE | Flourishing language; natural law concern |
| "Basic goods like life and knowledge are fundamental" | STRONG | Finnis's natural law theory |
| "Reason shows us how to live well" | MODERATE | Natural law rationalism |
| "Some things violate human dignity and nature" | MODERATE–STRONG | Natural law objection to certain acts |
| "Evolution shaped our nature and our goods" | MODERATE | Naturalistic natural law |
| "Practical reasonableness is a key human good" | STRONG | Finnis language |

### Common Confusions
1. **Natural law ≠ state of nature**: Natural law theory is not about returning to nature; it's about human nature understood rationally.
2. **Natural law ≠ evolutionary biology**: While some natural law theorists incorporate evolution, natural law is fundamentally rational, not purely biological.
3. **Teleology ≠ divine command**: Aquinas grounds teleology in divine reason, but secular natural law theorists (Finnis, Arnhart) argue for teleology without theology.
4. **Basic goods ≠ happiness**: Basic goods in Finnis's account are objective goods (life, knowledge, friendship), not subjective feelings.

### Cross-Branch Correlations
- **Natural Law + Virtue Ethics**: Both appeal to human nature and telos; Aquinas and neo-Aristotelians integrate natural law and virtue ethics.
- **Natural Law + Moral Realism**: Natural law theory typically affirms moral realism; human goods are objective facts.
- **Natural Law + Deontology**: Natural law can ground agent-centered duties (duties to perfect one's nature and respect others' nature).
- **Natural Law ↔ Contractarianism**: Tension; contractarians see morality as constructed through agreement, not grounded in nature.
- **Natural Law + Consequentialism**: Consequentialists might argue that pursuing natural human goods has best consequences, but they're not motivated by nature itself.

### Internal Variants
1. **Thomistic natural law**: Grounded in human nature and divine reason (Aquinas); telos; basic goods.
2. **Finnisian natural law**: Basic human goods (incommensurable); avoids metaphysical claims about nature; secular natural law.
3. **Grisezian natural law**: Basic goods; collateral damage doctrine; emphasis on practical reasonableness.
4. **Evolutionary natural law**: Human nature as evolved; virtues as natural excellences (Arnhart, Cosmides & Tooby).
5. **Secular natural law**: Natural law without theological foundations; grounds in human nature understood rationally or scientifically.

## 10. Moral Particularism

**SEP Reference**: `moral-particularism` (Dancy, McKeever, Ridge)

### Core Thesis
Morality is **fundamentally particular**, not universal. There are no exceptionless moral principles or rules that apply across situations. Moral judgment requires attention to context, particularity, and moral nuance. What is a reason in one situation may not be a reason in another. Principles are at best generalizations from particular cases, not foundations of morality.

### Key Thinkers
- **Jonathan Dancy**: Moral particularism; no principles needed; reasons are holistic (context-dependent); particularism in epistemology and semantics.
- **Piers Norris Cole, Olivia Frey**: Defenses and refinements of particularism; challenges to principle-based approaches.
- **Brad Hooker, Frank Jackson**: Principalism (moderate universalism); principles are generalizations, but moral judgment remains particular.
- **Margaret Olivia Little**: Particularism applied to bioethics; thick ethical concepts; context-sensitivity.

### Main Arguments
1. **Context-sensitivity**: A feature that is a moral reason in one context may not be in another (or may be a reason against). Money is a reason to help, but not in all contexts.
2. **No exceptionless principles**: Every principle has exceptions or requires so much qualification that it ceases to be a useful principle.
3. **Holism of reasons**: Reasons are holistic; they depend on the configuration of features in a situation. You can't mechanically apply rules.
4. **Particularist epistemology**: We know moral truths through sensitivity to particularity, not through grasping universal principles.
5. **Thick ethical concepts**: Morally salient features are captured by thick concepts (cruelty, betrayal, integrity) that resist abstraction into thin principles.

### Key Objections
1. **Generality objection**: If there are no principles, how do we distinguish moral reasoning from arbitrary preference? Doesn't morality require some consistency?
2. **The epistemological problem**: How do we come to know the moral features of a situation without principles to guide us? Particularism seems to rely on moral intuition or perception without justification.
3. **Practical impossibility**: In complex situations, how can agents rely on sensitivity to context without any principles? This seems psychologically unrealistic.
4. **Principle-like principles**: Even particularists seem to appeal to something principle-like (e.g., "respond to moral features"). Doesn't this undermine particularism?
5. **Generalization concerns**: Some worry particularism cannot accommodate the intuition that similar cases should be treated similarly (fairness).

### Diagnostic Markers

| Signal | Strength | Plain Language |
|--------|----------|---|
| "There are no absolute rules" | MODERATE–STRONG | Particularism; context-sensitivity |
| "Every situation is unique and demands attention" | MODERATE | Particularist concern for nuance |
| "Principles are at best rough guides" | MODERATE–STRONG | Skepticism of principles; particularism |
| "You can't just apply a rule; you need judgment" | MODERATE | Context-sensitivity; practical reasoning |
| "Cruelty is wrong, but the reason depends on context" | MODERATE–STRONG | Holism of reasons; particularism |
| "What counts as cheating depends on the situation" | WEAK–MODERATE | Context-sensitivity; not necessarily particularist |
| "No exceptions—that's too rigid" (about principles) | MODERATE | Anti-principle stance |

### Common Confusions
1. **Particularism ≠ relativism**: A particularist can be a moral realist; they deny principles, not objectivity.
2. **Particularism ≠ subjectivism**: Particularism is about the structure of morality (context-dependent), not about what determines morality (subjectivity).
3. **Particularism ≠ virtue ethics**: While Dancy is sympathetic to virtue ethics, particularism is a distinct meta-ethical claim. Virtue ethicists can be universalists.
4. **Particularism ≠ unprincipled**: A particularist can be highly principled in the sense of committed to moral reasoning; they just deny that principles are foundational.

### Cross-Branch Correlations
- **Particularism + Virtue Ethics**: Both emphasize particularity and practical judgment; many virtue ethicists are also particularists (though not necessarily).
- **Particularism + Care Ethics**: Both emphasize context, relationships, and nuance. Care ethics is often particularist about moral principles.
- **Particularism ↔ Deontology**: Tension; deontology is principle-based, particularism denies principles. But some deontologists argue for flexible principles.
- **Particularism + Moral Realism**: A particularist can be a realist; moral facts are about particulars, not universal principles.
- **Particularism ↔ Contractarianism**: Tension; contractarians ground morality in agreement on principles. Particularists deny that principles are foundational.

### Internal Variants
1. **Strict particularism**: No principles; judgment is based entirely on sensitivity to context (Dancy's early position).
2. **Moderate particularism**: Principles are rough generalizations from particular judgments, but not foundational (Dancy's refined position).
3. **Particularism about thick concepts**: Emphasis on morally salient features (thick concepts) that resist abstraction into thin principles (Little, McKeever).
4. **Epistemological particularism**: Principles are not our primary route to moral knowledge; direct sensitivity to moral features is (Dancy).

```
                          PRINCIPLE-BASED
                                |

         |                       |                            |
         |                       |                            |

         |                       |                            |
         |                       |                            |

                                |
                                |
```

**Key Relationships**:
- **Deontology** and **Contractarianism** both emphasize principles and duty, but differ on justification (Kant: reason; Rawls: fairness).
- **Consequentialism** and **Virtue Ethics** are foundationally opposed (consequences vs. character) but can be integrated (virtue consequentialism).
- **Care Ethics** and **Particularism** both reject abstract universalism but differ in their basis (relationships vs. context-sensitivity).
- **Natural Law** bridges virtue ethics and deontology through appeal to nature and human goods.
- **Emotivism** and **Moral Realism** are meta-ethical opposites that can support various normative theories.

## Probing Questions: Targeting Specific School Pairs

Use these questions to distinguish between adjacent or overlapping schools.

### 1. Virtue Ethics vs. Deontology
- "When you say someone is 'a good person,' what do you mean? Are you describing their character, or their adherence to duties?"
- "Can a dutiful person be morally deficient? What if they follow all their duties but lack virtue?"
- "Is the goal to become a certain kind of person, or to do certain kinds of acts?"
- "When faced with a moral dilemma, do you ask 'What does a virtuous person do?' or 'What are my duties?'"

### 2. Deontology vs. Consequentialism
- "Would you ever do something you believe is wrong if the consequences were good enough?"
- "Are there things that are always wrong, regardless of consequences?"
- "Does the badness of lying depend on whether lying would actually cause harm, or is lying wrong in itself?"
- "What matters more: having good intentions and following principles, or producing good outcomes?"
- "If breaking a promise would save a life, should you break it?"

### 3. Consequentialism vs. Care Ethics
- "When you make a moral decision, do you think about what would produce the best results overall, or about what matters in your specific relationships?"
- "Is it okay to sacrifice the interests of someone close to you for the greater good?"
- "Can morality be impartial, or must it acknowledge special obligations to those we're connected to?"
- "Should moral rules apply the same way to everyone, or does context and relationship matter?"

### 4. Care Ethics vs. Virtue Ethics
- "Is morality fundamentally about developing character, or about responding to relationships and needs?"
- "What's more important: attentiveness to particular others, or cultivating internal excellences?"
- "Can virtues like courage be developed in isolation, or are they always embedded in relationships?"
- "When you feel empathy or compassion, is that moral motivation, or is moral motivation something deeper?"

### 5. Contractarianism vs. Natural Law
- "Is justice grounded in what people would agree to, or in human nature and natural human goods?"
- "Can we justify moral rules without appeal to any objective human nature?"
- "If people agreed to an unjust contract, would that make it just?"
- "Are human rights grounded in agreements, or in what humans naturally require to flourish?"

### 6. Natural Law vs. Virtue Ethics
- "Is virtue defined by human nature, or is human nature a foundation for developing virtue?"
- "Can something be virtuous if it violates human nature, or are virtues always aligned with nature?"
- "Is eudaimonia (flourishing) a matter of perfecting human capacities, or participating in the divine telos?"

### 7. Moral Realism vs. Emotivism
- "When you say something is 'morally wrong,' are you stating a fact, or expressing a negative attitude?"
- "Can people be mistaken about morality, or do they just have different attitudes?"
- "Is moral disagreement like disagreement about color preferences, or like disagreement about science?"
- "Are there moral truths we're discovering, or are we just deciding what to value?"

### 8. Moral Relativism vs. Moral Realism
- "Are there objective moral truths, or is morality culturally/individually relative?"
- "Can slavery be objectively wrong, or was it 'right for that time'?"
- "Is moral progress possible, or just change?"
- "When you judge another culture's moral practices, are you making an objective judgment or imposing your values?"

### 9. Particularism vs. Deontology
- "Can a moral principle have no exceptions?"
- "Is the moral significance of a feature (like causing pain) constant across all contexts, or does it vary?"
- "Is fairness based on applying the same rules to everyone, or on responding to what each situation demands?"

### 10. Contractarianism vs. Consequentialism
- "Should laws be designed to maximize overall welfare, or to be fair and justifiable to all?"
- "If a policy would produce great overall benefits but some people could reasonably reject it, is it justified?"
- "Is the legitimacy of authority grounded in producing good consequences, or in being justifiable to those governed?"

### 11. Care Ethics vs. Contractarianism
- "Can contracts capture what morality demands in intimate relationships?"
- "Is the basis of moral obligation mutual advantage and fairness, or attentiveness and response to needs?"
- "Can love and friendship be understood as contractual?"

### 12. Virtue Ethics vs. Particularism
- "Are virtues stable traits we cultivate, or do they vary contextually?"
- "Is practical wisdom (phronesis) a consistent capacity, or is moral judgment always situation-dependent?"
- "Can we say a person is courageous in general, or only that they respond courageously in specific contexts?"

## Scoring Rubric: Signal Strength and Confidence Thresholds

### Signal Strength Classification

| Strength | Criteria | Confidence Level |
|----------|----------|---|
| VERY STRONG | Direct use of distinctive terminology; explicit commitment to framework; clear philosophical reasoning | 85%+ confidence in school attribution |
| STRONG | Multiple distinctive markers; consistent reasoning; explicit framework (though perhaps not perfectly articulated) | 70-85% confidence |
| MODERATE–STRONG | Mix of distinctive and general markers; coherent reasoning aligned with school; reasonable attribution | 60-70% confidence |
| MODERATE | General markers present; could fit this school but also others; weak or partial commitment | 45-60% confidence |
| WEAK–MODERATE | Marker present but could easily fit multiple schools; vague or non-distinctive language | 30-45% confidence |
| WEAK | Isolated marker; insufficient for confident attribution; could reflect any framework | < 30% confidence |

### Confidence Thresholds

**Attribution Threshold**: A school attribution requires MODERATE (45%+) confidence. Below that, note the signal as "unclear" or "insufficient for attribution."

**Robust Profile**: A robust profile of a school requires:
- At least 3 STRONG or VERY STRONG markers
- OR 5+ MODERATE–STRONG markers with internal consistency
- Absence of contradictory markers from opposed schools

**Hybrid or Integration**: If a person shows equally strong markers from multiple schools (e.g., MODERATE–STRONG for both virtue ethics and deontology), they likely integrate frameworks. Note this as "integrated" or "eclectic."

### Interpreting Weak Signals

Weak or weak–moderate markers should be noted but not weighted heavily. A single "I try to be a good person" is insufficient for virtue ethics attribution; it's too general. However, multiple weak markers (5+ across a category) can constitute a MODERATE–STRONG signal through aggregation.

## Cross-Branch Correlations for Each School

### Virtue Ethics
**Positive Correlations**:
- Care Ethics: Both emphasize relationships, emotional attunement, and particularity.
- Natural Law: Both appeal to human nature and telos.
- Moral Realism: Both affirm objective moral facts.
- Deontology (moderate): Virtues support duties; duties express virtues.

**Negative Correlations**:
- Emotivism: Non-cognitivism conflicts with virtue as objective excellences.
- Moral Relativism: Universal human virtues conflict with relativism.
- Contractarianism (strong): Contractarians treat virtue as instrumental; virtue ethicists treat it as foundational.

**Integration Patterns**:
- Virtue + Consequentialism: "Virtues are reliable guides to good consequences."
- Virtue + Care: "Care is a virtue embedded in relationships."
- Virtue + Natural Law: "Virtues perfect human nature."

---

### Deontology
**Positive Correlations**:
- Moral Realism: Both affirm objective moral facts (duties, rights).
- Natural Law: Both appeal to objective moral order.
- Contractualism (Scanlon): Duties are grounded in reasonable rejection test.
- Virtue Ethics (moderate): Virtues support duties.

**Negative Correlations**:
- Emotivism: Non-cognitivism conflicts with objective duties.
- Consequentialism (strong): Teleological vs. agent-centered reasoning.
- Care Ethics (strong): Particularism vs. universalism.

**Integration Patterns**:
- Deontology + Consequentialism: "Rules that maximize good consequences."
- Deontology + Virtue: "Duties express virtues; virtues support duties."
- Deontology + Contractarianism: "Duties derived from fair agreement."

---

### Consequentialism
**Positive Correlations**:
- Moral Realism: Both affirm objective facts about welfare/good.
- Contractarianism (rule consequentialism): Rules justified by consequences.
- Emotivism (possible): Expressivism about goals/desires.
- Moral Relativism (act consequentialism for individuals): Individual agents maximize their good.

**Negative Correlations**:
- Deontology (strong): Rule-based vs. outcome-focused.
- Virtue Ethics (in approach): Character vs. consequences.
- Care Ethics (strong): Impartiality vs. particular relationships.
- Natural Law (if teleology divorced from flourishing): Nature vs. welfare.

**Integration Patterns**:
- Consequentialism + Virtue: "Virtues reliably produce good outcomes."
- Consequentialism + Rules: "Rules whose acceptance maximizes good."
- Consequentialism + Care: "Caring relationships produce good consequences."

---

### Care Ethics
**Positive Correlations**:
- Virtue Ethics: Both emphasize character, relationships, particularity.
- Particularism: Both reject universal principles.
- Moral Realism (moderate): Objective facts about relationships.
- Emotivism (possible): Expressivism about relationships/attitudes.

**Negative Correlations**:
- Deontology (strong): Universalism vs. particularism.
- Contractarianism (strong): Individual rational agents vs. interdependent relationships.
- Consequentialism (impartialist form): Impartial welfare vs. particular relationships.
- Moral Relativism (if taken as "anything goes"): Care ethics has normative force.

**Integration Patterns**:
- Care + Virtue: "Care is a virtue embedded in relationships."
- Care + Particularism: "Morality is responsive to context and relationship."
- Care + Deontology: "Duties of care based on relationships."

---

### Contractarianism
**Positive Correlations**:
- Moral Realism (moderate): Facts about agreements and justifiability.
- Deontology (Scanlon's version): Duties grounded in reasonable rejection.
- Emotivism (Gibbard's norm-expressivism): Expresses norms/commitments.
- Consequentialism (rule consequentialism): Contracts justified by outcomes.

**Negative Correlations**:
- Care Ethics (strong): Relationships vs. contracts.
- Virtue Ethics (strong): Character vs. agreement.
- Natural Law (strong): Nature vs. agreement.
- Moral Relativism (strong): Universal justifiability vs. relativism.

**Integration Patterns**:
- Contractarianism + Consequentialism: "Contracts justified by good outcomes."
- Contractarianism + Deontology: "Duties grounded in fair agreement."
- Contractarianism + Virtue: "Virtues support contractual cooperation."

### Natural Law
**Positive Correlations**:
- Virtue Ethics: Both appeal to nature and telos.
- Moral Realism: Both affirm objective facts (human goods, natural order).
- Deontology (agent-centered): Duties to perfect nature and respect others' nature.

**Negative Correlations**:
- Contractarianism (strong): Nature vs. agreement.

**Integration Patterns**:
- Natural Law + Virtue: "Virtues perfect nature."

**Positive Correlations**:
- Emotivism/Expressivism: Both deny objective moral facts.
- Particularism (possible): Context-dependent facts.
- Moral anti-realism: Both deny moral realism.
- Some readings of Care Ethics: Context and culture matter.

**Negative Correlations**:
- Moral Realism: Objective vs. relative.

### Moral Realism
**Positive Correlations**:
- Deontology: Both affirm objective duties.

## Common Mistakes in Ethical Profiling

### 1. Confusing Normative and Meta-Ethical Claims
- **Error**: Attributing someone to "moral realism" when they say "some things are objectively wrong."
- **Note**: "Objectively wrong" is a normative claim (expressing moral conviction); meta-ethical realism is about whether those claims express truths. The person is likely a committed normative ethicist, but their meta-ethical view is underdetermined.
- **Fix**: Ask meta-ethical follow-up questions: "Are those objective facts, or your firm beliefs?" "Could you be mistaken about moral facts?"

### 2. Equating Universalism with Deontology
- **Error**: Assuming that anyone who appeals to universal principles is a deontologist.
- **Note**: Consequentialists also affirm universal principles (e.g., "maximize good for everyone"). The difference is in what is foundational (principles vs. consequences).
- **Fix**: Distinguish: "What makes an action right—following a principle, or producing good consequences?"

### 3. Treating Virtue Ethics as "Character-Based Consequentialism"
- **Error**: Assuming virtue ethics is about cultivating virtues to produce good consequences.
- **Note**: Virtue ethics is foundationally about character and excellence, not about outcomes.
- **Fix**: Ask: "Is the virtue itself the goal, or is virtue valuable because of its effects?"

### 4. Conflating Care Ethics with Simple Sentiment
- **Error**: Treating care ethics as "just caring about people's feelings."
- **Note**: Care ethics is a sophisticated framework involving attentiveness, responsiveness, and relational reasoning.
- **Fix**: Explore: "How do you balance caring for those close to you with obligations to distant others?"

### 5. Assuming Contractarianism is Self-Interest
- **Error**: Treating contractarians as mere egoists.
- **Note**: Contractarians constrain egoism through agreement and fairness; they argue for altruistic principles that all would agree to.
- **Fix**: Ask: "Are agreements about mutual advantage, or about what's fair to everyone?"

### 6. Treating Moral Relativism as Tolerance
- **Error**: Assuming someone who respects other cultures is a moral relativist.
- **Note**: You can be a moral universalist and deeply respect other cultures (attributing differences to error, context, or limited information).
- **Fix**: Probe: "Is slavery wrong even if a culture practiced it and defended it?"

### 7. Confusing Moral Anti-Realism with Amorality
- **Error**: Assuming an expressivist or anti-realist is morally unserious.
- **Note**: Emotivists and expressivists can be deeply morally committed; they just deny that commitments are factual beliefs.
- **Fix**: Clarify: "You don't think moral claims state facts, but you're still morally committed, right?"

### 8. Equating Natural Law with "Appeal to Nature"
- **Error**: Treating any claim about "human nature" as natural law theory.
- **Note**: Natural law theory is a specific philosophical position about objective human goods and teleology. Many non-philosophers appeal to nature without endorsing natural law theory.
- **Fix**: Probe deeper: "Is human nature a moral standard, or just a description of how we are?"

### 9. Treating Particularism as Unprincipled
- **Error**: Assuming a particularist is making arbitrary judgments.
- **Note**: Particularists can be highly principled in their reasoning; they deny that universal principles are foundational, not that reasoning should be principled.
- **Fix**: Ask: "Do you think moral reasons can be universal or always context-dependent?"

### 10. Ignoring Philosophical Integration
- **Error**: Forcing someone into a single school when they integrate multiple frameworks.
- **Note**: Most people intuitively draw from multiple schools. A person might use deontological language for rights, consequentialist language for welfare, virtue language for character—all coherently.
- **Fix**: Note integrations explicitly: "This person integrates deontological and consequentialist reasoning."

## Final Profiling Guidelines

### Weighting and Interpretation

1. **Rare Signals**: Very distinctive terminology (e.g., "phronesis," "prima facie duties," "basic goods") warrant high confidence even if isolated, because few people use these terms outside the relevant philosophical tradition.

2. **Common Signals**: General statements like "it depends" or "consequences matter" are present across many schools and warrant low–moderate confidence.

3. **Clustered Signals**: Multiple signals from one school across different dimensions (theoretical reasoning, case judgments, meta-ethical claims) warrant higher confidence.

4. **Opposed Signals**: If someone shows strong signals from opposed schools (e.g., strong consequentialism and strong deontology), attribute to "integrated" or "eclectic," not to either pure school.

5. **Developmental Consideration**: Someone may be learning or exploring; provisional signals (hesitations, questions) suggest open inquiry rather than firm commitment.

### Reporting Profiles

A complete ethical profile should include:

- **Primary School(s)**: Highest confidence attribution(s).
- **Secondary Schools**: Moderate confidence; integrated or complementary views.
- **Meta-Ethical Stance**: Realism, expressivism, relativism, or unclear.
- **Notable Integrations**: How the person combines schools (e.g., "virtue consequentialist," "care-sensitive deontologist").
- **Confidence Summary**: Overall confidence level (high, moderate, low) and whether more information is needed.
- **Distinctive Features**: Unusual or novel combinations; departures from typical school views.

### What This Profiling Reveals

Ethical profiling reveals:
- How someone **thinks about obligation and right action**.
- What they take as **foundational** (character, rules, consequences, relationships, agreement, nature).
- Their **meta-ethical commitments** (realism, expressivism, relativism).
- How they **resolve moral conflicts** (appeal to principles, consequences, relationships, judgment).
- What **values and goods** they prioritize.

It does NOT reveal:
- How morally good they actually are (virtuous action, moral behavior).
- What they actually DO in moral situations (there's a theory-practice gap).
- Their *sincerity* in moral commitments.
