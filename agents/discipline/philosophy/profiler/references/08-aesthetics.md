# Branch 8: Aesthetics — What Is Beauty and Art?

**Core Question:** What makes something beautiful? What is art? Is aesthetic value objective or subjective?

**SEP Sources:** aesthetics, aesthetic-judgment, art-definition, beauty

---

## School 1: Formalism

### Core Thesis
Aesthetic value resides exclusively in the formal properties of an artwork—shape, color, composition, structure, line, balance, spatial relationships. The meaning or emotional content external to form is irrelevant to aesthetic judgment. Art is autonomous: it has value independent of representation, didactic purpose, or moral content. The viewer's task is disinterested contemplation of form alone.

### Key Thinkers
- **Clive Bell** (1881–1964): Coined "significant form"—the arrangement of lines, colors, and spatial relationships that produces aesthetic emotion. The content is irrelevant; the form is everything.
- **Immanuel Kant** (1724–1804): Aesthetic judgment is disinterested pleasure—free from desire, utility, moral interest, or conceptual dependence. Beauty is formal harmony perceived through imagination and understanding.
- **Roger Fry** (1866–1934): Formalist critic; championed form over narrative or representational accuracy in evaluating art.
- **Michael Fried** (1939–): Defended formalist abstraction; argued modernism's achievement was purifying art into pure form.

### Main Arguments

1. **Formal properties are the only objective features of art.** Meaning, emotion, and intent are subjective and variable; form (line, color, proportion) is stable and observable.

2. **Disinterested contemplation is the proper aesthetic attitude.** We appreciate form when we are not motivated by practical desire, moral judgment, or personal interest. This is what distinguishes aesthetic from everyday perception.

3. **Formal properties generate aesthetic emotion.** The arrangement of colors and lines—their balance, harmony, tension—produces a specific qualitative experience (Clive Bell's "aesthetic emotion"). This is primary and non-derivative.

4. **Representation and narrative are distractions.** If you focus on what the work depicts or its story, you miss its true aesthetic content. A portrait is evaluated as a formal arrangement of shapes and colors, not as resemblance to the sitter.

5. **Modernism vindicates formalism.** The evolution toward abstraction and reduction shows art's true nature: pure form, freed from narrative and representation.

### Key Objections

1. **Formalism ignores the role of content and context.** A photograph of a loved one has aesthetic significance partly because of its representational content and what it depicts, not only its formal properties. Formalism cannot account for why the *same* formal arrangement matters differently depending on content.

2. **Aesthetic emotion is not purely formal.** Bell's "significant form" is vague and circular—we recognize it by the aesthetic emotion it produces, but we identify the emotion by the form. The theory lacks independent criteria for what counts as "significant."

3. **The disinterestedness requirement is too strict.** Much art is engaged, political, morally purposive. A propaganda poster can be formally beautiful; a painting critiquing injustice has aesthetic value because it matters, not despite its meaning.

4. **Formalism cannot explain why some form matters and other form doesn't.** Two arrangements might be equally balanced and harmonious, but one moves us and the other doesn't. Form alone cannot explain this difference.

5. **Modernism is a contingent historical movement, not a vindication of essence.** The move toward abstraction was artistic choice, not revelation of art's true nature. Many non-formalist artworks remain powerful and important.

6. **Formalism excludes much that we call art:** conceptual art, performance art, installation, and works explicitly designed to be non-beautiful. If their value is not formal, formalism cannot account for them.

### Diagnostic Markers

**Strong Formalism:**
- "The composition is what makes it beautiful, not the story."
- "I judge art by form, balance, color harmony—not by what it represents."
- "The content doesn't matter; what matters is how it's arranged."
- When discussing art, focuses exclusively on arrangement, balance, spatial relationships, line quality, color harmony.
- Dismisses representational or narrative content as "not the point."
- Prefers abstract art or defends form in representational works.
- Uses terms: "significant form," "composition," "disinterestedness," "pure art," "form over content."

**Weaknesses:**
- Cannot account for art where non-formal features are clearly central (conceptual art, political art, narrative art).
- Struggles with why the same formal arrangement matters differently in different contexts.
- Theory becomes circular: aesthetic emotion is caused by significant form; significant form is recognized by aesthetic emotion.

**Confusions:**
- Confusing "form" with "abstract" or "non-representational." Form exists in representational art too; formalism claims it's the *only* thing that matters, not that representation is absent.
- Mistaking technical skill (drawing, composition) for formalism. A formally competent representational painting is not formalist if its value is partly representational.
- Assuming that valuing form means rejecting all content. Formalists do reject content's aesthetic relevance, but may engage with content intellectually or morally.

### Cross-Branch Correlations
- **Objectivism (School 5):** Formalism often assumes formal properties are objective and mind-independent, supporting objectivism.
- **Institutionalism (School 3):** Formalism might resist institutionalism (not all institutional art is formally significant), but both can coexist if institutions privilege formal merit.
- **Expressionism (School 2):** Direct opposition—formalism rejects expression as irrelevant to aesthetic value.
- **Sublime (School 6):** Formalism accounts poorly for the sublime's overwhelming, non-formal character.

## School 2: Expressionism

### Core Thesis
Art's fundamental value lies in the expression and evocation of emotion. A work is successful as art to the extent that it authentically conveys feeling and moves the audience emotionally. The artist's emotional intent and the viewer's emotional response are central to aesthetic judgment. Form is valuable only insofar as it serves emotional expression.

### Key Thinkers
- **Leo Tolstoy** (1828–1910): Art is the infection of emotion from artist to audience. A work is art if the artist genuinely felt and successfully transmitted the emotion to others.
- **Benedetto Croce** (1866–1952): Art is expression—the objectification of feeling in intuition. An artwork exists when an emotion is given intuitive form in the artist's mind, regardless of physical execution.
- **R.G. Collingwood** (1889–1943): Art is the expression of emotion, not the arousal of emotion. The artist clarifies and expresses an emotion through the creative process; the audience re-enacts that expression.
- **Susanne Langer** (1895–1985): Art symbolizes feeling and emotion through its sensory and formal properties.

### Main Arguments

1. **Emotion is the heart of art.** Without emotional content, technical perfection is mere craft. A technically flawless but emotionally cold work is not truly art.

2. **Expression is active and intentional.** The artist must genuinely feel and work to express that feeling. Fake or manufactured emotion produces pseudo-art. Authenticity is essential.

3. **Emotional transmission is the measure of artistic success.** If the audience does not feel what the artist felt, communication has failed. Successful art "infects" the audience with emotion.

4. **Form serves expression.** Composition, color, line, subject matter—all are tools for conveying emotion. They matter only because they facilitate emotional communication.

5. **Emotion is non-conceptual.** You cannot fully capture a work's emotional content in words or concepts. Art conveys what cannot be said.

### Key Objections

1. **Expression is neither necessary nor sufficient for art.** Conceptual art, found objects, and readymades may have no emotional intent. A person's sincere diary entry expresses emotion but is not art. Expression alone cannot define art.

2. **How do we identify the emotion expressed?** Without shared conventions or context, it's unclear what emotion a work expresses. Two viewers may feel entirely different emotions from the same work. Is the theory committed to relativism?

3. **Emphasis on artist's intent is problematic.** The artist's psychological state at creation is inaccessible; we have only the work itself. Moreover, a work can be aesthetically powerful whether or not it matched the artist's emotional intent.

4. **The audience's emotional response is not reliable.** People respond to art for many reasons (memory, distraction, projection). Individual emotional response is idiosyncratic and tells us little about the work's aesthetic merits.

5. **Much art is not primarily emotional.** Conceptual art, minimalism, installation art, and formal experimentation may aim at intellectual engagement rather than emotional transmission. Expressionism cannot account for these.

6. **Expression theory conflates art with psychology.** If art is about expressing the artist's feelings, art becomes psychotherapy or confession, not a distinct human practice.

### Diagnostic Markers

**Strong Expressionism:**
- "Good art makes you FEEL something."
- "The point of art is to move you emotionally."
- "If it doesn't evoke emotion, it's not really art."
- When discussing art, focuses on emotional impact, what the artist "was trying to say," and the feelings it produces.
- Values emotional authenticity and sincerity in artists.
- Dismisses formally complex but emotionally flat art as "cold" or "not real art."
- Uses terms: "expression," "emotion," "feeling," "authenticity," "moving," "connect," "impact."

**Weaknesses:**
- Cannot account for non-emotional art (conceptual, formal, decorative).
- Struggles with identifying what emotion is expressed (too subjective).
- Makes art dependent on artist's psychology, which is hard to verify and variable.

**Confusions:**
- Confusing "emotional impact" with "emotional expression." A work can move you without expressing the artist's emotion.
- Mistaking the viewer's emotional state for the work's emotional content. Strong personal reactions may reflect the viewer, not the work.
- Assuming emotional authenticity is identifiable and morally valuable. Sincere emotion can produce bad art; inauthentic intent can produce powerful work.

### Cross-Branch Correlations
- **Formalism (School 1):** Direct opposition—expressionism rejects form-centered aesthetic evaluation.
- **Sublime (School 6):** Some sublime experiences are emotional (awe, terror), aligning with expressionism, but the sublime's overwhealming character may exceed emotion.
- **Subjectivism (School 4):** Expressionism, by emphasizing personal response, can slide toward subjectivism.
- **Objectivism (School 5):** In tension—if emotional response is subjective, expressionism seems to undermine objective aesthetic judgment.

## School 3: Institutionalism

### Core Thesis
Something is art if and only if it is conferred the status of art by the artworld—the network of artists, critics, curators, museums, galleries, historians, and educated viewers. There is no essential property that makes something art; rather, art is a status conferred through social practice and institutional recognition. The artworld decides what counts as art by presenting and discussing objects as artworks.

### Key Thinkers
- **Arthur Danto** (1924–2013): The artworld is necessary to distinguish an artwork from a mere object. Two perceptually identical objects—one a readymade artwork, one an ordinary thing—differ in artworld status, not appearance. Interpretation and artworld context make it art.
- **George Dickie** (1926–2020): Art is whatever the institutional framework (museums, galleries, critics, etc.) confers the status of art upon. A thing is art if presented as a candidate for appreciation within the artworld.
- **Denis Dutton** (1944–2010): Challenged pure institutionalism but acknowledged institutionalism's explanatory power for much contemporary art.
- **Noel Carroll** (1947–2023): Refined institutional theory; art requires artworld recognition *and* some intentional connection to art traditions.

### Main Arguments

1. **No intrinsic aesthetic property defines art.** Readymades (Duchamp's fountain), found objects, and conceptual art show that arthood is independent of formal beauty, emotional expression, or representational accuracy. What makes a urinal art? Not its properties but its institutional status.

2. **The artworld is constitutive of art.** Objects become artworks by being presented to the artworld for appreciation. This is not recognition of pre-existing arthood; rather, artworld status *confers* arthood.

3. **Art is an historically evolving practice.** As art practices change, the definition shifts. Conceptual art, performance, digital art—these were once rejected as "not real art" but were admitted into the artworld and thereby became art.

4. **Interpretation is essential.** A work's status as art depends on how it is interpreted within artworld discourse. The same object presented differently (in a museum versus a trash heap) has different arthood.

5. **The artworld is self-perpetuating.** The artworld validates itself; what the artworld says is art, is art. This is not circular reasoning but a recognition of how social institutions work.

### Key Objections

1. **Institutionalism is vacuous and circular.** It defines art in terms of the artworld, and the artworld as the institution that recognizes art. This tells us nothing about why the artworld makes certain choices or what distinguishes art-recognizing institutions from other institutions.

2. **It conflates art with artworld-recognized objects.** Perhaps the artworld recognizes some art but also misses much art (folk art, art from non-Western traditions, future art). And it may recognize non-art (kitsch, failed attempts).

3. **It makes art entirely contingent and non-evaluative.** If the artworld can declare anything art, aesthetic judgment becomes impossible. There is no standard for whether the artworld's choices are good or bad.

4. **The artworld is not clearly bounded.** Who counts as part of the artworld? How much consensus is required? If a single influential curator declares something art, is it art? The theory needs clear criteria.

5. **It ignores the role of properties.** While institutional status matters, artworld recognition typically depends on works having certain properties—novelty, intentionality, skill, engagement with art traditions. Institutionalism underplays these.

6. **It cannot account for folk and traditional art.** Much art from non-Western or pre-modern traditions was not created for artworld recognition and had no artworld. Yet these are paradigmatically artworks. Institutionalism struggles to include them.

### Diagnostic Markers

**Strong Institutionalism:**
- "If the art world says it's art, it's art."
- "Art is whatever gets presented in museums and galleries."
- "The status of art is conferred by institutions and critics."
- When discussing controversial artworks, defers to institutional recognition as the deciding factor.
- Accepts conceptual, institutional, and performance art readily.
- Views art as historically contingent; dismisses essentialist definitions.
- Uses terms: "artworld," "institutional status," "cultural practice," "canonized," "recognized," "framed as art."

**Weaknesses:**
- Cannot explain what makes the artworld the authority on art.
- Seems to make aesthetic judgment impossible (anything the artworld says is art).
- Cannot account for non-institutional, folk, or traditional art.
- Vacuous: tells us art is what institutions say is art, but doesn't explain why institutions make those choices.

**Confusions:**
- Confusing "the artworld recognizes it" with "therefore it's art." Recognition might be error or fashion.
- Assuming all institutional decisions are justified. Museums make mistakes and have biases.
- Mistaking the origin of the artworld (socially constructed) with its epistemology (how we know something is art). Even if art is socially created, we can still ask whether institutional recognition is reliable.

### Cross-Branch Correlations
- **Formalism (School 1):** Can coexist if the artworld privileges formally significant works, but institutionalism in principle rejects formalism's claim that form is *the* aesthetic criterion.
- **Expressionism (School 2):** Can coexist if institutions privilege emotional expression, but institutionalism denies that expression is necessary for arthood.
- **Subjectivism (School 4):** Both reject objective aesthetic criteria, but institutionalism grounds judgment in collective practice, while subjectivism leaves it to individuals.
- **Objectivism (School 5):** In strong tension—institutionalism seems to deny objective aesthetic properties, while objectivism affirms them.

## School 4: Aesthetic Subjectivism

### Core Thesis
Beauty and aesthetic value are not objective features of the world but are mind-dependent and vary from person to person. Aesthetic judgments express personal preferences and emotional responses, not facts about objects. "Beauty is in the eye of the beholder"—there is no fact of the matter about what is beautiful, only individual perceptions and tastes. Aesthetic disagreement reflects difference in taste, not error in perception.

### Key Thinkers
- **David Hume** (1711–1776): Explored aesthetic judgment as subjective but recognized the problem of varying tastes. He argued for a "standard of taste"—educated judges with appropriate disposition and experience, whose judgments cohere. But this standard itself is conventional, not objective.
- **Peter Kivy** (1934–): Contemporary defender of aesthetic subjectivism; argues aesthetic properties are relational—how objects affect perceivers—and therefore mind-dependent.
- **Marcia Muelder Eaton** (1938–): Aesthetic properties are neither fully objective nor fully subjective but are relational and context-dependent.
- **Igor Stravinsky** (1882–1971): "The facts of aesthetic experience simply cannot be established upon the judgment of the senses."

### Main Arguments

1. **Aesthetic properties are not intrinsic to objects.** Redness is intrinsic to the apple; beauty is not intrinsic to anything. Beauty exists only in relation to a perceiver's sensibility.

2. **Taste varies widely, and no variation is objectively wrong.** Two people may find the same work beautiful/ugly with equal sincerity. This disagreement cannot be resolved by appealing to facts about the work.

3. **Aesthetic statements are not truth-apt.** When I say "This painting is beautiful," I am not making a claim that can be true or false but rather expressing my preference. It is more like saying "I like this" than stating a fact.

4. **There is no objective standard for aesthetic judgment.** Any proposed standard (formal properties, emotional expression, cultural convention) either is subjective itself or underdetermines judgment. Disagreement persists even among experts.

5. **Subjectivism respects individual autonomy.** No one can be wrong about their aesthetic responses; your taste is your own. This avoids aesthetic elitism and respects diverse cultures and perspectives.

### Key Objections

1. **Subjectivism seems to make aesthetic criticism incoherent.** If beauty is purely subjective, what grounds criticism? I cannot say your taste is bad or mistaken, only different. Yet we plainly do criticize taste and judge some judgments better than others.

2. **Expertise and education matter in aesthetics.** Trained musicians, art historians, and critics make better aesthetic judgments than novices. Subjectivism cannot explain why education in aesthetics improves judgment if judgment is purely subjective.

3. **Perceptual error is possible.** I might misjudge a work due to poor conditions, distraction, or misunderstanding. My immediate subjective response is fallible. Objectivism better accounts for this possibility.

4. **Some aesthetic judgments are widely shared and stable.** If beauty were purely subjective, we would expect aesthetic fashion to be random. But aesthetic evolution shows patterns and reasons (works we initially rejected are now valued; others fall into obscurity). This suggests some objective basis.

5. **Hume's "standard of taste" undermines subjectivism.** If consensus among experienced judges is the standard, then taste is not purely individual but is socially and educationally constituted—a move away from pure subjectivism.

6. **Aesthetic properties can be perceived and discussed.** We point to features of works and explain aesthetic judgments. This practice presupposes that aesthetic properties are stable enough to perceive and reason about—not purely subjective.

### Diagnostic Markers

**Strong Subjectivism:**
- "Beauty is in the eye of the beholder."
- "There's no objective standard for beauty; it's all personal preference."
- "You can't say someone's taste is wrong; it's just different."
- When discussing aesthetic disagreement, refuses to privilege any judgment as correct.
- Dismisses appeals to expertise, training, or standards.
- Values individual taste and rejects hierarchies of aesthetic judgment.
- Uses terms: "personal preference," "subjective," "taste," "in the eye of the beholder," "no accounting for taste."

**Weaknesses:**
- Cannot ground criticism or explain why education improves judgment.
- Makes aesthetic expertise and discussion seem irrational.
- Struggles to explain widespread consensus and stable aesthetic patterns.

**Confusions:**
- Confusing "disagreement" with "subjectivity." People disagree about many objective matters (history, science). Disagreement alone does not establish subjectivity.
- Mistaking "taste is personal" (true) with "taste is purely subjective" (stronger claim). Personal judgment can track objective features.
- Assuming that rejecting aesthetic objectivity requires rejecting standards altogether. Hume shows standards can be conventional and shared without being objective in a metaphysical sense.

### Cross-Branch Correlations
- **Institutionalism (School 3):** Both reject objective standards, but institutionalism grounds judgment in collective practice, while subjectivism leaves it to individuals.
- **Objectivism (School 5):** Direct opposition.
- **Expressionism (School 2):** Can overlap if emotional response is subjective, but expressionism claims emotion is *expressed*, suggesting some structure beyond mere preference.
- **Formalism (School 1):** Can coexist in principle (someone might claim formal properties are perceived subjectively), but formalism usually assumes form is objective.

## School 5: Aesthetic Objectivism

### Core Thesis
Beauty and aesthetic value are real, mind-independent properties of objects (or features of objects and perceivers in relation). Some things really are more beautiful or aesthetically valuable than others, independent of anyone's preferences or judgments. Aesthetic facts exist and can be true or false. Aesthetic disagreement reflects differences in perception, sensitivity, or knowledge, not merely different preferences. The task of aesthetics is to discover and understand these objective aesthetic properties.

### Key Thinkers
- **Plato** (428–348 BCE): Beauty is an objective Form or Idea, accessible through reason and philosophical ascent. The beautiful in sensory objects partakes in the Form of Beauty.
- **Aristotle** (384–322 BCE): Aesthetic properties are real and tied to form, function, and excellence. A beautiful object achieves excellence in its kind.
- **Frank Sibley** (1923–1996): Aesthetic properties are objective and perceivable but require perceptual sensitivity. They are dependent on lower-level physical properties but are not reducible to them.
- **Noël Carroll** (1947–2023): Defended objective standards for art while acknowledging that standards are historically contingent and context-dependent.
- **Mary Mothersill** (1928–): Argued that aesthetic properties, while subjective in how they depend on experience, are objective in that facts about them can be true or false.

### Main Arguments

1. **Aesthetic properties are perceivable features of objects.** When we perceive an artwork as graceful, balanced, or moving, we perceive something; these are not mere projections. Aesthetic properties are as real as colors or shapes.

2. **Sensitivity and cultivation improve aesthetic perception.** Education, exposure, and reflection enhance our ability to perceive aesthetic properties. This improvement is not mere conditioning but development of genuine perception.

3. **Disagreement can be error.** Two judges may conflict about a work's beauty; at least one is mistaken. This is possible because beauty is objective—there is a fact of the matter to get right or wrong.

4. **Expertise is intelligible.** Critics, conservators, and educated audiences have better aesthetic judgment because they perceive aesthetic properties more accurately and comprehensively. Objectivism explains why expertise matters.

5. **Aesthetic properties depend on lower-level properties.** A work's color, form, composition—these physical properties ground its aesthetic properties. Aesthetic facts are not mysterious or floating free from physical facts.

6. **Aesthetic evolution makes sense.** Why do we revise aesthetic canons? Not due to arbitrary fashion but because we discover aesthetic merits we previously missed or recognize that our judgments were confused.

### Key Objections

1. **What are aesthetic properties made of?** If beauty is objective, what is its metaphysical nature? Plato's Forms seem distant and inaccessible; contemporary accounts struggle to explain how aesthetic properties relate to physical properties.

2. **Aesthetic properties seem dependent on response.** Beauty seems to require being perceived as beautiful; it is not merely a physical arrangement. This suggests aesthetic properties are relational or response-dependent, not purely objective.

3. **Cultural and historical variation is pervasive.** What is beautiful in one culture is ugly in another; aesthetic canons shift dramatically. This suggests objectivity is illusory or limited to narrow domains.

4. **Disagreement persists among experts.** Trained judges, equally informed and sensitive, disagree profoundly. If aesthetic properties were straightforwardly objective, convergence should be easier.

5. **The dependency of aesthetic properties on non-aesthetic properties is loose and variable.** The same physical arrangement can be differently aesthetically evaluated in different contexts. This looseness suggests objectivism misses something.

6. **Objectivism threatens to be circular.** If we identify objective aesthetic properties by appeal to expert perception, and experts by appeal to reliable aesthetic perception, the theory risks circularity.

### Diagnostic Markers

**Strong Objectivism:**
- "Some things are objectively more beautiful than others."
- "You can be wrong about beauty; it's not just opinion."
- "Educated judgment is more reliable than untrained judgment."
- When discussing aesthetics, appeals to standards, expertise, and discovery.
- Recognizes that education and experience improve aesthetic perception.
- Resistant to complete relativism; maintains that some canons are justified.
- Uses terms: "objective," "aesthetic facts," "real properties," "genuinely beautiful," "expertise," "reliable perception."

**Weaknesses:**
- Difficult to explain the metaphysical nature of aesthetic properties.
- Struggles with persistent expert disagreement.
- Cannot fully account for cultural and historical variation in taste.
- Risk of circularity in identifying both objective properties and reliable judges.

**Confusions:**
- Confusing "objective" with "universal." Objective properties can be culturally variable and context-dependent.
- Mistaking expert consensus for proof of objectivity. Experts can be systematically mistaken or influenced by fashion.
- Assuming that objectivity requires independence from all human response. Aesthetic properties may be relational (dependent on how objects appear to perceivers) yet objective (determining facts about those relations).

### Cross-Branch Correlations
- **Formalism (School 1):** Formalism typically assumes formal properties are objective, supporting objectivism.
- **Expressionism (School 2):** Tension—if emotion is subjective, expressionism seems to support subjectivism; but objectivism can accommodate expression as an objective feature.
- **Institutionalism (School 3):** In tension—institutionalism denies objective standards, while objectivism affirms them.
- **Subjectivism (School 4):** Direct opposition.
- **Sublime (School 6):** Objectivism can account for sublime properties as objective features of experience.

## School 6: The Sublime

### Core Thesis
The sublime is a distinct aesthetic category beyond beauty. It is characterized by overwhelming awe, even fear or terror, in the face of vastness, power, or overwhelming infinity that exceeds comprehension. The sublime can be unpleasant yet profoundly valuable. The subject encounters something that seems to exceed form, comprehension, and safe appreciation. Key dimensions: mathematical sublime (infinite magnitude), dynamical sublime (overwhelming power), and the mixture of pleasure and pain, fear and admiration.

### Key Thinkers
- **Immanuel Kant** (1724–1804): Distinguished the beautiful (form, harmony, purposiveness) from the sublime (formlessness, vastness, power that initially repels). The sublime arises in the confrontation between sensibility and reason; reason triumphs, producing a complex pleasure mixed with pain. Mathematical sublime (infinite magnitude) and dynamical sublime (overwhelming force).
- **Edmund Burke** (1729–1797): The sublime is rooted in pain, fear, and terror. Distant or imagined danger produces the sublime pleasure of terror. Darkness, obscurity, and vastness are sublime; clarity and definiteness are beautiful.
- **Thomas Weiskel** (1940–): Psychoanalytic reading of the sublime as anxiety and ego dissolution—the subject's coherence is threatened and reconstituted.
- **Jean-Luc Nancy** (1940–): The sublime is the limit of presentation; it cannot be fully represented or comprehended.
- **Elaine Scarry** (1953–): Beauty (including its relationship to justice) versus the sublime as dissolutive and fragmenting.

### Main Arguments

1. **The sublime is distinct from beauty.** Beauty is harmonious, bounded, and comprehensible. The sublime is vast, formless, overwhelming, and exceeds comprehension. A beautiful landscape is delightful; a sublime landscape (vast mountain, infinite ocean) inspires awe mixed with fear.

2. **The sublime provokes a paradoxical response: pleasure and pain.** The viewer is initially frightened or repelled—confronted by vastness or power that seems threatening. Yet this very confrontation produces a strange pleasure: the recognition of reason, imagination, or the self overcoming the threat.

3. **The mathematical sublime involves infinite magnitude.** Attempting to grasp the infinite (the starry sky, the ocean, vast desert) produces a failure of imagination and sensibility but a triumph of reason. We recognize infinity intellectually even though we cannot sensibly comprehend it.

4. **The dynamical sublime involves overwhelming force.** Thunder, storms, volcanoes, hurricanes—natural forces that dwarf human power—produce the dynamical sublime. Fear is genuine (we are physically threatened or humbled), yet the pleasure is real (we recognize reason and self-preservation, or we stand at safe distance in imagination).

5. **The sublime cannot be fully formalized or represented.** Unlike beauty, which can be captured and composed, the sublime resists form. The attempt to represent or express it produces fragmentary, difficult, even ugly forms—because the content exceeds representation.

6. **The sublime is modernist and post-romantic.** It captures experiences central to modernity: alienation, vastness, incomprehensibility, the excess of nature and power beyond human scale. Romantic and modern art embrace the sublime where classical art sought beauty.

### Key Objections

1. **The sublime conflates aesthetic categories with emotional reactions.** Awe and fear are psychological states, not aesthetic properties. Calling fear "sublime" conflates affect with aesthetics. Many non-aesthetic things provoke awe (giant machinery, wealth).

2. **Burke's theory seems to reduce the sublime to danger.** If the sublime is rooted in fear and pain, it is unclear why safe distance is necessary. And if it is safe (imagined or distant danger), then actual pain is not sublime. The theory collapses into either non-aesthetic fear or fake emotion.

3. **Kant's explanation is unclear.** How does imagination's failure produce pleasure? How does reason's triumph transform fear into aesthetic delight? The mechanism is mysterious and perhaps mysterious because it doesn't exist.

4. **The sublime is too closely tied to Romanticism and particular cultural moments.** The category may not be universal but culturally and historically specific. Non-Western and pre-modern traditions may not have the concept or value it as central.

5. **Not all vast or overwhelming things are sublime.** A vast parking lot or a bureaucratic building is not sublime, even if it exceeds comprehension in some sense. The theory needs stricter criteria.

6. **The relationship to beauty is unclear.** Can something be both beautiful and sublime? Are they on a spectrum, or categorically distinct? The theory leaves this ambiguous.

### Diagnostic Markers

**Strong Sublime:**
- "The greatest art provokes awe and a sense of the infinite—a mix of fear and pleasure."
- "True aesthetic experience is sublime, not merely beautiful—it should move you profoundly, even uncomfortably."
- "Some things are too vast, powerful, or overwhelming to fully comprehend—that's where the real aesthetic experience begins."
- When discussing art or nature, emphasizes overwhelming scale, formlessness, infinity, vastness, power.
- Values the difficult, fragmentary, or grotesque if it conveys the sublime.
- Distinguishes sharply between beautiful (harmonious, pleasurable) and sublime (awe-struck, vertiginous).
- Uses terms: "sublime," "awe," "overwhelming," "infinite," "vast," "terror," "formless," "beautiful," "mathematical sublime," "dynamical sublime."

**Weaknesses:**
- Conflates aesthetic and psychological categories (awe, fear).
- Unclear mechanism for transforming fear into pleasure.
- Too historically and culturally specific; may not be universal.
- Difficult to distinguish from non-aesthetic experiences (mountain climbing, storms).

**Confusions:**
- Confusing "overwhelming" with "sublime." Size and power alone do not produce the sublime; the specific mixture of reason, imagination, and self-recognition does.
- Mistaking Romantic aesthetics for universal aesthetics. The emphasis on the sublime is modern and Western; other traditions may prioritize beauty or harmony.
- Assuming the sublime requires danger or pain. Safe appreciation of vastness (stars, desert) is sublime without danger.
- Conflating the sublime with the grotesque or ugly. While the sublime may exceed beauty and be formally chaotic, it is not simply the opposite of beauty.

### Cross-Branch Correlations
- **Formalism (School 1):** The sublime exceeds form; formalism struggles to account for it.
- **Expressionism (School 2):** Some sublime experiences are emotional (awe, terror), aligning with expressionism, but the sublime's structured paradox (pleasure-in-fear) exceeds simple emotional expression.
- **Objectivism (School 5):** The sublime, as an overwhelming category tied to real power and vastness, supports objectivism.
- **Subjectivism (School 4):** The sublime, as intensely personal and variable, seems subjective; but the structured nature of the sublime (the paradoxical pleasure) suggests structure beyond individual preference.

**Spectrum Notes:**
- Left-Right: Disinterested → Engaged; Formal → Emotional; Objective → Subjective.
- The Sublime cuts across all positions; any school can incorporate it.
- Institutionalism is orthogonal; it addresses how art gets status, not what makes it beautiful.
- Pure positions are rare; most theorists blend schools or shift depending on context.

## 8+ Probing Questions for Diagnosis

**General Openings:**
1. "What makes something beautiful? Is it a property of the object, a response in the viewer, or something else?"
2. "Can you be wrong about what's beautiful, or is beauty purely a matter of personal taste?"
3. "What's the difference between good art and bad art? On what basis would you judge?"

**Formalism Tests:**
4. "I've shown you two paintings with identical formal composition (same colors, balance, shapes) but different subjects—one a landscape, one a portrait. Would they be equally beautiful?"
5. "Does the story or meaning behind an artwork matter to its beauty, or is it the visual composition that counts?"

**Expressionism Tests:**
6. "If an artist created a technically perfect work without any emotional intent, would it be real art?"
7. "When you experience art, what matters more—understanding what the artist felt, or having your own emotional response?"

**Institutionalism Tests:**
8. "If a museum displays something as art and the artworld validates it, does that make it art—even if you don't think it's beautiful or expressive?"
9. "Could something be a 'real artwork' without institutional recognition? Would folk art from pre-modern cultures count?"

**Objectivism vs. Subjectivism Tests:**
10. "If two educated, attentive viewers completely disagree about a work's beauty, does that mean beauty is subjective, or can one person be mistaken?"
11. "Can aesthetic education actually improve your judgment, or does it just change your preferences?"

**Sublime Tests:**
12. "Is there a difference between 'beautiful' and 'sublime'? What about facing something vast or overwhelming that's not entirely pleasant—is that aesthetic?"
13. "Can art be valuable even if it's difficult, ugly, or disturbing—because it evokes awe or confronts you with something beyond comprehension?"

**Deep Integration Tests:**
14. "A readymade or conceptual artwork: is it art? What makes something art if it's not beautiful, expressive, or formally interesting?"
15. "When aesthetic standards change over time—we now value works we once dismissed—does that show standards are subjective, or that we've learned better?"

## Scoring Rubric

For each of the six schools, score agreement on a 0–5 scale:

**0 – No endorsement:** Actively rejects the school; opposes its claims.
**1 – Minimal:** Occasional language but inconsistent; denies core thesis.
**2 – Weak:** Tentative endorsement; acknowledges some insights but resists full commitment.
**3 – Moderate:** Clear agreement; school is a major influence; may blend with others.
**4 – Strong:** Primary commitment; language, logic, and judgment align with school.
**5 – Pure:** Consistent, rigorous, and exclusive commitment; all judgments flow from school's premises.

### Scoring Template

| School | Evidence | Notes | Score |
|--------|----------|-------|-------|
| **Formalism** | Form-focused language; dismissal of content; emphasis on disinterested contemplation; preference for abstraction or formal purity. | Look for: "composition," "form over content," "pure art," disinterest in narrative or representation. | _/5 |
| **Expressionism** | Emphasis on emotion, feeling, authenticity; art as communication or transmission of affect; importance of artist's intent. | Look for: "makes you feel," "emotional authenticity," "moving," "what the artist meant," emotional impact. | _/5 |
| **Institutionalism** | Recognition of artworld status; acceptance of conceptual/institutional art; deference to museums, critics, curators; flexibility on what counts as art. | Look for: "if the artworld says so," "institutional recognition," comfort with contemporary/avant-garde art, fluidity. | _/5 |
| **Subjectivism** | Rejection of objective standards; emphasis on personal taste; refusal to rank judgments; "beauty is subjective"; skepticism of expertise. | Look for: "beauty is subjective," "no accounting for taste," "just personal preference," rejection of hierarchy. | _/5 |
| **Objectivism** | Appeal to standards, expertise, discovery; belief in aesthetic facts; confidence that education improves judgment; willingness to say others are wrong. | Look for: "objective standards," "expertise," "better judgment," "some things really are more beautiful," grounds for criticism. | _/5 |
| **Sublime** | Emphasis on vastness, awe, overflow of comprehension; paradoxical pleasure-in-fear; romantic or modernist bent; valuing the overwhelming over the harmonious. | Look for: "sublime," "awe," "overwhelming," "beautiful but also scary," romantic language, confrontation with the infinite. | _/5 |

**Multi-School Profiling:**
- **High Formalism + High Objectivism:** Believes in formal aesthetic properties and their objectivity. Likely trained in visual arts or philosophy. Values modernist abstraction.
- **High Expressionism + High Subjectivism:** Believes art is about emotional transmission but doubts objective standards. Likely values authenticity and personal connection.
- **High Institutionalism + High Subjectivism:** Flexible on what counts as art and bases judgment on social recognition plus personal response. Comfortable with contemporary art; skeptical of absolute standards.
- **High Objectivism + Moderate to Low Institutionalism:** Believes in aesthetic standards but grounds them in properties/expertise, not institutional decree. Likely values critical assessment independent of canon.
- **High Sublime:** May score high on other schools but overlays them with emphasis on overwhelming, paradoxical, or formless experiences. Romantic or modernist sensibility.

## Plain-Language Diagnostic Markers

### Red Flags for Each School

**Formalism Red Flags:**
- Consistently reduces all aesthetic judgment to formal vocabulary.
- Dismisses representational, narrative, or political art as "not about aesthetics."
- Becomes circular: "This is beautiful because it has significant form; I know it has significant form because it's beautiful."
- Resistant to acknowledging non-formal sources of value.

**Expressionism Red Flags:**
- Over-reliance on "how it makes me feel" without reference to the work's structure or content.
- Conflation of the viewer's emotional state with the work's emotional content.
- Inability to distinguish authentic expression from mere emotional triggers.
- Dismissal of non-emotional art as "not real art."

**Institutionalism Red Flags:**
- Pure deference to artworld judgment without independent assessment.
- Circular: "It's art because institutions recognize it; institutions recognize it because it's art."
- Inability to criticize institutional choices or acknowledge non-institutional art.
- Conflation of "present in an institution" with "good art."

**Subjectivism Red Flags:**
- Wholesale rejection of expertise and education as relevant to aesthetic judgment.
- Treating "disagreement" as evidence of subjectivity without considering that disagreement about objective matters is possible.
- Inability to explain why aesthetic canons shift or why some works endure.
- Inconsistency: may claim taste is personal yet express strong aesthetic judgments.

**Objectivism Red Flags:**
- Confidence in standards without acknowledging historical and cultural variation.
- Circular appeal to expertise: experts are those with good taste; good taste is what experts have.
- Difficulty explaining persistent expert disagreement or aesthetic revisions.
- Risk of aesthetic elitism; dismissal of alternative traditions or tastes as simply "wrong."

**Sublime Red Flags:**
- Conflation of "vast" or "powerful" with "sublime."
- Reduction of sublime to mere emotion (fear, awe) without the structured paradox (pleasure-in-pain, reason-triumphing-over-sensibility).
- Over-application; sees the sublime everywhere.
- Romantic nostalgia; assumes pre-modern or non-Western traditions lacked the sublime.

## Common Confusions and Clarifications

### Confusion 1: Form vs. Content
**Common Mistake:** Treating "form" as identical to "abstract" or "non-representational."
**Clarification:** Form (shape, color, composition, line) exists in representational art too. Formalism claims form is the *only* source of aesthetic value, not that representation is absent. A portrait is evaluated for formal arrangement, not resemblance.

### Confusion 2: Emotion vs. Expression
**Common Mistake:** Conflating "art that evokes emotion in me" with "art that expresses the artist's emotion."
**Clarification:** Expressionism requires the artist to express or communicate an emotion; the viewer's response is secondary and contingent. A work can evoke sadness in the viewer without expressing sadness—it might express joy that the viewer finds discordant.

### Confusion 3: Subjective vs. Objective
**Common Mistake:** "Objective" means universal or unchanging; "subjective" means arbitrary or mere preference.
**Clarification:** Objective properties can be culturally variable and context-dependent (chess beauty standards differ across cultures, yet chess beauty can be real). Subjective properties can be systematic and discoverable (how people reliably respond). Objectivity is about tracking facts independent of individual preference, not universality.

### Confusion 4: Institutional Status vs. Artistic Quality
**Common Mistake:** Assuming that institutional recognition guarantees aesthetic quality.
**Clarification:** Institutionalism defines art status, not quality. A work can be institutional art (in a museum) yet aesthetically bad. Conversely, non-institutional folk art can be aesthetically excellent. Institutionalism answers "what is art?" not "what is good art?"

### Confusion 5: Aesthetic Properties vs. Emotional Response
**Common Mistake:** Treating aesthetic properties as identical to emotional reactions.
**Clarification:** An aesthetic property (gracefulness, boldness, harmony) is a feature of the work or work-context relation. An emotional response is a personal psychological state. They correlate but are distinct. Some aesthetic properties provoke no emotion; some emotional responses don't track aesthetic properties.

### Confusion 6: Beauty vs. Sublime
**Common Mistake:** Using "beautiful" and "sublime" interchangeably or seeing them as points on a spectrum.
**Clarification:** Beauty (in the philosophical sense) is harmonious, comprehensible, and bounded. The sublime is vast, formless, overwhelming, and exceeds comprehension. A beautiful landscape is delightful; a sublime landscape provokes awe-mixed-with-fear. They are categorically distinct, though a single object might be both.

### Confusion 7: Expertise and Authority
**Common Mistake:** Assuming that expertise in aesthetics comes from any authority figure (celebrity, wealth, status).
**Clarification:** Aesthetic expertise, on objectivist accounts, comes from education, attentiveness, familiarity, and refined perception. A celebrity with no art training has no special authority. Expertise is demonstrable through consistent, reasoned judgment and the ability to perceive and articulate aesthetic features others miss.

### Confusion 8: Disinterestedness and Engagement
**Common Mistake:** Assuming that caring about art's meaning or political content means you're not engaging aesthetically.
**Clarification:** Disinterestedness (in Kant's sense) means aesthetic judgment is free from practical desire and moral interest—you're not valuing the art because owning it is useful or because supporting it is morally righteous. But understanding political content or narrative can be part of aesthetic perception; disinterestedness means these don't *motivate* aesthetic judgment (you don't judge the work beautiful because its politics match yours).

## Cross-Branch Integration and Dialectics

### Formalism vs. Expressionism
- **Tension:** Formalists claim content and emotion distract from form; expressionists claim form is merely the vehicle for emotion.
- **Synthesis Attempt:** A work can be formally excellent *and* expressively powerful. Form serves expression; expression shapes form. Neither is reducible to the other.
- **Who Dominates:** Depends on the work and viewer. Abstract art tends toward formalism; representational or figurative art toward expressionism. Both are viable perspectives, differently applicable.

### Objectivism vs. Subjectivism
- **Tension:** Objectivists claim aesthetic facts exist; subjectivists deny this. Disagreement seems to favor subjectivity; expertise seems to favor objectivity.
- **Synthesis Attempt:** Aesthetic properties are relational (dependent on how works appear to perceivers) yet objective (determining facts about those relations). Perception can improve; judgment can be wrong or right, yet disagreement persists. This is consistent with objectivity about relational properties.
- **Who Dominates:** Most theorists occupy a middle ground: aesthetic properties are real but response-dependent, standards are conventional yet binding within a community, expertise is real yet culturally inflected.

### Institutionalism vs. Aesthetic Properties
- **Tension:** Institutionalists deny that aesthetic properties define art; formalists, expressionists, etc., claim they do.
- **Synthesis Attempt:** Art status may be institutional, but *quality* is grounded in aesthetic properties. The artworld decides what is art; aestheticians debate what makes art good.
- **Who Dominates:** Contemporary practice: artworld recognition is necessary for art status, but institutions make better choices when artists and works have strong aesthetic properties. Institutional status without aesthetic merit is fragile and contentious.

### Sublime and Other Schools
- **Sublime + Formalism:** The sublime exceeds form; formalism cannot account for it. Some modernists embrace this: abstraction as the attempt to formalize the formless.
- **Sublime + Expressionism:** Sublime experience is intensely emotional, but the emotion is structured and paradoxical (not simple feeling). Expression of the sublime is difficult and fragmentary.
- **Sublime + Objectivism:** Sublime experiences track real features of objects and the world (vastness, power, infinity). These are objective, even if the experience is overwhelming.
- **Sublime + Subjectivism:** Individual responses to vastness vary; yet the sublime has structure (the mixture of fear and pleasure) that suggests something beyond individual preference.

## Special Cases and Edge Scenarios

### Case 1: Conceptual Art
**Challenge:** Conceptual art (instruction-based, conceptual, often visually uninteresting) seems to support institutionalism over formalism or expressionism.

**Formalist Response:** It's not real art; formalism has no problem dismissing it. Or: formalism reinterprets conceptual art as formal arrangement of ideas/concepts.

**Expressivist Response:** Conceptual art expresses the artist's intellectual or critical intent. The emotion is intellectual-philosophical.

**Institutionalist Response:** By definition, if the artworld presents it as art, it is art.

**Objectivist Response:** Conceptual art can have objective aesthetic properties (cleverness, rigor, depth); these are why institutions and critics value it.

**Diagnostic Implication:** How a viewer responds to conceptual art reveals their aesthetic commitments. Pure formalists reject it; institutionalists accept it readily; objectivists grapple with its properties; expressionists strain to find emotion.

### Case 2: Folk and Traditional Art
**Challenge:** Non-Western, pre-modern, or folk art wasn't created for artworld recognition yet is paradigmatically art.

**Institutionalist Problem:** Must either deny these are art (implausible) or expand "artworld" to include non-institutional recognition (dilutes the concept).

**Formalist and Expressivist Response:** These schools don't require institutional recognition; they can straightforwardly value folk art's formal beauty or emotional expression.

**Objectivist Response:** Folk art has objective aesthetic properties; that's why it endures and is valued across cultures.

**Diagnostic Implication:** Acknowledgment of non-institutional art's validity is incompatible with pure institutionalism. It favors formalism, expressionism, or objectivism.

### Case 3: Kitsch and Bad Taste
**Challenge:** Some popular, commercially successful art is widely dismissed by critics and educated viewers as kitsch or bad taste.

**Subjectivist Problem:** Cannot explain why some taste is "bad"; must treat all preferences as equally valid.

**Objectivist Response:** Kitsch lacks objective aesthetic merit; educated judgment recognizes its shallowness or cliché.

**Formalist Response:** Kitsch is formally shallow; genuine aesthetic value requires formal complexity and refinement.

**Expressivist Response:** Kitsch expresses cheap sentiment; authentic emotion is harder to fake and more rewarding.

**Institutionalist Response:** Kitsch is outside the artworld's serious appreciation; though it may be commodified, it's not valued as art by critical institutions.

**Diagnostic Implication:** Pure subjectivism cannot dismiss kitsch without contradiction. Willingness to critique popular taste aligns with objectivism, formalism, or institutionalism.

### Case 4: The Grotesque and Ugly
**Challenge:** Some celebrated art is deliberately ugly or grotesque. Is ugliness ever aesthetically valuable?

**Formalist Response:** Formal interest (asymmetry, tension, complexity) can create aesthetic value even in ugly content.

**Expressivist Response:** Grotesque or disturbing art expresses difficult emotions or truths; the form's ugliness matches the content's horror.

**Sublime Response:** Grotesque and overwhelming can be sublime—the formless, the excessive, the boundary-violating produces awe.

**Objectivist Response:** Ugliness can have objective aesthetic properties beyond mere unpleasantness (depth, honesty, power).

**Diagnostic Implication:** Valuing "ugly" art requires moving beyond beauty-as-pleasantness. It aligns with expressionism, the sublime, or a more complex objectivism.

### Case 5: Digital and Algorithmic Art
**Challenge:** Art generated by algorithms or AI: is it art? Is it expressive? Does it have aesthetic value?

**Institutionalist Response:** If the artworld presents it as art, it is.

**Expressivist Problem:** Can an algorithm express emotion? Or only the programmer? This strains expressionism.

**Formalist Response:** Digital art can have formal properties; evaluation is by formal merit.

**Objectivist Response:** Algorithmic art can have objective aesthetic properties (complexity, coherence, novelty).

**Diagnostic Implication:** Discomfort with algorithmic art often reflects commitments to expression or authorial intent. Formalism and objectivism adapt more easily.

## Session Summary: What to Listen For

When profiling someone's aesthetics, listen for:

1. **Frame of Reference:** Do they emphasize the work itself (formalism), their emotional response (expressionism), the artworld's judgment (institutionalism), or their personal taste (subjectivism)?

2. **Standards Talk:** Do they appeal to objective criteria (objectivism, formalism) or resist universal standards (subjectivism, institutionalism)?

3. **Authority:** Do they defer to expertise and education (objectivism, formalism) or resist hierarchies (subjectivism)?

4. **Content vs. Form:** Do they care about what art represents or expresses, or only how it's composed?

5. **Emotional Language:** Do they emphasize feeling and authenticity (expressionism) or distance from personal preference (formalism)?

6. **Institutional Language:** Do they mention museums, galleries, critics, the artworld, or do they evaluate art independently?

7. **Comparative Judgments:** Do they say "that's better art" (implying objectivity) or "I prefer that" (implying subjectivity)?

8. **Disagreement Handling:** When confronted with disagreement, do they argue their view is correct, or accept that taste differs?

9. **Overwhelming Experience:** Do they valorize the sublime—awe, vastness, formlessness, confrontation with the infinite—or prefer beauty's harmony?

10. **Non-Western or Folk Art:** Do they readily accept non-institutional art as valuable, or does their framework exclude it?

**Red Flag Combinations:**
- High Subjectivism + Strong Criticism = Inconsistency; they may be relativist in theory but realist in practice.
- High Institutionalism + No Exposure to Non-Western Art = Limited perspective; they haven't tested their framework.
- High Formalism + Complete Indifference to Content = Possible rigidity; they may miss complexity in representational or narrative art.
- High Expressionism + Dismissal of Technical Skill = Possible confusion of authenticity with lack of craft.
