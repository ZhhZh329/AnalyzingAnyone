## Core Question
What is the source and structure of human knowledge? How do we justify our beliefs?

## SEP-Flagged Caution
The SEP warns that the rationalist/empiricist classification "should only be adopted with great caution" because the views of individual philosophers are far more subtle than the classification suggests. Locke (an "empiricist") accepted intuition/deduction for math and morality. The profiler must treat these as spectrums, not bins.

## Schools of Thought

### 1.1 Rationalism
- **SEP Source**: plato.stanford.edu/entries/rationalism-empiricism/
- **Core Thesis**: Reason, rather than experience, is the primary source of knowledge. Defends one or more of: innate knowledge, innate concepts, or intuition/deduction thesis.
- **Key Thinkers**: Descartes, Spinoza, Leibniz
- **Main Arguments**: (1) Mathematical knowledge seems independent of experience. (2) Some concepts (infinity, perfection) cannot be derived from sensory experience. (3) Deductive reasoning yields certainty that observation cannot.
- **Key Objections**: (1) How do innate ideas get activated? (2) Empiricists explain apparent a priori knowledge through abstraction from experience. (3) History of "self-evident" truths being overturned.
- **Diagnostic Markers** (what people say):
  - "You can figure that out just by thinking about it carefully" → strength: strong
  - "Some truths are self-evident" → strength: strong
  - "Logic and math tell us about real things, not just symbols" → strength: moderate
  - "I trust careful reasoning over messy data" → strength: moderate
  - "Experience can be misleading but logic can't" → strength: strong
- **Common Confusions**:
  - Often confused with Foundationalism because both seek bedrock certainty, but Rationalism is about the SOURCE of knowledge (reason), while Foundationalism is about the STRUCTURE (basic vs derived beliefs)
- **Cross-Branch Correlations**:
  - Often pairs with Moral Realism in Ethics (moral truths are knowable by reason)
  - Often pairs with Idealism in Metaphysics (reality is fundamentally rational/mental)
- **Internal Variants**: Continental Rationalism (Descartes, Spinoza, Leibniz), Critical Rationalism (Popper)

### 1.2 Empiricism
- **SEP Source**: plato.stanford.edu/entries/rationalism-empiricism/
- **Core Thesis**: Experience, especially sensory experience, is the primary source of knowledge. Denies innate knowledge and innate concepts.
- **Key Thinkers**: Locke, Hume, Berkeley; modern: Quine
- **Main Arguments**: (1) All ideas trace back to sensory impressions. (2) Claims that can't be verified empirically lack content. (3) Science (humanity's most successful knowledge-producing enterprise) is empirical.
- **Key Objections**: (1) How do we know the senses are reliable without already having knowledge? (2) Mathematical/logical truths seem non-empirical. (3) The problem of induction (Hume).
- **Diagnostic Markers**:
  - "Show me the evidence" → strength: strong
  - "I need to see it to believe it" → strength: moderate
  - "If you can't test it, it's just speculation" → strength: strong
  - "I trust data over theory" → strength: moderate
  - "Experience is the best teacher" → strength: weak (too common)
- **Common Confusions**:
  - Often confused with Scientific Realism (Phil of Science), but empiricism is about knowledge SOURCE while scientific realism is about whether theories are TRUE
  - Often confused with Skepticism, but empiricists affirm that experience CAN produce knowledge
- **Cross-Branch Correlations**:
  - Often pairs with Physicalism in Metaphysics
  - Often pairs with Consequentialism in Ethics (outcome-focused reasoning)
  - Often pairs with Scientific Realism in Phil of Science

### 1.3 Pragmatism
- **SEP Source**: plato.stanford.edu/entries/pragmatism/
- **Core Thesis**: Truth and meaning are understood in terms of practical consequences. Knowledge is validated by effectiveness in practice. Anti-foundationalist.
- **Key Thinkers**: Peirce (pragmaticism — meaning clarification), James (practical satisfactions), Dewey (experimentalism — inquiry as problem-solving)
- **Main Arguments**: (1) Abstract metaphysical disputes with no practical difference are empty. (2) All inquiry is problem-driven. (3) Knowledge is always provisional and revisable (fallibilism).
- **Key Objections**: (1) Conflates truth with usefulness (a false belief can be useful). (2) Vague on what "practical consequences" means. (3) Can't handle pure mathematics.
- **Diagnostic Markers**:
  - "Does it work?" → strength: strong
  - "What difference does it make in practice?" → strength: strong
  - "Theories are tools, not pictures of reality" → strength: strong
  - "I don't care about the theory, show me the results" → strength: moderate
  - "Everything is provisional — we can always revise" → strength: moderate
- **Common Confusions**:
  - Often confused with Instrumentalism (Phil of Science), but pragmatism is broader — it's an epistemology, not just a view about science
  - Often confused with Relativism, but pragmatists believe truth is real, just defined by practical success not correspondence
- **Cross-Branch Correlations**:
  - Often pairs with Compatibilism in Metaphysics (pragmatic resolution of free will)
  - Often pairs with Instrumentalism in Phil of Science
  - Peirce-type pragmatism pairs with Scientific Realism; James-type with Constructivism
- **Internal Variants**: Peirce (logic/semiotics focus), James (lived experience), Dewey (social/democratic inquiry), Rorty (neo-pragmatism, anti-representationalism)

### 1.4 Skepticism
- **SEP Source**: plato.stanford.edu/entries/skepticism/
- **Core Thesis**: Knowledge (or certain kinds) is impossible, unreliable, or unjustified. Ranges from global (no knowledge at all) to local (no knowledge in specific domains).
- **Key Thinkers**: Pyrrho, Sextus Empiricus, Descartes (methodological), Hume (about induction)
- **Main Arguments**: (1) For any belief, there's a possible scenario where it's false (brain in a vat). (2) Induction can't be justified without circularity. (3) The senses demonstrably deceive us sometimes.
- **Key Objections**: (1) Self-refuting (the skeptic claims to know that knowledge is impossible). (2) Pragmatically unlivable. (3) Sets the bar for "knowledge" unreasonably high.
- **Diagnostic Markers**:
  - "How can we ever really know?" → strength: strong
  - "We might all be wrong about everything" → strength: strong
  - "I'm not sure we can trust our own thinking" → strength: moderate
  - "There are always alternative explanations" → strength: moderate
  - "I'm comfortable saying 'I don't know'" → strength: weak (too general)
- **Common Confusions**:
  - Methodological skepticism (Descartes — doubt as tool to find certainty) vs substantive skepticism (no certainty exists)
  - Often confused with Relativism, but skeptics doubt ALL claims while relativists affirm many (relative to context)
- **Cross-Branch Correlations**:
  - Often pairs with Moral Anti-Realism in Ethics
  - Often pairs with Agnosticism in Phil of Religion
  - Academic skepticism vs Pyrrhonian skepticism (suspend judgment vs deny knowledge)

### 1.5 Foundationalism
- **SEP Source**: plato.stanford.edu/entries/justep-foundational/
- **Core Thesis**: Knowledge has a foundational structure. Some beliefs are "basic" (justified without inference) and all other justified beliefs rest on these.
- **Key Thinkers**: Descartes (classical), Plantinga (reformed), modern modest foundationalists
- **Diagnostic Markers**:
  - "We need to start from what's absolutely certain" → strength: strong
  - "There have to be some bedrock truths" → strength: strong
  - "Without foundations, everything collapses" → strength: moderate
  - "First principles matter" → strength: moderate
- **Common Confusions**:
  - Classical foundationalism (basic beliefs must be INFALLIBLE) vs modest foundationalism (basic beliefs just need PRIMA FACIE justification) — very different commitments
  - Often confused with Rationalism, but foundationalism is about STRUCTURE not SOURCE

### 1.6 Coherentism
- **SEP Source**: plato.stanford.edu/entries/justep-coherentist/
- **Core Thesis**: A belief is justified by its coherence with other beliefs in a system. No beliefs are epistemically privileged. Justification is holistic.
- **Key Thinkers**: BonJour (early), Quine (web of belief)
- **Diagnostic Markers**:
  - "It all has to fit together" → strength: strong
  - "I judge beliefs by how well they connect to everything else I know" → strength: strong
  - "There's no view from nowhere — every belief is part of a web" → strength: moderate
  - "Consistency matters more than certainty" → strength: moderate
- **Common Confusions**:
  - The "isolation objection": a perfectly coherent belief system could have no connection to reality. If someone seems coherentist, check whether they care about empirical grounding too.
- **Cross-Branch Correlations**:
  - Often pairs with Holism in Phil of Science
  - Natural fit with systems thinking across domains

### 1.7 Reliabilism
- **SEP Source**: plato.stanford.edu/entries/reliabilism/
- **Core Thesis**: A belief is justified if produced by a reliable cognitive process — one that tends to produce true beliefs. Justification is external (doesn't require the believer's awareness).
- **Key Thinkers**: Goldman
- **Diagnostic Markers**:
  - "I trust this source because it has a good track record" → strength: strong
  - "The method matters more than the feeling of certainty" → strength: moderate
  - "If the process is reliable, the output is justified" → strength: strong
  - "I trust experts and tested methods" → strength: moderate
- **Common Confusions**:
  - Often confused with Empiricism (both trust evidence), but reliabilism is about the PROCESS, not the SOURCE

### 1.8 Virtue Epistemology
- **SEP Source**: plato.stanford.edu/entries/epistemology-virtue/
- **Core Thesis**: Knowledge and justification are understood in terms of intellectual virtues — stable character traits like open-mindedness, intellectual courage, thoroughness. A belief counts as knowledge when it's the product of intellectual virtue.
- **Key Thinkers**: Sosa, Zagzebski
- **Diagnostic Markers**:
  - "Being open-minded is essential to knowing" → strength: strong
  - "The character of the knower matters" → strength: strong
  - "Intellectual humility is a virtue" → strength: moderate
  - "Good thinking requires good character" → strength: moderate
- **Cross-Branch Correlations**:
  - Strong parallel with Virtue Ethics in Ethics (both center on character)

### 1.9 Social Constructivism
- **SEP Source**: plato.stanford.edu/entries/social-epistemology/
- **Core Thesis**: Knowledge is constructed through social processes, linguistic practices, and cultural frameworks rather than discovered as mind-independent truth. What counts as knowledge reflects social power.
- **Key Thinkers**: Berger & Luckmann, Kuhn (partially), Foucault (partially)
- **Diagnostic Markers**:
  - "Knowledge is socially constructed" → strength: strong
  - "What counts as 'true' depends on who has power" → strength: strong
  - "Science is shaped by the culture that produces it" → strength: moderate
  - "There's no view from nowhere" → strength: moderate
  - "Objectivity is a myth" → strength: strong
- **Common Confusions**:
  - Often confused with Relativism, but constructivists may still believe some constructions are better than others
  - Often confused with Pragmatism (both question correspondence truth), but constructivism emphasizes SOCIAL processes while pragmatism emphasizes PRACTICAL consequences

## Spectrum Map
```
Most trust in reason ←————————————————→ Most trust in experience
Rationalism — Foundationalism — Coherentism — Reliabilism — Empiricism — Pragmatism

Most certainty possible ←——————————————→ Least certainty possible
Foundationalism — Rationalism — Reliabilism — Coherentism — Pragmatism — Skepticism

Individual knower ←————————————————————→ Social/collective knowing
Foundationalism — Rationalism — Empiricism — Virtue Epistemology — Constructivism
```

## Probing Questions
Each question targets a specific distinction:
1. "When you really need to figure something out, do you prefer to think it through logically or go look at the evidence?" — distinguishes Rationalism from Empiricism
2. "Do you think there are some things we can just KNOW without having to check?" — distinguishes Rationalism from Empiricism/Skepticism
3. "When two experts disagree, how do you decide who's right?" — distinguishes Reliabilism from Virtue Epistemology
4. "Is there a foundation everything else rests on, or is knowledge more like a web where everything supports everything?" — distinguishes Foundationalism from Coherentism
5. "If a belief works perfectly in practice but can't be proven 'true,' does it count as knowledge?" — distinguishes Pragmatism from Rationalism/Empiricism
6. "How much does your culture and background affect what you 'know'?" — distinguishes Constructivism from Rationalism/Empiricism
7. "Can we ever be 100% certain about anything?" — gauges Skepticism
8. "Is good thinking more about following the right method or having the right character?" — distinguishes Reliabilism from Virtue Epistemology
9. "Do you think science discovers truth or constructs useful models?" — distinguishes Empiricism/Realism from Pragmatism/Constructivism
10. "If something has always worked before, can you count on it working again?" — probes Problem of Induction (Empiricism vs Skepticism)

## Scoring Rubric
- **Strong signal**: Person explicitly argues for the position or uses its key concepts (e.g., "knowledge must be grounded in basic beliefs" = strong Foundationalism)
- **Moderate signal**: Person's reasoning pattern aligns without explicit commitment (e.g., consistently appeals to evidence without naming empiricism)
- **Weak signal**: Passing comment or single data point
- **Negative signal**: Person explicitly rejects this position
- **Confidence thresholds**:
  - High: 3+ strong signals OR 5+ moderate signals
  - Moderate: 1-2 strong signals OR 3-4 moderate signals
  - Low: Only weak signals
  - Undetermined: Insufficient signal (fewer than 2 weak signals)
- Percentages must sum to 100% within the branch
- Allow hybrid positions (e.g., 40% Empiricism, 30% Pragmatism, 20% Reliabilism, 10% Undetermined)
- Flag contradictions (e.g., strong Foundationalism + strong Coherentism = tension worth surfacing)
