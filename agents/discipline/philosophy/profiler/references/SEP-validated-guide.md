# Philosophical Profiling Skills — SEP-Validated Guide

## Purpose

This guide provides **Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy (SEP)–validated** definitions, distinctions, and diagnostic criteria for every school within each major branch of philosophy. Each entry is cross-referenced against SEP articles to ensure academic accuracy. Skills built from this guide will analyze a person's philosophical standing with rigorous grounding.

**SEP Base URL**: `https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/`

---

## How to Use This Document

For each branch, you'll find:
1. **SEP Reference(s)** — The specific SEP article(s) used to validate each school
2. **SEP-Validated Definition** — The school's core claim, stated in terms consistent with SEP
3. **SEP-Noted Distinctions** — Important nuances the SEP highlights that naive classifications miss
4. **Diagnostic Markers** — What to listen for when profiling (derived from SEP descriptions)
5. **SEP-Flagged Cautions** — Classification pitfalls the SEP explicitly warns against

## BRANCH 1: EPISTEMOLOGY

**SEP Articles**: `rationalism-empiricism`, `epistemology`, `pragmatism`, `skepticism`, `foundationalism`, `coherentism-epistemology`, `reliabilism`, `social-epistemology`, `epistemology-virtue`

### SEP-Flagged Caution (Critical)
The SEP warns that the rationalist/empiricist classification "should only be adopted with great caution" because "the views of the individual philosophers are a lot more subtle and complex than the simple-minded classification suggests." For example, Locke rejected innate knowledge but accepted intuition/deduction for mathematics and morality. Descartes and Locke had "remarkably similar views on the nature of our ideas." The profiling skill must therefore treat these as spectrums, not bins.

### Schools

#### 1.1 Rationalism
- **SEP Article**: `rationalism-empiricism`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: The view that reason, rather than experience, is the primary source of knowledge. Rationalists typically defend one or more of: (a) the Innate Knowledge thesis (we have knowledge not derived from experience), (b) the Innate Concept thesis (we have concepts not derived from experience), or (c) the Intuition/Deduction thesis (some propositions are known by intuition alone or by deduction from intuited premises).
- **Key Thinkers (per SEP)**: Descartes, Spinoza, Leibniz (the "Continental Rationalists")
- **Diagnostic Markers**: Appeals to logical self-evidence; trusts mathematical/deductive reasoning over observation; believes some knowledge is innate or a priori; comfortable with metaphysical claims based on reason alone
- **SEP-Noted Distinction**: The SEP emphasizes that rationalism is not exhaustive — Divine revelation is an alternative source neither rational nor empirical. The label can "impede rather than advance our understanding" when applied too broadly.

#### 1.2 Empiricism
- **SEP Article**: `rationalism-empiricism`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: The view that experience, especially sensory experience, is the primary source of knowledge. Empiricists typically deny innate knowledge and innate concepts, holding that all ideas trace back to sensory experience.
- **Key Thinkers (per SEP)**: Locke, Hume, Berkeley (the "British Empiricists"); also Reid in some formulations
- **Diagnostic Markers**: Demands evidence, observation, and data; distrusts purely abstract reasoning; suspicious of claims that can't be verified empirically; favors the scientific method
- **SEP-Noted Distinction**: Locke, despite being classified as empiricist, accepted the Intuition/Deduction thesis for knowledge of God, mathematics, and morality. Classification must account for these hybrid positions.

#### 1.3 Pragmatism
- **SEP Article**: `pragmatism`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: Truth and meaning are understood in terms of practical consequences and usefulness. Knowledge is validated by its effectiveness in practice rather than by correspondence to abstract reality.
- **Key Thinkers**: Peirce, James, Dewey
- **Diagnostic Markers**: Evaluates ideas by asking "does it work?"; resists abstract metaphysics; focuses on consequences and problem-solving; comfortable with provisional truth; anti-foundationalist
- **SEP-Noted Distinction**: Peirce's pragmatism (pragmaticism) differs significantly from James's — Peirce focused on meaning clarification, James on practical satisfactions. The skill should distinguish these variants.

#### 1.4 Skepticism
- **SEP Article**: `skepticism`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: The view that knowledge (or certain kinds of knowledge) is impossible, unreliable, or unjustified. Ranges from global skepticism (no knowledge at all) to local skepticism (no knowledge in specific domains like morality or the external world).
- **Key Varieties**: Pyrrhonian (suspension of judgment), Cartesian (methodological doubt), Humean (about induction), Modern (about external world)
- **Diagnostic Markers**: Persistent doubt; questions assumptions others take for granted; "how can we ever really know?"; highlights the limits of human cognition; comfortable with uncertainty

#### 1.5 Foundationalism
- **SEP Article**: `justep-foundational`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: The view that knowledge has a foundational structure — some beliefs are "basic" (justified without inference from other beliefs) and all other justified beliefs ultimately rest on these basic beliefs.
- **Diagnostic Markers**: Seeks bedrock principles; uncomfortable with circular reasoning; wants to identify "self-evident" starting points; tends toward systematic thinking
- **SEP-Noted Distinction**: Classical foundationalism (basic beliefs must be infallible) vs. modest foundationalism (basic beliefs need only be prima facie justified) are very different commitments.

#### 1.6 Coherentism
- **SEP Article**: `justep-coherentist`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: The view that a belief is justified not by resting on basic beliefs but by its coherence with other beliefs in a system. No beliefs are epistemically privileged; justification is holistic.
- **Diagnostic Markers**: Evaluates beliefs by how they fit together; comfortable with mutual support among beliefs; distrusts the idea of "self-evident" truths; systems thinking
- **SEP-Noted Distinction**: Coherentism faces the "isolation objection" — a perfectly coherent set of beliefs could have no connection to reality. The skill should note if someone shows awareness of this.

#### 1.7 Reliabilism
- **SEP Article**: `reliabilism`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: A belief is justified if it is produced by a reliable cognitive process — one that tends to produce true beliefs. Justification is an external matter, not requiring the believer's awareness of the process's reliability.
- **Key Thinkers**: Goldman
- **Diagnostic Markers**: Trusts well-tested methods and institutions; "this source is reliable"; less concerned with introspective justification; process-oriented thinking about knowledge

#### 1.8 Virtue Epistemology
- **SEP Article**: `epistemology-virtue`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: Knowledge and justification are understood in terms of intellectual virtues — stable character traits like open-mindedness, intellectual courage, and thoroughness. A belief counts as knowledge when it is the product of intellectual virtue.
- **Key Thinkers**: Sosa, Zagzebski
- **Diagnostic Markers**: Emphasizes intellectual character; values open-mindedness, curiosity, and intellectual honesty as epistemic goods; judges knowledge claims partly by the character of the knower

#### 1.9 Constructivism / Social Constructivism
- **SEP Article**: `social-epistemology`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: Knowledge is constructed through social processes, linguistic practices, and cultural frameworks rather than discovered as mind-independent truth. What counts as knowledge reflects social power structures and conventions.
- **Diagnostic Markers**: "Knowledge is socially constructed"; emphasizes cultural context; suspicious of claims to objective, culture-free knowledge; highlights how power shapes what is considered "true"

## BRANCH 2: ETHICS (Moral Philosophy)

**SEP Articles**: `ethics-virtue`, `ethics-deontological`, `consequentialism`, `care-ethics`, `contractarianism`, `moral-relativism`, `moral-realism`, `moral-anti-realism`, `natural-law-ethics`, `moral-particularism`

### SEP-Flagged Caution
The SEP emphasizes that "any plausible normative ethical theory will have something to say about" virtues, consequences, and rules. The three major frameworks (virtue ethics, deontology, consequentialism) are distinguished by which concept is most foundational, not by exclusive concern with only one. The profiling skill must capture this nuance — most people draw from multiple frameworks.

### Schools

#### 2.1 Virtue Ethics
- **SEP Article**: `ethics-virtue`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: The approach that emphasizes virtues (excellences of character) as foundational, in contrast to duty-based or consequence-based frameworks. A virtue is "an excellent trait of character" — not merely a tendency to act well but a deep disposition involving emotion, perception, and practical wisdom (phronesis). Central concepts: arête (excellence), phronesis (practical wisdom), eudaimonia (flourishing).
- **Key Thinkers (per SEP)**: Plato, Aristotle (Western founders); Mencius, Confucius (Eastern); modern revival: Anscombe (1958), MacIntyre, Foot, Hursthouse
- **Diagnostic Markers**: Asks "what kind of person should I be?" rather than "what should I do?"; values character and moral development; thinks about role models; emphasizes practical wisdom over rules; connects morality to human flourishing
- **SEP-Noted Distinction**: "Virtue ethics" (the third major approach) must be distinguished from "virtue theory" (which can exist within consequentialism or deontology). Also: the fully virtuous person acts well "without a struggle against contrary desires" — the distinction between virtue and mere continence (strength of will) is diagnostically important.

#### 2.2 Deontology
- **SEP Article**: `ethics-deontological`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: Normative theories that judge the morality of choices "by criteria different from the states of affairs those choices bring about." Some choices are morally forbidden regardless of their consequences. Deontology's strengths lie in (1) categorical prohibition of acts like killing innocents even for good outcomes, and (2) permission for individuals to pursue their own projects without constant demands to maximize others' welfare.
- **Key Thinkers**: Kant (categorical imperative), Ross (prima facie duties)
- **Key Varieties (per SEP)**: Agent-centered (constraints on one's own actions), patient-centered (rights of the affected), contractualist (principles no one could reasonably reject — Scanlon)
- **Diagnostic Markers**: "Some things are just wrong regardless of consequences"; appeals to duties, rights, and obligations; uncomfortable with "the ends justify the means"; values moral rules and principles; Kantian reasoning ("could everyone do this?")
- **SEP-Noted Distinction**: The SEP notes that deontological concepts (rights, duties, permissions) fit uneasily within realist-naturalist metaethics, which may explain why "many naturalists, if they are moral realists, are consequentialists." Profiling should note metaethical compatibility.

#### 2.3 Consequentialism / Utilitarianism
- **SEP Article**: `consequentialism`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: Whether an act is morally right depends only on its consequences. All consequentialists agree that morally right choices increase the Good. The Good is typically understood as "agent-neutral" — states of affairs that all agents have reason to achieve regardless of personal involvement.
- **Key Thinkers**: Bentham, Mill, Sidgwick, Singer
- **Key Varieties (per SEP)**: Act vs. rule consequentialism; maximizing vs. satisficing; hedonistic vs. preference vs. objective-list theories of the Good
- **Diagnostic Markers**: Evaluates actions by outcomes; "what produces the most good?"; willing to sacrifice one for many; cost-benefit thinking; focuses on aggregate welfare
- **SEP-Noted Distinction**: The SEP notes that consequentialists can explain many moral intuitions that trouble deontologists — e.g., when two promises conflict, which to keep, and where to draw lines between permissible and impermissible killing. The term "consequentialism" functions as a "family resemblance term" with no firm agreement on exact boundaries.

#### 2.4 Care Ethics
- **SEP Article**: `feminism-ethics`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: Morality is fundamentally about caring relationships. Moral action arises from attentiveness to the needs of particular others in concrete situations, not from abstract principles. Challenges the emphasis on impartiality and universality in traditional ethics.
- **Key Thinkers**: Gilligan, Noddings, Held
- **Diagnostic Markers**: Emphasizes relationships and responsibilities to specific people; "it depends on the situation and who's involved"; values empathy and attentiveness; skeptical of abstract moral rules that ignore context

#### 2.5 Contractarianism / Contractualism
- **SEP Article**: `contractarianism`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: Morality is understood as a set of rules that rational, self-interested agents would agree to for mutual advantage (Hobbesian contractarianism), or as principles that no one could reasonably reject (Scanlonian contractualism).
- **Key Thinkers**: Hobbes, Rawls, Gauthier, Scanlon
- **Diagnostic Markers**: Frames morality in terms of agreements and fairness; "what rules would everyone agree to?"; uses thought experiments like veils of ignorance; social contract reasoning
- **SEP-Noted Distinction**: The SEP notes that it's "not entirely clear whether a contractualist account is really normative as opposed to metaethical" — this ambiguity is itself diagnostically useful.

#### 2.6 Moral Relativism
- **SEP Article**: `moral-relativism`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: Moral judgments are true or false only relative to particular standpoints (cultural, individual, or historical). There are no universal moral truths.
- **Diagnostic Markers**: "Morality differs across cultures and that's fine"; resists moral judgment of other cultures; "who are we to say?"; emphasizes cultural context in moral evaluation

#### 2.7 Moral Realism
- **SEP Article**: `moral-realism`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: There are objective moral facts, independent of what anyone thinks or feels. Moral statements can be literally true or false.
- **Diagnostic Markers**: "Slavery was objectively wrong, not just wrong by our standards"; believes moral progress is real; thinks moral disagreements have correct answers

#### 2.8 Emotivism / Expressivism
- **SEP Article**: `moral-cognitivism` (non-cognitivism section)
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: Moral statements don't express beliefs that can be true or false — they express attitudes, emotions, or prescriptions. "Murder is wrong" means something like "Boo murder!" (Ayer) or "Don't murder!" (Hare).
- **Key Thinkers**: Ayer, Stevenson, Hare, Blackburn, Gibbard
- **Diagnostic Markers**: "That's just your opinion"; treats moral disagreement as a clash of feelings rather than beliefs; skeptical that moral claims are factual; "morality is just how we feel about things"

#### 2.9 Natural Law Theory
- **SEP Article**: `natural-law-ethics`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: Morality is grounded in human nature as created/structured by God or in objective features of practical reason. Certain basic goods (life, knowledge, friendship, etc.) are self-evidently good and provide the foundation for moral reasoning.
- **Key Thinkers**: Aquinas, Finnis
- **Diagnostic Markers**: Appeals to human nature or "the way things are meant to be"; connects morality to purpose/teleology; believes moral truths are discoverable by reason; often (but not always) religious grounding

#### 2.10 Moral Particularism
- **SEP Article**: `moral-particularism`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: Moral judgment is not guided by general principles. The same feature (e.g., causing pain) can count for or against an action depending on context. There are no moral principles that hold in all cases.
- **Key Thinkers**: Dancy
- **Diagnostic Markers**: "It all depends on the specific situation"; resists moral generalizations; emphasizes judgment over rules; each moral situation is unique

## BRANCH 3: METAPHYSICS

**SEP Articles**: `metaphysics`, `idealism`, `physicalism`, `dualism`, `monism`, `nominalism-metaphysics`, `realism`, `determinism-causal`, `freewill`, `compatibilism`

### Schools

#### 3.1 Idealism
- **SEP Article**: `idealism`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: Reality is fundamentally mental or mind-dependent. Physical objects either don't exist independently of mind (Berkeley) or are manifestations of a universal mind/spirit (Hegel).
- **Key Thinkers**: Plato (Forms), Berkeley (subjective idealism), Hegel (absolute idealism), Kant (transcendental idealism)
- **Diagnostic Markers**: "Reality is ultimately mental"; consciousness is primary; the physical world depends on perception or thought; "to be is to be perceived"

#### 3.2 Physicalism / Materialism
- **SEP Article**: `physicalism`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: Everything that exists is physical or supervenes on the physical. There are no non-physical substances, properties, or facts.
- **Diagnostic Markers**: "Everything is ultimately matter and energy"; consciousness will eventually be explained by neuroscience; no room for souls, spirits, or non-physical entities; trusts the natural sciences as the ultimate arbiter of what exists

#### 3.3 Dualism
- **SEP Article**: `dualism`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: Mind and body — or the mental and the physical — "are, in some fundamental sense, different kinds of things." Dualism is the second most popular response to the mind-body problem among professional philosophers, after materialism and ahead of idealism and panpsychism (per a 2020 survey cited in SEP).
- **Key Varieties (per SEP)**: Substance dualism (mind and body are different substances — Descartes); property dualism (physical substances can have irreducibly mental properties — Chalmers); predicate dualism (mental and physical predicates are not inter-translatable)
- **Diagnostic Markers**: "Consciousness can't be fully explained by physics"; "there's something about subjective experience that goes beyond the brain"; mind and body feel fundamentally different; open to souls or non-physical aspects of mind
- **SEP-Noted Distinction**: The SEP notes that "dualism is very common in the history of ideas" and "intuitively appealing, even among its opponents." The meta-problem of consciousness (why dualism seems true regardless of whether it is) is itself diagnostically relevant — someone attracted to dualism may be responding to genuine philosophical puzzles rather than naivety.

#### 3.4 Monism
- **SEP Article**: `monism`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: There is only one fundamental kind of substance or reality. Can take multiple forms: materialist monism (everything is physical), idealist monism (everything is mental), neutral monism (reality is fundamentally neither mental nor physical but some neutral substance).
- **Key Thinkers**: Spinoza, Russell (neutral monism)
- **Diagnostic Markers**: Seeks unified explanations; uncomfortable with fundamental dualisms; "it's all one thing at bottom"

#### 3.5 Nominalism vs. Realism (about universals)
- **SEP Article**: `nominalism-metaphysics`, `universals-medieval`
- **Nominalism**: Only particular things exist; universals (like "redness" or "justice") are just names we use to group similar things. There are no abstract objects.
- **Realism**: Universals exist independently of the things that instantiate them. There really is such a thing as "redness" or "justice" beyond any particular red thing or just act.
- **Diagnostic Markers (Nominalism)**: "There's no such thing as 'justice' floating out there — just individual just acts"; skeptical of abstract entities
- **Diagnostic Markers (Realism)**: "Mathematical truths exist independently of us"; believes in objective abstract entities; Platonist tendencies

#### 3.6 Free Will Positions
- **SEP Articles**: `freewill`, `determinism-causal`, `compatibilism`
- **Determinism**: Every event (including human decisions) is causally necessitated by prior events plus laws of nature. Free will is an illusion.
- **Libertarianism (metaphysical)**: Genuine free will exists and is incompatible with determinism. Agents have the power to act otherwise.
- **Compatibilism**: Free will and determinism are compatible. Freedom means acting according to one's desires without external constraint, even if those desires are determined.
- **Diagnostic Markers (Determinism)**: "We're just following the laws of physics"; "free will is an illusion"; fatalism about human choice
- **Diagnostic Markers (Libertarianism)**: "I could have genuinely done otherwise"; strong sense of personal agency; resists reduction of choice to physics
- **Diagnostic Markers (Compatibilism)**: "Free will just means acting on your own desires"; redefines freedom rather than denying it; pragmatic about the free will debate

## BRANCH 4: PHILOSOPHY OF MIND

**SEP Articles**: `dualism`, `functionalism`, `physicalism`, `behaviorism`, `mind-identity`, `panpsychism`, `consciousness`, `eliminative-materialism`, `phenomenology`

### SEP-Flagged Caution
The SEP notes that the mind-body problem involves both consciousness and thought ("broadly construed"), and that "there is no convincing consensus on how to build a satisfactorily unified picture of creatures possessed of both a mind and a body." Materialist views (behaviorism, functionalism, identity theory, computational theory) attempt to explain mental states as physical states, but they differ significantly in how.

### Schools

#### 4.1 Substance Dualism
- **SEP Article**: `dualism`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: Mind and body are fundamentally different substances. The mind is non-physical and distinct from the brain.
- **Key Thinker**: Descartes
- **Diagnostic Markers**: Believes in souls or non-physical minds; "my consciousness is separate from my brain"; strong intuitions about an afterlife

#### 4.2 Property Dualism
- **SEP Article**: `dualism` (property dualism section), `qualia`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: The physical world has irreducibly mental properties (qualia, phenomenal consciousness) even though there is only one kind of substance (physical). Consciousness is a real but non-physical property of physical brains.
- **Key Thinker**: Chalmers ("hard problem of consciousness")
- **Diagnostic Markers**: "Consciousness is real but can't be reduced to brain activity"; takes the "hard problem" seriously; believes in qualia; "there's something it's like to be conscious that physics can't capture"

#### 4.3 Functionalism
- **SEP Article**: `functionalism`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: Mental states are defined by their functional roles — their causal relations with sensory inputs, behavioral outputs, and other mental states. The same mental state can be "realized" by different physical substrates (silicon, neurons, etc.). This "multiple realizability" is functionalism's key insight.
- **Key Thinkers**: Putnam (early), Fodor (computational version)
- **Diagnostic Markers**: "What matters is the function, not the substrate"; "a sufficiently complex computer could be conscious"; thinks about minds in terms of inputs, outputs, and processes; comfortable with AI consciousness in principle
- **SEP-Noted Distinction**: Functionalism is compatible with both materialism and dualism, since functional roles could in principle be filled by non-physical states. This is an important nuance for profiling.

#### 4.4 Identity Theory (Mind-Brain Identity)
- **SEP Article**: `mind-identity`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: Each type of mental state is identical with a particular type of neural/brain state. Pain just IS C-fiber firing (for example).
- **Key Thinkers**: Place, Smart, Armstrong
- **Diagnostic Markers**: "Mental states literally are brain states"; "consciousness is just what the brain does"; straight reduction of mind to brain
- **SEP-Noted Distinction**: SEP notes this seems to entail that "no creatures with brains unlike ours can share our sensations" — a "species-chauvinistic" implication that motivated functionalism as an alternative.

#### 4.5 Eliminative Materialism
- **SEP Article**: `eliminative-materialism`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: Our commonsense understanding of the mind ("folk psychology" — beliefs, desires, intentions) is radically false and will eventually be replaced by mature neuroscience, much as "phlogiston" was eliminated in chemistry.
- **Key Thinkers**: Churchland, Churchland
- **Diagnostic Markers**: "Beliefs and desires don't really exist"; "folk psychology is a bad theory"; expects neuroscience to replace mental vocabulary; radical reductionism

#### 4.6 Panpsychism
- **SEP Article**: `panpsychism`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: Consciousness (or proto-consciousness) is a fundamental feature of all physical matter, not just brains. Even elementary particles have some form of experiential quality.
- **Key Thinkers**: Chalmers (sympathetic), Goff, Strawson
- **Diagnostic Markers**: "Consciousness is everywhere in nature, not just in brains"; sees mind as fundamental rather than emergent; comfortable with consciousness in simple systems
- **SEP-Noted Distinction**: The SEP notes panpsychism "has gained prominence in recent discussions" as a kind of theory where "things described by physical science are deep-down, partly or wholly, mental in nature."

#### 4.7 Phenomenology (of Mind)
- **SEP Article**: `phenomenology`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: The study of the structures of first-person experience. Understanding consciousness requires examining experience from within, not reducing it to physical processes.
- **Key Thinkers**: Husserl, Merleau-Ponty, Heidegger
- **Diagnostic Markers**: Prioritizes subjective experience; "you can't understand consciousness from the outside"; values introspection and lived experience; suspicious of purely third-person accounts

#### 4.8 Enactivism / Extended Mind
- **SEP Article**: `embodied-cognition`, `extended-mind`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: Mind is not confined to the brain but extends into the body and environment. Cognition is enacted through bodily interaction with the world, not just computed inside the skull.
- **Key Thinkers**: Varela, Thompson (enactivism), Clark and Chalmers (extended mind)
- **Diagnostic Markers**: "My smartphone is part of my mind"; thinking is embodied; cognition involves the whole organism-environment system

## BRANCH 5: POLITICAL PHILOSOPHY

**SEP Articles**: `liberalism`, `libertarianism`, `conservatism`, `socialism`, `communitarianism`, `anarchism`, `republicanism`, `feminism-political`, `cosmopolitanism`, `justice-distributive`

### Schools

#### 5.1 Classical Liberalism
- **SEP Article**: `liberalism`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: Individual rights, limited government, rule of law, and personal liberty are paramount. Government's role is to protect natural rights (life, liberty, property).
- **Key Thinkers**: Locke, Mill, Adam Smith
- **Diagnostic Markers**: Prioritizes individual freedom; "the government that governs least governs best"; strong property rights; tolerant of inequality if liberty is preserved

#### 5.2 Egalitarian Liberalism
- **SEP Article**: `justice-distributive`, `rawls`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: Liberty must be combined with fair equality of opportunity and provisions for the least advantaged. Justice requires institutions that distribute benefits and burdens fairly.
- **Key Thinkers**: Rawls (veil of ignorance, difference principle)
- **Diagnostic Markers**: "What would be fair if no one knew their position?"; concerned with inequality; supports social safety nets; fairness-focused

#### 5.3 Libertarianism (political)
- **SEP Article**: `libertarianism`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: Individual liberty and property rights are near-absolute. The state should be minimal (night-watchman state) or nonexistent. Taxation for redistribution is coercive.
- **Key Thinkers**: Nozick, Hayek, Rothbard
- **Diagnostic Markers**: "Taxation is theft"; maximally skeptical of government; strong self-ownership; free markets as moral imperative

#### 5.4 Conservatism
- **SEP Article**: `conservatism`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: Values tradition, organic social order, and gradual change. Skeptical of radical reform and abstract theory. Social institutions embody accumulated wisdom.
- **Key Thinkers**: Burke, Oakeshott
- **Diagnostic Markers**: "Don't fix what isn't broken"; values tradition and custom; skeptical of utopian schemes; respects existing institutions

#### 5.5 Socialism / Marxism
- **SEP Article**: `socialism`, `marx`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: Advocates collective or social ownership of the means of production. Marxism specifically analyzes society through class struggle and historical materialism.
- **Key Thinkers**: Marx, Engels, contemporary democratic socialists
- **Diagnostic Markers**: Class-consciousness; "capitalism exploits workers"; supports collective ownership; analyzes power in economic terms

#### 5.6 Communitarianism
- **SEP Article**: `communitarianism`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: Critiques liberal individualism. The self is constituted by community ties, not prior to them. Shared values, traditions, and the common good should have weight alongside individual rights.
- **Key Thinkers**: MacIntyre, Sandel, Taylor, Walzer
- **Diagnostic Markers**: "We are shaped by our communities"; emphasizes belonging and shared identity; "individual rights aren't everything"; values the common good

#### 5.7 Anarchism
- **SEP Article**: `anarchism`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: Rejects the state and all coercive hierarchical authority as illegitimate. Advocates voluntary association and mutual aid.
- **Key Thinkers**: Bakunin, Kropotkin, Goldman
- **Diagnostic Markers**: "All government is coercion"; favors voluntary cooperation; deeply skeptical of authority; values autonomy above all

#### 5.8 Republicanism
- **SEP Article**: `republicanism`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: Freedom is understood as non-domination — not being subject to another's arbitrary will, even if they don't actually interfere. Requires active citizenship and strong civic institutions.
- **Key Thinkers**: Pettit, Skinner
- **Diagnostic Markers**: "Freedom means nobody can push you around, even if they choose not to"; values civic participation; concerned with power imbalances even without overt oppression

#### 5.9 Cosmopolitanism
- **SEP Article**: `cosmopolitanism`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: All human beings belong to a single moral community. Obligations extend beyond national borders. Global justice and human rights take priority over national sovereignty.
- **Key Thinkers**: Nussbaum, Appiah
- **Diagnostic Markers**: "National borders are morally arbitrary"; global perspective; supports international institutions; concerned with global inequality

## BRANCH 6: PHILOSOPHY OF SCIENCE

**SEP Articles**: `scientific-realism`, `scientific-method`, `karl-popper`, `thomas-kuhn`, `feyerabend`, `constructive-empiricism`, `logical-empiricism`

### Schools

#### 6.1 Logical Positivism / Logical Empiricism
- **SEP Article**: `logical-empiricism`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: Meaningful statements are either analytically true (true by definition) or empirically verifiable. Metaphysical claims that are neither are literally meaningless.
- **Key Thinkers**: Carnap, Ayer, Vienna Circle
- **Diagnostic Markers**: "If you can't verify it, it's meaningless"; strong empiricism about science; dismisses metaphysics; verificationist about meaning

#### 6.2 Falsificationism
- **SEP Article**: `karl-popper`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: Science advances through bold conjectures and attempted refutations. A theory is scientific if and only if it is falsifiable. Theories are never confirmed, only corroborated by surviving tests.
- **Key Thinkers**: Popper
- **Diagnostic Markers**: "The mark of science is that it can be proven wrong"; values bold predictions; skeptical of confirmation; emphasizes testing and criticism

#### 6.3 Kuhnian Paradigm Theory
- **SEP Article**: `thomas-kuhn`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: Science proceeds through periods of "normal science" (puzzle-solving within a paradigm) punctuated by revolutionary paradigm shifts. Scientists within different paradigms may be working in "different worlds."
- **Key Thinkers**: Kuhn
- **Diagnostic Markers**: Thinks about science as shaped by community consensus; "scientific revolutions change everything"; aware of the social dimensions of science; skeptical of purely cumulative progress

#### 6.4 Scientific Realism
- **SEP Article**: `scientific-realism`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: Mature scientific theories are approximately true descriptions of the world, including unobservable entities (electrons, quarks, etc.). The success of science is best explained by its theories being (roughly) true.
- **Diagnostic Markers**: "Electrons really exist"; trusts science to reveal how the world really is; "the success of science would be a miracle if its theories weren't true"

#### 6.5 Instrumentalism
- **SEP Article**: `scientific-realism` (contrasted)
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: Scientific theories are useful tools for prediction and control, not descriptions of reality. Whether theoretical entities "really exist" is beside the point.
- **Diagnostic Markers**: "Science gives us useful models, not truth"; doesn't care if electrons "really" exist as long as the math works; pragmatic about scientific claims

#### 6.6 Constructive Empiricism
- **SEP Article**: `constructive-empiricism`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: Science aims to produce theories that are empirically adequate (save the phenomena) — not necessarily true about unobservable entities. We should believe what a theory says about observables but remain agnostic about unobservables.
- **Key Thinkers**: van Fraassen
- **Diagnostic Markers**: "I believe in what I can observe; the rest is optional"; agnostic about theoretical entities; empirically careful

#### 6.7 Methodological Anarchism
- **SEP Article**: `feyerabend`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: There is no universal scientific method. "Anything goes" — science has advanced through rule-breaking as often as rule-following. Methodological pluralism is essential.
- **Key Thinkers**: Feyerabend
- **Diagnostic Markers**: "There's no single scientific method"; celebrates unconventional approaches; skeptical of methodological orthodoxy

## BRANCH 7: PHILOSOPHY OF RELIGION

**SEP Articles**: `philosophy-religion`, `cosmological-argument`, `teleological-arguments`, `ontological-arguments`, `problem-evil`, `faith`, `reformed-epistemology`, `process-theism`, `religious-pluralism`

### Schools

#### 7.1 Theism (Classical)
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: God exists as an omniscient, omnipotent, perfectly good, personal being who created and sustains the universe.
- **Diagnostic Markers**: Believes in a personal God; trusts arguments for God's existence; prayer is meaningful; theodicy is a serious puzzle but not fatal

#### 7.2 Deism
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: God created the universe but does not intervene in it. No miracles, revelation, or personal relationship with God.
- **Diagnostic Markers**: "God set things in motion then stepped back"; rejects miracles; religion of reason

#### 7.3 Atheism (Philosophical)
- **SEP Article**: `atheism-agnosticism`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: The belief that God does not exist, supported by arguments (problem of evil, divine hiddenness, lack of evidence).
- **Diagnostic Markers**: "There's no evidence for God"; the problem of evil is decisive; naturalistic worldview

#### 7.4 Agnosticism
- **SEP Article**: `atheism-agnosticism`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: Suspension of judgment about God's existence — the evidence is insufficient either way.
- **Diagnostic Markers**: "We can't know either way"; comfortable with uncertainty about God; neither affirms nor denies

#### 7.5 Fideism
- **SEP Article**: `faith`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: Religious belief is properly based on faith rather than reason or evidence. Reason may be inadequate or inappropriate for religious truth.
- **Key Thinkers**: Kierkegaard (leap of faith)
- **Diagnostic Markers**: "You just have to believe"; faith transcends reason; proud of believing without (or against) evidence

#### 7.6 Reformed Epistemology
- **SEP Article**: `reformed-epistemology`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: Belief in God can be "properly basic" — justified without argument or evidence from other beliefs, similar to trust in memory or perception.
- **Key Thinkers**: Plantinga, Wolterstorff
- **Diagnostic Markers**: "I don't need proof — belief in God is as basic as belief in other minds"; rejects the demand for evidence as the only path to justified belief

#### 7.7 Process Theology
- **SEP Article**: `process-theism`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: God is not static and omnipotent in the classical sense but is in process, evolving with creation. God persuades rather than coerces, and genuinely changes in response to the world.
- **Key Thinkers**: Whitehead, Hartshorne
- **Diagnostic Markers**: "God changes and grows with us"; rejects divine omnipotence; relational view of God

#### 7.8 Religious Pluralism
- **SEP Article**: `religious-pluralism`
- **SEP-Validated Definition**: Multiple religions offer valid paths to the same ultimate reality. No single religion has exclusive access to truth.
- **Key Thinkers**: Hick
- **Diagnostic Markers**: "All religions are paths up the same mountain"; respects all traditions equally; pluralistic and inclusive

## BRANCH 8: AESTHETICS

**SEP Articles**: `aesthetics`, `aesthetic-judgment`, `art-definition`, `beauty`

### Schools

#### 8.1 Formalism — Focus on form, structure, proportion, and composition as the source of aesthetic value. Art is autonomous from external meaning.
#### 8.2 Expressionism — Art's value lies in expressing or evoking emotion. (Tolstoy, Croce, Collingwood)
#### 8.3 Institutionalism — Art is whatever the artworld (critics, galleries, museums) recognizes as art. (Danto, Dickie)
#### 8.4 Aesthetic Subjectivism — Beauty is purely in the eye of the beholder. No objective aesthetic facts.
#### 8.5 Aesthetic Objectivism — Beauty is a real, mind-independent property. Some things really are more beautiful.
#### 8.6 The Sublime — Experiences of overwhelming awe that go beyond beauty. (Kant's "dynamical sublime," Burke's terror-based sublime)

## IMPLEMENTATION NOTE FOR COWORK

When building each skill's reference file:

1. **Fetch the SEP article** using web_search + web_fetch for the specific URL
2. **Extract the core definition** — use the article's opening paragraphs which typically state the defining thesis
3. **Note the SEP's own cautions** — these are gold for the profiling skill because they prevent naive classification
4. **Extract the key distinctions** — the SEP is excellent at noting where schools overlap or where classification breaks down
5. **Build diagnostic markers** from the language the SEP uses to characterize each position's commitments
6. **Record the SEP URL** as the source field so each school definition is traceable

The profiling skill should present its classifications as **SEP-grounded** — this gives users confidence that the analysis reflects actual academic philosophy, not pop-philosophy simplifications.
