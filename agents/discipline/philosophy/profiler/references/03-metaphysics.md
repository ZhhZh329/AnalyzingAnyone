# Branch 3: Metaphysics — What Is the Nature of Reality?

## Overview
Metaphysics asks fundamental questions about what exists and what its nature is. This branch addresses the ultimate constituents of reality, the nature of mind and matter, abstract objects, and human agency.

---

## 1. IDEALISM

### Core Thesis
Reality is fundamentally mental or mind-dependent. The physical world either does not exist independently of minds, or it is ultimately dependent on mind for its existence.

### Historical Development
- **George Berkeley** (1685–1753): Immaterialism. "Esse est percipi" (to be is to be perceived). Physical objects are collections of sensory ideas that exist only when perceived by minds or God's mind.
- **G.W.F. Hegel** (1770–1831): Absolute Idealism. Reality is the development of Absolute Spirit (Geist) realizing itself through history and human consciousness.
- **Immanuel Kant** (1724–1804): Transcendental Idealism. Space and time are forms of human intuition, not things-in-themselves. We access phenomena (things as they appear), not noumena (things-in-themselves).

### Main Arguments
1. **The Argument from Perception**: All knowledge comes through mental representations. We never access reality "unmediated" by mind. Therefore, mind-independence is epistemically inaccessible.
2. **The Conceptual Dependence Argument**: Properties like color, sound, and taste seem to depend on the perceiving mind. Why not extend this to all properties?
3. **The Ideality of Space and Time (Kant)**: Space and time are conditions of human experience, not features of things-in-themselves.
4. **The Coherence Argument**: Only minds and mental contents can be made fully intelligible. Matter remains mysterious.

### Key Objections
1. **The Problem of Unexplained Regularity**: If reality is mind-dependent, why do unperceived objects behave consistently? Berkeley's answer (God's mind) is theologetically heavy.
2. **The Problem of Other Minds**: How do we know other minds exist? Idealism seems to make this harder, not easier.
3. **The Kantian Veil**: If we never access things-in-themselves, in what sense can we claim they exist at all?
4. **The Explanatory Gap**: Idealism explains the content of experience but struggles to explain why experience has the specific character it does.

### Diagnostic Markers

| Indicator | Strength | Details |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| "Reality is fundamentally mental or mind-dependent" | STRONG | Direct affirmation of idealist thesis |
| "We only ever access our own ideas/representations" | STRONG | Epistemological foundation of idealism |
| "Matter is ultimately dependent on conscious observation" | STRONG | Empirical idealism or Berkeley-style |
| "Space and time are features of how we perceive, not of reality itself" | STRONG | Kantian idealism |
| "The universe must be intelligible to mind because it emerges from mind" | MODERATE | Hegel-influenced absolute idealism |
| "Science describes mental phenomena, not independent physical facts" | MODERATE | Anti-realist about scientific entities |
| "God's mind grounds the existence of all things" | MODERATE-STRONG | Theological idealism (Berkeley) |
| "Without observers, the concept of 'physical properties' loses meaning" | MODERATE | Constructivist idealism |

### Common Confusions
- **Idealism vs. Ideation**: Idealism is not about having grand ideals; it's about the metaphysical status of ideas.
- **Idealism vs. Solipsism**: Idealism does not entail solipsism (only my mind exists). Idealists like Berkeley posit God's mind or other minds.
- **Kant's Idealism vs. Hegel's**: Kant is transcendental idealist (space/time are subjective forms); Hegel is absolute idealist (reality is Spirit).
- **Epistemological Idealism vs. Metaphysical**: Some confuse the claim "we access only ideas" (epistemology) with "only ideas exist" (metaphysics).

### Cross-Branch Correlations
- **Epistemology**: Typically correlates with Internalism about justification (what justifies belief depends on internal mental states).
- **Philosophy of Mind**: Often accepts Dualism or Property Dualism (mind is fundamental).
- **Free Will**: Tends toward Libertarianism (genuine agency seems to require a non-physical mind).
- **Aesthetics**: Supports mind-dependence of aesthetic properties.

## 2. PHYSICALISM / MATERIALISM

### Core Thesis
Everything that exists is ultimately physical, or supervenes on the physical. All facts about the world are grounded in physical facts. There are no non-physical entities or properties.

### Historical Development
- **Thomas Hobbes** (1588–1679): Early materialist. All reality, including thought, is body in motion.
- **Modern Physicalism** (20th century onward): Refined through philosophy of mind (Quine, Lewis, Dennett). Physicalism became dominant in analytic philosophy.
- **David Lewis** (1941–2001): Developed physicalism with careful treatment of properties, mental states, and possible worlds.

### Main Arguments
1. **The Causal Argument**: Physics provides a complete causal explanation of the physical world. Non-physical entities would violate causal closure.
2. **The Success of Science**: Physics, chemistry, biology explain natural phenomena without positing non-physical entities.
3. **The Argument from Parsimony (Occam's Razor)**: Postulating only physical entities is more parsimonious than postulating both physical and non-physical.
4. **The Supervenience Argument**: All mental and abstract properties supervene on physical properties. No change in the physical without change in the mental.
5. **The Argument from Evolution**: Human minds evolved from non-conscious matter; consciousness must supervene on physical organization.

### Key Objections
1. **The Hard Problem of Consciousness (Chalmers)**: Why does physical processing feel like something (qualia)? This explanatory gap suggests consciousness is not purely physical.
2. **The Knowledge Argument (Jackson's Mary)**: Even if Mary knows all physical facts about color, she learns something new when she sees red for the first time, suggesting non-physical knowledge.
3. **The Problem of Intentionality**: How do physical states (neuronal firings) represent or "point to" non-physical contents?
4. **The Problem of Abstract Objects**: Do numbers, sets, and propositions exist? If so, they seem non-physical.
5. **The Argument from Consciousness**: Some claim conscious experience is categorically different from any physical process.

### Diagnostic Markers

| Indicator | Strength | Details |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| "Everything is ultimately matter and energy" | STRONG | Direct physicalist claim |
| "Science will eventually explain consciousness" | STRONG | Physicalist optimism about explanatory gaps |
| "Mental states are just brain states" | STRONG | Identity theory version of physicalism |
| "There's nothing over and above the physical" | STRONG | Anti-fundamentalist stance |
| "Non-physical entities would violate physics" | MODERATE-STRONG | Causal argument for physicalism |
| "Consciousness emerges from physical complexity" | MODERATE | Emergentist physicalism |
| "All abstract objects are ultimately physical or social constructs" | MODERATE | Nominalist physicalism |
| "The burden is on opponents to show what's non-physical" | MODERATE | Physicalist default assumption |

### Common Confusions
- **Physicalism vs. Reductionism**: Physicalism allows for non-reductive physicalism (properties supervene but don't reduce).
- **Physicalism vs. Determinism**: One can be a physicalist indeterminist (physical world has indeterminacy).
- **Physicalism vs. Behaviorism**: Behaviorism says mental states are behavioral dispositions; physicalism allows internal physical states.
- **Physicalism vs. Materialism**: Often used interchangeably, though physicalism is more precise (allows for non-material forces like gravity).

### Cross-Branch Correlations
- **Epistemology**: Often correlates with Empiricism (physical facts are knowable through experience).
- **Philosophy of Mind**: Typically rejects Dualism in favor of Functionalism or Identity Theory.
- **Free Will**: Often combined with Compatibilism (free will compatible with physical determinism).
- **Logic & Metaphysics**: Supports Nominalism about abstract objects.

## 3. DUALISM

### Core Thesis
Reality contains at least two fundamentally different kinds of substance or properties: mind and matter. Mental phenomena cannot be reduced to or explained by physical phenomena alone.

### Historical Development
- **René Descartes** (1596–1650): Substance Dualism. Two substances: res cogitans (thinking substance) and res extensa (extended substance). Mind and body are distinct.
- **David Chalmers** (1966–): Property Dualism. Only physical substances exist, but consciousness is a fundamental, irreducible property alongside physical properties.
- **Karl Popper & John Eccles**: Interactive dualism. Mind and brain interact through quantum phenomena.
- **Thomas Nagel**: Consciousness as a fundamental feature, questioning physicalism's completeness.

### Varieties of Dualism

#### A. Substance Dualism (Cartesian)
- Minds are immaterial substances; bodies are material substances.
- They causally interact (problem of interaction).
- Advantages: Straightforward account of consciousness, free will as non-physical agency.
- Problems: How do immaterial and material substances interact? Where is the mind?

#### B. Property Dualism
- Only one substance (the physical), but consciousness is a fundamental property not reducible to other physical properties.
- Mental properties supervene on physical but are irreducible.
- Advantages: Avoids interaction problem; respects physics as causally closed.
- Problems: Why accept fundamental mental properties if physics is complete?

#### C. Predicate Dualism
- There is only one kind of thing (the physical), but different predicates apply (physical vs. mental).
- Mental discourse is a different vocabulary, not a different ontology.
- Advantages: Minimal ontology; respects physicalism.
- Problems: Doesn't capture the intuition that consciousness is genuinely different.

### Main Arguments for Dualism
1. **The Argument from Consciousness (The Hard Problem)**: Subjective experience—qualia, "what it's like"—seems categorically different from physical facts.
2. **The Knowledge Argument (Jackson)**: Mary knows all physical facts but learns something new upon seeing red, suggesting non-physical knowledge.
3. **The Modal Argument**: We can conceive of conscious beings without bodies; therefore, consciousness is not identical to physical states.
4. **The Argument from Free Will**: Genuine free will seems incompatible with physical determination, requiring non-physical agency.
5. **The Distinctness of Mental Properties**: Mental properties have features (subjectivity, intentionality, normativity) not found in physical properties.

### Key Objections
1. **The Problem of Interaction**: How can non-physical minds causally affect physical bodies? What is the mechanism?
2. **The Argument from Causal Closure**: If physics is causally closed, there's no causal role for non-physical entities.
3. **The Argument from Evolution**: Consciousness evolved from non-conscious matter; evolution operates on physical principles.
4. **The Problem of Other Minds**: We infer other minds from behavior; if minds were non-physical, we'd have no evidence of them.
5. **The Argument from Parsimony**: Postulating non-physical entities multiplies entities beyond necessity.
6. **The Problem of Mental Causation**: If mental events have physical effects, and physical events have sufficient physical causes, what role do mental causes play?

### Diagnostic Markers

| Indicator | Strength | Details |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| "Consciousness cannot be explained purely in physical terms" | STRONG | Core dualist conviction |
| "There's something about subjective experience that physics can't capture" | STRONG | Qualia-based argument |
| "The mind and brain are fundamentally different" | STRONG | Substance or property dualism |
| "We have non-physical free will" | STRONG | Libertarian variant |
| "Consciousness is a fundamental feature of reality, not derived from physics" | STRONG | Property dualism stance |
| "I can imagine being conscious without a body" | MODERATE-STRONG | Modal argument for dualism |
| "The hard problem of consciousness shows physicalism is incomplete" | MODERATE | Chalmers-influenced |
| "Mental properties have features (intention, subjectivity) that physical properties don't" | MODERATE | Distinctness argument |

### Common Confusions
- **Dualism vs. Non-physicalism**: Dualism is one form of non-physicalism; others include Idealism and Panpsychism.
- **Substance Dualism vs. Property Dualism**: Substance dualism posits two substances; property dualism posits one substance with two kinds of properties.
- **Dualism vs. Interactive Causation**: You can believe in mental causation without dualism (physicalist mental causation).
- **Dualism vs. Libertarianism**: While dualists often endorse libertarian free will, one can be a dualist compatibilist.

### Cross-Branch Correlations
- **Epistemology**: Often correlates with Rationalism (privileged introspective access to mental properties).
- **Philosophy of Mind**: Fundamental position; many debates turn on dualism acceptance.
- **Free Will**: Tends toward Libertarianism (genuine agency requires non-physical will).
- **Metaphysics**: Often combined with nominalism about abstract objects.

### SEP Note
The Stanford Encyclopedia notes that Dualism, while less popular among professional philosophers than physicalism, is "intuitively appealing, even among opponents" and ranks as the second most popular metaphysical position after physicalism in surveys of professional philosophers' views.

## 4. MONISM

### Core Thesis
Ultimately, there is only one fundamental substance or kind of thing. All apparent plurality is either illusory or grounded in one fundamental reality.

### Historical Development
- **Baruch Spinoza** (1632–1677): Substance Monism. Only one substance (God or Nature) with infinite attributes, of which we know two: extension (space) and thought (mind).
- **Bertrand Russell** (1872–1970): Logical atomism; later skeptical of traditional monism but developed formal accounts.
- **Parmenides** (5th century BCE): Early monist; only being is; change and plurality are illusory.
- **Arthur Schopenhauer** (1788–1860): Will as the fundamental reality underlying all phenomena.

### Varieties of Monism

#### A. Materialist Monism
- Only matter (or matter-energy) is fundamental.
- All apparent mental phenomena reduce to matter.
- Advantages: Parsimonious; aligns with physics.
- Problems: Same as physicalism; can't explain consciousness.

#### B. Idealist Monism
- Only mind or mental reality is fundamental.
- All matter is fundamentally mental or mind-dependent.
- Advantages: Solves mind-body problem by denying the problem's premise.
- Problems: Hard to explain physical regularity; counterintuitive.

#### C. Neutral Monism
- Fundamental reality is neither mental nor physical but a neutral substance with both mental and physical aspects.
- Mental and physical are different aspects of one underlying reality.
- Advantages: Avoids problems of both physicalism and idealism.
- Problems: Unclear what the neutral substance is; seems ad hoc.

#### D. Spinozist Monism
- One infinite substance with infinite attributes.
- Mental and physical are parallel attributes expressing the same reality from different perspectives.
- Advantages: Elegant resolution of mind-body problem; respects causal closure (parallelism).
- Problems: Why is mental different from physical if they're the same substance?

### Main Arguments for Monism
1. **The Argument from Causal Interaction**: If there are multiple substances, how do they interact? Positing one substance avoids the problem.
2. **The Argument from Parsimony**: Postulating one substance is more parsimonious than postulating many.
3. **The Argument from Unity**: Reality seems to be a unified whole, not a collection of disparate things.
4. **The Argument from Dependence**: All apparent things seem dependent on something more fundamental, suggesting a single ground.

### Key Objections
1. **The Plurality Problem**: Why do we perceive multiple things if only one substance exists? Monism must explain apparent plurality.
2. **The Property Problem**: If only one substance exists, what explains the apparent difference between mental and physical properties?
3. **The Problem of Change**: How can a single, unchanging substance give rise to a changing world?
4. **The Conceptual Problem**: What is the alleged fundamental substance? Neutral monism's "neutral substance" is mysterious.
5. **The Problem of Individuation**: How are individual things individuated if only one substance exists?

### Diagnostic Markers

| Indicator | Strength | Details |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| "Reality is ultimately unified; apparent plurality is illusory or derivative" | STRONG | Core monist conviction |
| "Mind and matter are two aspects of one underlying reality" | STRONG | Neutral monism or Spinozism |
| "All causation flows from one fundamental substance" | MODERATE-STRONG | Monist picture |
| "The distinctions we draw between mental and physical are conceptual, not real" | MODERATE | Spinozist perspective |
| "Reality is fundamentally interconnected; nothing is truly separate" | MODERATE | Holistic monism |
| "Science will eventually show all phenomena derive from one unified principle" | MODERATE | Physicalist monism optimism |

### Common Confusions
- **Monism vs. Holism**: Holism claims the whole determines the parts; monism claims only one substance exists. Related but distinct.
- **Monism vs. Non-dualism**: Non-dualism is sometimes used as a synonym for monism, but can refer to absence of fundamental distinctions.
- **Spinozism vs. Pantheism**: Spinoza's view is sometimes confused with pantheism (God is everything); they're related but not identical.

### Cross-Branch Correlations
- **Epistemology**: Often correlates with Rationalism (knowledge of the one fundamental substance through reason).
- **Philosophy of Mind**: Offers a solution to the mind-body problem by denying the problem's premise.
- **Metaphysics**: Rejects nominalism; abstract objects may be modes of the one substance.
- **Free Will**: Often compatible with determinism (everything follows from the one substance's nature).

## 5. NOMINALISM vs. REALISM ABOUT UNIVERSALS

### Core Question
Do abstract objects (universals, properties, sets, numbers) have mind-independent existence? Or are they mind-dependent, conventional, or non-existent?

### Background: The Problem of Universals
- **What are universals?** Things that can be multiply instantiated: redness is shared by many red objects; the property "justice" is instantiated in many just acts.
- **The Problem**: How do abstract objects exist? Where do they exist? How do we access them? Do they exist at all?

### Nominalism

#### Core Thesis
Abstract objects do not exist. What we call "universals" or "properties" are actually:
- Linguistic conventions (words we use)
- Mental constructs (ideas in minds)
- Physical patterns (similarities among physical objects)
- Or they don't exist at all

#### Varieties
1. **Predicate Nominalism**: "Red" is just a predicate we apply to similar objects; there's no abstract object "redness."
2. **Conceptualism**: "Justice" is a mental concept; it exists only in minds.
3. **Trope Nominalism**: What exist are particular tropes (particular instances of properties), not universals. Red-here-now is a trope.
4. **Mereological Nominalism**: Abstract objects are just collections of concrete particulars.

#### Main Arguments
1. **The Argument from Ontological Parsimony**: Why posit mysterious abstract objects when linguistic conventions or mental constructs suffice?
2. **The Causal Argument**: Abstract objects don't interact causally; our knowledge of them is inexplicable if they're causally inert.
3. **The Verification Argument**: We can't verify the existence of abstract objects; we have no sensory access to them.
4. **The Ockham's Razor Argument**: Concrete objects suffice to account for the world; abstract objects are unnecessary.

#### Key Objections
1. **The Indispensability Argument**: Math, logic, and science are indispensable; math and logic presuppose numbers and propositions; therefore, numbers and propositions must exist.
2. **The Truthmaking Problem**: How are mathematical truths true if there are no mathematical objects?
3. **The Multiple Realizability Problem**: "Justice" can be instantiated in infinitely many ways; there must be a universal unifying these instances.
4. **The Explanatory Problem**: Nominalism struggles to explain why "red" objects share something in common.

#### Diagnostic Markers

| Indicator | Strength | Details |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| "Justice is just a word we use; there's no 'justice' floating around" | STRONG | Predicate nominalism |
| "Abstract objects don't exist independently; they're mental or linguistic constructs" | STRONG | Nominalist position |
| "Numbers are useful fictions, not real abstract entities" | STRONG | Fictionalist nominalism |
| "Only concrete, physical particulars truly exist" | STRONG | Concrete nominalism |
| "Properties are just similarities among objects, not abstract universals" | MODERATE | Nominalist account of properties |
| "We can explain everything using only physical objects and their relations" | MODERATE | Physicalist nominalism |

### Realism About Universals

#### Core Thesis
Abstract objects exist independently of minds or language. Universals are real entities that can be multiply instantiated.

#### Varieties
1. **Platonic Realism**: Abstract objects exist in a non-spatial, non-temporal realm (Plato's realm of forms).
2. **Aristotelian Realism**: Universals exist as abstract entities but are instantiated in concrete particulars.
3. **Immanent Realism**: Universals exist in concrete particulars, not in an abstract realm.
4. **Class Nominalism (Modified Realism)**: What exist are classes (sets) of particulars. "Red" is the class of red objects.

#### Main Arguments
1. **The Indispensability Argument**: Mathematics and science presuppose abstract objects. Our best theories are committed to their existence.
2. **The Truthmaking Argument**: Mathematical truths (e.g., "2 + 2 = 4") require mathematical objects to make them true.
3. **The Explanatory Argument**: Universals explain why we can group objects ("all these things are red"), understand language, and form thoughts.
4. **The Argument from Identity Conditions**: Without universals, how do we explain what makes two objects "the same kind"?

#### Key Objections
1. **The Ontological Problem**: How do abstract objects exist? Where are they? How do we know about them?
2. **The Epistemological Problem**: How do we have access to causally inert abstract objects?
3. **The Causal Exclusion Problem**: Abstract objects don't seem to have causal powers; why posit them?
4. **The Indispensability Argument Can Be Resisted**: We can reinterpret mathematical language nominalistcally or fictionionally.

#### Diagnostic Markers

| Indicator | Strength | Details |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| "Numbers and abstract objects must exist for math to be true" | STRONG | Indispensability argument |
| "There really is something 'in common' that makes things red; that something is the property redness" | STRONG | Realist about universals |
| "Properties are real, abstract features of the world" | STRONG | Universals realism |
| "Our scientific theories presuppose the existence of numbers and sets" | MODERATE-STRONG | Indispensability commitment |
| "Mathematical statements correspond to facts about abstract objects" | MODERATE | Platonist realism |
| "Universals exist immanently in concrete particulars" | MODERATE | Aristotelian realism |

### Cross-Branch Correlations
- **Epistemology**: Nominalists tend toward empiricism; realists toward rationalism.
- **Philosophy of Language**: Affects interpretation of singular terms, predication, and reference.
- **Metaphysics**: Nominalists are typically physicalists; realists can be dualists or idealists.
- **Philosophy of Math**: Nominalists prefer nominalist mathematics; realists embrace Platonism about math.

## 6. FREE WILL POSITIONS

### Core Question
Do humans have free will? Is free will compatible with determinism? Are humans morally responsible for their actions?

### Determinism vs. Indeterminism
- **Determinism**: Every event, including human actions, is necessitated by prior causes according to natural laws.
- **Indeterminism**: Some events are not necessitated; there is genuine contingency and spontaneity in nature.

### Four Main Positions

---

### A. HARD DETERMINISM

#### Core Thesis
Determinism is true, and free will requires the absence of determinism (libertarian condition). Therefore, no one has free will or moral responsibility.

#### Key Figures
- **Galen Strawson**: Influence and the will; we never have ultimate control.
- **Derk Pereboom**: Hard determinism about free will but disputes implications for responsibility.

#### Main Arguments
1. **The Consequence Argument (van Inwagen)**: If determinism is true, our actions are consequences of events before we were born and laws of nature. We have no control over these. Therefore, we have no control over our actions.
2. **The Argument from the Absence of Ultimate Control**: For genuine free will, we'd need to have caused ourselves (infinite regress problem) or acted uncaused (arbitrary). Neither is possible.
3. **The Argument from Neuroscience**: Brain imaging shows neural activity precedes conscious awareness; we don't consciously "decide" our actions.

#### Key Objections
1. **The Revisionists' Counterargument**: We should revise what free will means, not deny it.
2. **The Empirical Objection**: People act as if they have free will; denying this seems to misinterpret the evidence.
3. **The Practical Problem**: Hard determinism seems self-undermining; if we have no free will, why believe hard determinism?

#### Diagnostic Markers

| Indicator | Strength | Details |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| "Determinism is true; no one has free will" | STRONG | Hard determinist conviction |
| "We have no ultimate control over our actions" | STRONG | Control-based argument |
| "If determinism is true, free will is impossible" | STRONG | Incompatibilist premise |
| "Neuroscience shows we don't consciously decide our actions" | MODERATE | Empirical hard determinism |
| "Moral responsibility requires libertarian free will; we don't have it" | MODERATE-STRONG | Responsibility-focused hard determinism |

---

### B. LIBERTARIANISM (Metaphysical)

#### Core Thesis
Humans have genuine free will. Free will is incompatible with determinism. Therefore, either determinism is false, or human actions are not determined (or both). Human agency is a fundamental feature of reality.

#### Key Figures
- **Robert Kane**: Libertarian free will through self-forming actions.
- **Roderick Chisholm**: Agent causation; the agent (not an event) is the cause.
- **Peter Hacker**: Human agency as irreducible.

#### Varieties
1. **Event-Causal Libertarianism**: Free will involves indeterministic causation; our mental states indeterministically cause our actions.
2. **Agent-Causal Libertarianism**: Agents (persons), not events, are causes; agents have causal power independent of events.
3. **Emergentist Libertarianism**: Free will emerges from complex neural systems; emergence provides the needed indeterminism.

#### Main Arguments
1. **The Argument from Phenomenology**: We experience ourselves as free agents making genuine choices; this is powerful evidence.
2. **The Argument from Responsibility**: Moral responsibility requires free will; we are clearly morally responsible; therefore, we have free will.
3. **The Argument from Deliberation**: When we deliberate, we assume our actions are not determined by prior causes; this assumption seems correct.
4. **The Argument from Agency**: Human agency is a fundamental feature of reality, not reducible to impersonal causation.

#### Key Objections
1. **The Randomness Problem**: If actions aren't determined, they seem random. How is randomness freedom?
2. **The Problem of Control**: If indeterminism means actions aren't caused, how do we control them?
3. **The Argument from Neuroscience**: Brain activity explains our choices; there's no need to posit non-physical agency.
4. **The Intelligibility Problem**: Agent causation (in the libertarian sense) is mysterious; we don't understand how agents cause events.

#### Diagnostic Markers

| Indicator | Strength | Details |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| "We have genuine free will to make different choices" | STRONG | Core libertarian claim |
| "Free will is incompatible with determinism" | STRONG | Incompatibilist premise |
| "We are genuinely morally responsible for our actions" | STRONG | Responsibility-based libertarianism |
| "Determinism would mean our choices are not truly ours" | STRONG | Control-based libertarianism |
| "Our agency is a fundamental feature of reality, not reducible to physics" | MODERATE-STRONG | Agent-causal libertarianism |
| "We experience ourselves as free; that experience is reliable" | MODERATE | Phenomenological libertarianism |

---

### C. COMPATIBILISM

#### Core Thesis
Free will is compatible with determinism. Free will does not require the absence of determinism. What matters for free will is that actions flow from our desires, values, and reasoning—not that they are undetermined.

#### Key Figures
- **Daniel Dennett**: Free will as evolved capacity for complex deliberation.
- **David Lewis**: Free will requires that agents act on their own desires and beliefs, not that desires are undetermined.
- **Harry Frankfurt**: Free will requires second-order volitions (desires about desires).
- **Michael McKenna**: Modern compatibilism; most popular position among professional philosophers.

#### Main Arguments
1. **The Conditional Analysis of Free Will**: "You act freely if you act on your own desires, values, and reasoning, absent external coercion or internal compulsion." Determinism doesn't undermine this.
2. **The Argument from Ordinary Practice**: We already distinguish free actions (you decide to read) from unfree actions (you're forced at gunpoint). This distinction survives determinism.
3. **The Argument from Moral Responsibility**: Moral responsibility requires only that actions reflect the agent's character and reasoning; determinism is compatible with this.
4. **The Argument from Intelligibility**: Compatibilism makes free will and responsibility intelligible; libertarianism does not.
5. **The Argument from Science**: Neuroscience and physics support determinism; a plausible theory of free will should accommodate this.

#### Key Objections
1. **The Argument from Intuition**: Compatibilism conflicts with our intuitive sense that free will requires genuine alternative possibilities.
2. **The Consequence Argument (van Inwagen)**: Even if determinism obtains and you act on your desires, you have no more control than a puppet.
3. **The Problem of Alternative Possibilities**: If determinism is true, you could never have done otherwise; how is that freedom?
4. **The Disappearing Agent Problem**: Compatibilism makes free will depend on causal history; an agent's "will" becomes just a determined state.

#### Diagnostic Markers

| Indicator | Strength | Details |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| "Free will just means acting on your own desires without external coercion" | STRONG | Conditional analysis of free will |
| "I think free will and science can both be true" | STRONG | Compatibilist reconciliation |
| "Moral responsibility doesn't require alternative possibilities; it requires that actions reflect your character" | STRONG | Compatibilist responsibility |
| "Determinism doesn't undermine what we ordinarily mean by 'free choice'" | STRONG | Ordinary practice compatibilism |
| "The important distinction is between actions we control and actions we don't, not between determined and undetermined" | MODERATE-STRONG | Control-based compatibilism |
| "Free will is about acting on your own reasoning, regardless of whether those reasons are determined" | MODERATE | Reasoning-based compatibilism |

#### Note
Compatibilism is the most popular position among professional philosophers (approximately 59–62% in surveys).

### D. HARD INCOMPATIBILISM

#### Core Thesis
Neither free will nor determinism is true. Humans lack moral responsibility because:
1. Free will (in the sense required for responsibility) is incompatible with both determinism and indeterminism.
2. Determinism is likely true.
3. Therefore, we lack free will and moral responsibility.

#### Key Figures
- **Derk Pereboom**: Hard incompatibilism about free will; skepticism about responsibility.
- **Gregg Caruso**: Hard incompatibilism; implications for criminal justice and punishment.

#### Main Arguments
1. **The Consequence Argument (Determinism)**: If determinism is true, we lack free will and responsibility (van Inwagen).
2. **The Randomness Argument (Indeterminism)**: If indeterminism is true, our actions might be random; that doesn't give us responsibility.
3. **The Impossibility Argument**: There's no metaphysical scenario (determined or undetermined) in which we'd have the free will needed for responsibility.
4. **The Scientific Argument**: Neuroscience suggests determinism; neuroscience also suggests consciousness is not where responsibility-grounding decisions occur.

#### Key Objections
1. **The Revisionist Objection**: Why not revise responsibility rather than deny free will?
2. **The Practical Objection**: Denying responsibility seems to undermine the criminal justice system and social order.
3. **The Motivation Problem**: If no one is responsible, why do anything?
4. **The Self-Undermining Problem**: Believing hard incompatibilism seems itself to require that you could have believed otherwise (which hard incompatibilism denies).

#### Diagnostic Markers

| Indicator | Strength | Details |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| "Neither determinism nor indeterminism is compatible with genuine free will" | STRONG | Core hard incompatibilist claim |
| "We lack moral responsibility because free will is impossible" | STRONG | Incompatibilist responsibility denial |
| "The problem of free will can't be solved by either side; free will is genuinely impossible" | MODERATE-STRONG | Impossibility argument |
| "Neuroscience suggests we're not where decisions are made; we're passive observers" | MODERATE | Scientific hard incompatibilism |

## PROBING QUESTIONS (10+)

### On Metaphysical Fundamentals

1. **"What do you think is the most fundamental level of reality? Is it mind, matter, or something else?"**
   - Idealist: Mental/consciousness
   - Physicalist: Physical/matter-energy
   - Dualist: Mind and matter equally
   - Monist: One underlying substance
   - Tests fundamental ontology

2. **"Do you think numbers, sets, and abstract properties actually exist, or are they just mental/linguistic tools?"**
   - Realist: They genuinely exist
   - Nominalist: They're constructs or conventions
   - Tests position on universals

3. **"Could the universe exist without any minds to perceive it, or does reality depend on observation?"**
   - Idealist: Depends on minds (or God's mind)
   - Physicalist: Exists independently
   - Tests mind-dependence of reality

4. **"Are space and time fundamental features of reality itself, or are they features of how we perceive reality?"**
   - Kantian idealist: Features of perception
   - Physicalist: Fundamental features of reality
   - Tests transcendental idealism

### On Consciousness and Mind-Body

5. **"Do you think consciousness can be fully explained by brain activity, or is there something non-physical about subjective experience?"**
   - Physicalist: Fully explainable by brain
   - Dualist: Something non-physical
   - Tests consciousness position

6. **"Imagine Mary, who knows all the physical facts about color but has never seen red. When she sees red for the first time, does she learn something new?"**
   - Yes → Property dualism or nominalism about physical facts
   - No → Physicalism
   - Tests Jackson's knowledge argument

7. **"Is consciousness a fundamental feature of reality, or does it emerge from physical processes?"**
   - Fundamental → Dualism or panpsychism
   - Emergent → Physicalism
   - Tests fundamentality claims

### On Free Will and Responsibility

8. **"Do you believe you could have done otherwise in any meaningful sense, or are your actions always determined by prior causes?"**
   - Could have → Libertarianism or incompatibilist indeterminism
   - Always determined → Hard determinism or compatibilism (if combined with "that's okay")
   - Tests free will position

9. **"Is free will compatible with living in a fully determined universe, or does genuine free will require that the universe is not fully determined?"**
   - Compatible → Compatibilism
   - Incompatible → Libertarianism or hard determinism
   - Tests compatibilist stance

10. **"If a neuroscientist could predict your decision before you consciously make it, would that show you don't have free will?"**
   - Yes → Libertarian or hard determinist
   - No → Compatibilist
   - Tests folk concepts of free will

11. **"Are you morally responsible for your actions if your behavior is ultimately the result of genetics and environment over which you had no control?"**
   - No → Hard determinist or hard incompatibilist
   - Yes → Compatibilist
   - Maybe (depends on definition) → Libertarian trying to preserve responsibility
   - Tests responsibility commitments

### On Universals and Abstracta

12. **"When we say two things are 'both red,' what does 'redness' refer to? Is it a real property or just a word we use?"**
   - Real property → Realism about universals
   - Just a word → Nominalism
   - Tests universals position

13. **"Do you think mathematical truths like '2 + 2 = 4' correspond to facts about abstract objects (numbers), or are they just useful fictions?"**
   - Correspond to facts → Realism about abstract objects
   - Useful fictions → Nominalism or fictionalism
   - Tests stance on mathematical ontology

## CROSS-BRANCH CORRELATIONS

### Common Bundles

#### Bundle A: Physicalist Scientism
- **Positions**: Physicalism, Compatibilism, Nominalism
- **Epistemology**: Empiricism; prioritizes scientific knowledge
- **Correlations**: "Science explains everything; consciousness will yield to neuroscience; abstract objects are linguistic conventions."
- **Frequency**: Very common among analytic philosophers and scientists

#### Bundle B: Dualist Libertarianism
- **Positions**: Dualism (property or substance), Libertarianism, Realism about universals
- **Epistemology**: Rationalism; trusts introspection and phenomenology
- **Correlations**: "Consciousness is non-physical; we have genuine free will; abstract moral truths exist."
- **Frequency**: Common among theists, phenomenologists, humanists

#### Bundle C: Idealist Rationalism
- **Positions**: Idealism, Rationalism, Realism about universals
- **Epistemology**: Reason as primary; knowledge through intellect
- **Correlations**: "Reality is fundamentally mental/rational; genuine knowledge is a priori; abstract objects are real."
- **Frequency**: Historically important (Plato, Hegel); less common today

#### Bundle D: Hard Determinist Materialism
- **Positions**: Physicalism, Hard Determinism, Nominalism
- **Epistemology**: Scientism; deterministic world-view
- **Correlations**: "Physics is complete; free will is an illusion; nothing is truly responsible."
- **Frequency**: Uncommon but coherent; some neuroscientists hold this

#### Bundle E: Mysterian Non-Reductionism
- **Positions**: Dualism or neutral monism, hard incompatibilism, realism about consciousness
- **Epistemology**: Epistemic humility; consciousness and responsibility are mysterious
- **Correlations**: "Consciousness is fundamentally different and unexplained; responsibility is problematic; we may be wrong about mind-body."
- **Frequency**: Growing in importance; Nagel, Chalmers influence

### Typical Incompatibilities

| Position A | Position B | Why Rarely Together |
|-----------|-----------|-------------------|
| Physicalism | Libertarianism | Libertarians need non-physical free will; physicalists deny non-physical |
| Idealism | Nominalism | Idealists typically reify mind/consciousness; nominalists deny abstract objects |
| Hard Determinism | Libertarianism | Opposite positions on free will |
| Substance Monism | Dualism | Monism posits one substance; dualism posits two |
| Kantian Idealism | Realism about things-in-themselves | Kant explicitly rejects access to things-in-themselves |

## PLAIN-LANGUAGE DIAGNOSTIC MARKERS

### Marker Profiles

#### Profile: Physicalist
- **Typical Phrases**:
  - "Everything is ultimately matter and energy"
  - "Once science fully develops, consciousness will be explained"
  - "Non-physical entities seem to violate physics"
  - "All causation is physical causation"
  - "The burden is on non-physicalists to show what's non-physical"

- **Attitude Toward Consciousness**: Optimistic that it's solvable by neuroscience; dismissive of hard problem
- **Attitude Toward Free Will**: Usually compatibilist; sees libertarianism as incoherent
- **Signs of Confusion**: May conflate physicalism with reductionism; may not acknowledge supervenience vs. reduction

#### Profile: Property Dualist (Modern)
- **Typical Phrases**:
  - "There's something about subjective experience that physics can't capture"
  - "The hard problem of consciousness is unsolved"
  - "Consciousness is a fundamental feature of reality"
  - "My experience of red isn't captured by neuroscience"
  - "Not everything is reducible to physics, even if supervenes on it"

- **Attitude Toward Consciousness**: Takes hard problem seriously; respects physics but demands more
- **Attitude Toward Free Will**: Often libertarian; sometimes compatibilist
- **Signs of Sophistication**: Distinguishes property from substance dualism; respects causal closure

#### Profile: Libertarian Incompatibilist
- **Typical Phrases**:
  - "Free will is real and incompatible with determinism"
  - "We could genuinely have done otherwise"
  - "Moral responsibility requires libertarian free will"
  - "Determinism would mean our choices aren't ours"
  - "The randomness problem can be solved by agent causation"

- **Attitude Toward Determinism**: Skeptical; emphasizes quantum indeterminacy, emergence, or free agency
- **Attitude Toward Responsibility**: Central conviction; responsibility is real and grounded in free will
- **Common Theistic Connection**: Often (but not always) connected to libertarian theism

#### Profile: Compatibilist
- **Typical Phrases**:
  - "Free will just means acting on your own desires without coercion"
  - "The important thing is that the action reflects your character"
  - "Free will and science can both be true"
  - "Determinism doesn't threaten moral responsibility"
  - "The distinction between free and unfree actions survives determinism"

- **Attitude Toward Science**: Accepts or welcomes scientific determinism; sees no conflict with responsibility
- **Attitude Toward Intuitions**: Revises folk intuitions about free will; defends revised conception
- **Signs of Sophistication**: Understands objections (consequence argument, alternative possibilities); responds systematically

#### Profile: Hard Determinist
- **Typical Phrases**:
  - "Free will and responsibility are illusions"
  - "Determinism is true; free will requires indeterminism; therefore, no free will"
  - "Neuroscience shows we don't decide our actions"
  - "The illusion of free will is evolutionarily adaptive"
  - "We should revise our practices of blame and punishment"

- **Attitude Toward Responsibility**: Skeptical; argues traditional responsibility is indefensible
- **Attitude Toward Determinism**: Typically assumes determinism
- **Practical Implications**: Often argues for reform of criminal justice and moral practices

#### Profile: Nominalist About Universals
- **Typical Phrases**:
  - "Justice is just a word we use; there's no abstract property 'justice'"
  - "Numbers are useful fictions"
  - "Only concrete, particular objects truly exist"
  - "Abstract objects seem unreal; I don't see evidence for them"
  - "We can explain everything using physical objects and their relations"

- **Attitude Toward Mathematics**: Either fictionalist (math is useful but not true) or reinterprets it
- **Attitude Toward Logic**: Develops nominalist logic; avoids quantification over abstract objects
- **Typical Conjunction**: With physicalism and empiricism

#### Profile: Realist About Universals/Platonist
- **Typical Phrases**:
  - "Numbers are real; without them, mathematics wouldn't be true"
  - "Properties like redness are real features of the world"
  - "Mathematical truths correspond to facts about abstract objects"
  - "There must be something in common that makes things the same kind"
  - "Abstract objects couldn't depend on minds; they must be objective"

- **Attitude Toward Mathematics**: Mathematics directly describes reality; mathematical truths are objective facts
- **Attitude Toward Properties**: Reifies shared features; sees universals as indispensable
- **Philosophical Tradition**: Connected to rationalism and Platonism

#### Profile: Kantian Transcendental Idealist
- **Typical Phrases**:
  - "Space and time are forms of human perception, not features of things-in-themselves"
  - "We can't access reality as it is in itself; only as it appears to us"
  - "A priori knowledge is possible because it concerns our forms of intuition"
  - "The mind structures experience according to categories like causality"
  - "Things-in-themselves are unknowable, but phenomena are knowable"

- **Attitude Toward Metaphysics**: Cautious; skeptical of metaphysical claims about the noumenal
- **Attitude Toward Science**: Affirms scientific knowledge as knowledge of phenomena
- **Sophistication Marker**: Distinguishes Kant from traditional idealism; avoids solipsism

## FINAL NOTES

### Integration with Other Branches
- **Epistemology**: Metaphysical positions correlate strongly with epistemological stances (physicalism with empiricism, idealism with rationalism, etc.)
- **Philosophy of Mind**: Metaphysics determines available options in philosophy of mind (dualism, functionalism, identity theory)
- **Ethics**: Free will positions ground responsibility; ontological commitments ground meta-ethics
- **Aesthetics**: Realism vs. nominalism affects whether beauty is objective or subjective

### Common Trajectory
Many philosophers:
1. Start with intuitions about consciousness (dualist)
2. Learn about physicalism and adopt it
3. Encounter the hard problem and adopt property dualism or mysterianism
4. Become compatibilists about free will while maintaining other commitments
5. Grow skeptical of abstract objects while remaining physicalists

### Persistent Tensions
- **Consciousness and Physics**: The hard problem remains unresolved; most metaphysical tensions in contemporary philosophy flow from it
- **Free Will and Determinism**: No position fully satisfies all intuitions and theoretical commitments
- **Abstracta and Ontology**: Nominalism about abstracta conflicts with indispensability arguments in math and logic
- **Mind and Matter**: The explanatory gap between mental and physical persists across positions
