## Core Question
What are the fundamental principles of valid reasoning, and are they universal or revisable?

## SEP-Flagged Caution
The Stanford Encyclopedia emphasizes that "classical logic" is not a neutral or uniquely privileged system—it is one choice among many legitimate logical frameworks. The existence of non-classical logics is not a sign of confusion or error, but reflects deep philosophical disagreements about what valid reasoning should countenance. The choice of logic is not merely technical: it embodies substantive commitments about the nature of truth, contradiction, evidence, and necessity. Classifying someone's logical commitments requires careful attention to their actual reasoning patterns, not assumptions about what "everyone" uses. Moreover, the boundaries between these schools are often fuzzy; a thinker may hold hybrid or position-specific views.

## Schools of Thought

### 9.1 Classical Logic

**SEP Source**: plato.stanford.edu/entries/logic-classical/

**Core Thesis**
Classical logic is the system of reasoning that enforces the Law of Non-Contradiction (no statement can be both true and false), the Law of Excluded Middle (every statement is either true or false), and bivalence (all propositions have exactly one of two truth values). It treats logical consequence as preservation of truth across all possible interpretations and anchors valid inference in truth-functional operations.

**Key Thinkers**
Aristotle (foundational), Frege, Russell, Whitehead, Gödel, Tarski, Quine

**Main Arguments**
1. **Universality & Intuitiveness**: Classical logic mirrors fundamental intuitions about reasoning (things either are or are not; you can't both accept and reject a claim). Its rules align with how practical inference seems to work.
2. **Mathematical Efficacy**: Classical logic is the foundation of standard mathematics, set theory, and scientific reasoning. Its success in formalizing these domains suggests alignment with objective structure.
3. **Metalogical Strength**: Classical logic has complete proof systems (Completeness Theorem), decidable fragments, and clear semantics. It provides maximum inferential power without collapse into inconsistency.

**Key Objections**
1. **Non-Classical Phenomena**: Some natural language phenomena (vagueness, paradoxes, quantum mechanics, belief reports) seem to resist classical treatment and suggest classical logic is over-idealized.
2. **Metaphysically Loaded**: Classical logic presupposes a particular metaphysical picture (bivalence, excluded middle) that may not hold in all domains; it thus disguises substantive assumptions as logical laws.
3. **Ignores Constructive Insight**: Insisting that every proposition is determinately true or false ignores epistemological gaps and constructive processes where truth is established through proof rather than pre-existing determination.

**Diagnostic Markers**
- "Either the statement is true or it's false—there's no third option." → strength: strong
- "If a contradiction follows, the whole theory is shot; we can't have both P and not-P." → strength: strong
- "The laws of logic are universal and apply to all domains of reasoning." → strength: moderate
- "Logic is just about tracking which statements are true under all interpretations." → strength: moderate
- "You can't consistently deny the Law of Excluded Middle without undermining rational discourse itself." → strength: strong

**Common Confusions**
Often confused with *naïve realism* or *metaphysical essentialism*—the view that logic simply reflects a mind-independent reality. In fact, classical logic makes substantive formal choices; one can be a realist but use non-classical logic. Also confused with the claim that classical logic is "the only real logic"; classicalists typically claim it's the right choice for most reasoning, not that alternatives are meaningless.

**Cross-Branch Correlations**
- **Epistemology**: Pairs naturally with foundationalism or evidentialism; strict criteria for justified belief mirror strict truth conditions.
- **Metaphysics**: Supports actualism and rejects modal pluralism; the real world either has a property or lacks it.
- **Philosophy of Mind**: Favors sharp boundaries in mental content; thoughts are either about X or not.
- **Metaphysics of Language**: Pairs with semantic maximalism—every meaningful sentence has a determinate truth value.

**Internal Variants**
- **Logical Positivism**: Classical logic + empiricist semantics; logical truths are analytic; synthetic knowledge is empirical.
- **Platonism in Logic**: Classical logic mirrors abstract logical structures; logical truths are objective discoveries.
- **Naturalized Classicism**: Classical logic is the best tool for scientific inference, even if not metaphysically privileged.

### 9.2 Intuitionistic Logic

**SEP Source**: plato.stanford.edu/entries/logic-intuitionistic/

**Core Thesis**
Intuitionistic logic rejects the Law of Excluded Middle and weakens classical inference rules in service of a constructivist epistemology. A proposition is true (in the intuitionistic sense) just when it has a constructive proof; truth is not determined in advance of all possible evidence. Logical consequence is defined by proof-realizability, not truth-preservation under all assignments. Negation is particularly weakened: ¬P means "assuming P leads to absurdity," not "P is false."

**Key Thinkers**
Brouwer, Heyting, Kolmogorov, Martin-Löf, Dummett, Prawitz

**Main Arguments**
1. **Constructive Semantics**: Truth just is the existence of a constructive proof or verification procedure. This aligns with mathematics as a human activity and avoids assuming truth exists independently of epistemic access.
2. **Rejection of Excluded Middle**: For undecidable propositions (e.g., transcendental truths, infinitary claims), asserting "P or not-P" goes beyond what we can actually prove and illegitimately inflates our knowledge.
3. **Proof as Fundamental**: Logical consequence should be understood as proof-preservation, not truth-preservation. This makes logic directly applicable to human reasoning and mathematical practice.

**Key Objections**
1. **Counterintuitiveness**: Rejecting excluded middle feels deeply wrong. Most people's actual reasoning treats "P or not-P" as inviolable, especially in ordinary domains where decidability is unproblematic.
2. **Mathematical Revisionism**: Intuitionistic mathematics abandons classical theorems (e.g., every bounded sequence has a convergent subsequence) and proves weaker results. This is costly and limits the scope of mathematics.
3. **Epistemological Overreach**: Why should logic be wedded to constructivism? One might accept classical logic but adopt a constructivist epistemology for knowledge (believing less). Logic and epistemology are separable concerns.

**Diagnostic Markers**
- "We can only assert what we've actually proved; to claim something is true without proof is to exceed our justification." → strength: strong
- "The statement 'either the continuum hypothesis is true or false' assumes a proof we don't have." → strength: strong
- "Negation isn't the opposite of truth; it's the impossibility of proof." → strength: moderate
- "Mathematics is a human constructive activity, not discovery of pre-existing abstract objects." → strength: moderate
- "Rejecting the Law of Excluded Middle protects us from asserting non-constructive existence claims." → strength: strong

**Common Confusions**
Often confused with skepticism or anti-realism, but intuitionionism is actually realist about constructive proofs—they're objective. Also confused with merely being epistemically cautious; one can limit belief without revising logic itself. Sometimes conflated with constructive mathematics specifically, though intuitionistic logic applies to reasoning more broadly.

**Cross-Branch Correlations**
- **Epistemology**: Endorses verificationism or constructivist justification. Belief requires accessible proof or verification conditions.
- **Metaphysics of Mathematics**: Commits to constructivism; numbers and proofs are human constructions, not abstract Platonic objects.
- **Philosophy of Mind**: Often paired with phenomenological or internalist approaches (mental content is transparent to consciousness).
- **Language & Meaning**: Pairs with anti-realist semantics; meaning is use or verification conditions, not independent truth conditions.

**Internal Variants**
- **Strict Intuitionism (Brouwer)**: Rejects even recursive/computable procedures; only mental constructions count.
- **Recursive Intuitionism (Kleene)**: Accepts computable functions as legitimate constructions.
- **Type-Theoretic Constructivism (Martin-Löf)**: Intuitionistic logic embedded in type theory; proofs are computational.

### 9.3 Fuzzy Logic

**SEP Source**: plato.stanford.edu/entries/logic-fuzzy/

**Core Thesis**
Fuzzy logic abandons bivalence and the sharp boundary between truth and falsehood. Truth values form a continuum (typically [0,1]), and propositions can be partially true or false. Logical operations generalize classical operations: conjunction becomes min(), disjunction becomes max(), negation becomes (1 - x). Consequence relations are defined by degree of truth preservation. This framework is motivated by real-world vagueness and gradual properties.

**Key Thinkers**
Lotfi Zadeh, Jan Łukasiewicz, Hans Kamp, Diana Raffman, Stephen Soames

**Main Arguments**
1. **Natural Vagueness**: Many predicates in natural language (tall, red, bald) have no sharp boundaries. Classical bivalence forces false precision, while fuzzy logic accommodates genuine indeterminacy.
2. **Many-Valued Truth**: Real phenomena come in degrees—confidence, temperature, color gradations. Forcing these into true/false creates artificial boundaries and loses information.
3. **Practical Efficacy**: Fuzzy logic has enormous engineering and control applications (fuzzy thermostats, decision systems). Its success suggests it captures genuine structure in realistic domains.

**Key Objections**
1. **Where Do Membership Degrees Come From?** Specifying that "tall" has truth value 0.7 seems arbitrary. Classical logic's approach (either a precise definition applies or it doesn't) avoids this arbitrariness.
2. **Sorites Paradox Recurs**: Fuzzy logic doesn't really solve sorites-like problems. If we're indifferent between 0.7 and 0.70001, we still have a continuum with no principled stopping point.
3. **Metalogical Awkwardness**: Fuzzy logic itself can be formalized in classical meta-logic, suggesting the appeal to fuzziness is limited to specific domains, not foundational reasoning.

**Diagnostic Markers**
- "Truth comes in degrees; very few propositions are completely true or completely false." → strength: strong
- "The boundary between tall and not-tall is genuinely vague, not merely unknown." → strength: strong
- "Classical logic oversimplifies by forcing everything into true/false categories." → strength: moderate
- "We should model borderline cases as having intermediate truth values, like 0.5." → strength: moderate
- "Vagueness is a feature of reality or language, not just epistemic ignorance." → strength: strong

**Common Confusions**
Confused with probability (probability measures uncertainty about determinate facts; fuzziness measures genuine indeterminacy). Also confused with many-valued logic more generally—fuzzy logic uses a continuum, while other many-valued systems use finite sets (e.g., true/false/unknown). Not the same as relativism (different people rating tallness differently), though fuzziness can be indexed to contexts.

**Cross-Branch Correlations**
- **Metaphysics**: Commits to genuine indeterminacy in reality; not just epistemic gaps, but ontological fuzziness.
- **Epistemology**: Often paired with defeasibility and graded justification (not all justified beliefs are equally certain).
- **Philosophy of Language**: Rejects semantic precision; meaning is inherently vague or gradient.
- **Aesthetics**: Explains why aesthetic properties (beautiful, sublime) resist sharp definition.

**Internal Variants**
- **Semantic Fuzziness**: Indeterminacy resides in meaning; the world is determinate but our concepts are fuzzy.
- **Ontological Fuzziness**: Reality itself is fuzzy; there's no determinate fact of the matter about borders.
- **Degree-Theoretic Fuzzy Logic**: Uses truth intervals or regions, not just point values.

### 9.4 Paraconsistent Logic

**SEP Source**: plato.stanford.edu/entries/logic-paraconsistent/

**Core Thesis**
Paraconsistent logic rejects the Principle of Explosion (ex falso quodlibet): from a contradiction, not everything follows. A contradiction—a true P and true ¬P—can exist in some specific domain or theory without rendering the entire system trivial. This allows for inconsistent but non-trivial theories, useful for modeling incomplete information, semantic paradoxes, or genuine but localized inconsistencies in naive set theory.

**Key Thinkers**
Graham Priest, Stanisław Jaśkowski, Newton da Costa, Jean-Yves Béziau, Jc Beall

**Main Arguments**
1. **Tolerating Inconsistency**: Real-world knowledge is inconsistent—naive set theory, semantic self-reference, belief systems with conflicting commitments. Classical logic's rejection of all such systems throws away useful work.
2. **Semantic & Paradoxical Phenomena**: The Liar Paradox and other paradoxes naturally generate contradictions if we accept intuitive principles. Rather than reject those principles, paraconsistent logic tames the contradiction.
3. **Pragmatic & Heuristic Value**: Inconsistent theories can be informationally useful and predictively successful before contradiction is resolved. Paraconsistent semantics respects this; classical logic loses it.

**Key Objections**
1. **Violates Intuition**: The Principle of Explosion feels foundational—if you accept both P and ¬P, you're saying something is wrong with your reasoning. Explosion is how classical logic polices contradiction.
2. **Abandons Standards of Rational Consistency**: A theory that admits contradictions seems, ipso facto, irrational. Paraconsistency appears to lower standards of logical coherence.
3. **Metalogical Costs**: Non-paraconsistent formalizations of paraconsistent logic itself use classical logic (in the meta-theory), seemingly undermining the motivation. Why not use classical logic throughout?

**Diagnostic Markers**
- "A contradiction can be true in a limited domain without making the entire system collapse." → strength: strong
- "Semantic paradoxes like the Liar show that naive principles generate contradictions—but that doesn't mean we should throw out those principles." → strength: strong
- "The rule 'from any contradiction, anything follows' is too strong; it conflates contradiction with inconsistency." → strength: moderate
- "Inconsistent theories can be informationally valuable and shouldn't be dismissed outright." → strength: moderate
- "Maybe some contradictions are true—this isn't incoherence, it's a richer metaphysics." → strength: strong

**Common Confusions**
Confused with simple inconsistency (badly reasoned theories) or with relativism (everything is true for someone). In fact, paraconsistent logic is rigorous and can enforce standards of reasoning within a paraconsistent framework. Also confused with dialetheism (see below), though dialetheism is often a motivation for paraconsistency. They're distinct: paraconsistency is a logical property; dialetheism is a metaphysical claim.

**Cross-Branch Correlations**
- **Metaphysics**: Can support object-theoretic pluralism or indeterminate identity; things can be and not be in some respects.
- **Philosophy of Language**: Accommodates semantic self-reference without classical explosion.
- **Philosophy of Mathematics**: Allows naive set theory to be true in an inconsistent way; accommodates intuitive set-theoretic principles despite Russell's paradox.
- **Metaphysics of Truth**: Pairs with approaches that see truth as non-bivalent or multi-valued locally.

**Internal Variants**
- **Relevant Logic**: Adds the requirement that antecedent and consequent are relevantly connected (from P, infer Q only if Q depends on P).
- **Adaptive Logic**: Allows classical logic where possible, reverts to paraconsistent reasoning where contradiction arises.
- **Object-Theoretic Paraconsistency**: Uses non-standard semantics (e.g., Routley stars) to allow truth-value gaps or truth-value gluts.

### 9.5 Modal Logic

**SEP Source**: plato.stanford.edu/entries/logic-modal/

**Core Thesis**
Modal logic extends classical logic with operators for necessity (□P: "necessarily P") and possibility (◇P: "possibly P"). These operators don't reduce to classical operators; they express modality beyond truth-value. A proposition can be true and yet not necessarily true. Different systems (K, T, S4, S5) make different assumptions about the accessibility relation between possible worlds, yielding different theorems about what must be, what can be, and how these modalities interact.

**Key Thinkers**
Saul Kripke, David Lewis, Ruth Barcan Marcus, Alvin Plantinga, Timothy Williamson

**Main Arguments**
1. **Irreducibility of Modality**: Facts about what's necessary or possible cannot be expressed in purely classical truth-functional terms. Modality is a fundamental feature of reality and reasoning that classical logic omits.
2. **Possible World Semantics**: Modal claims are naturally understood via possible worlds (interpretations where different things are true). This semantics is systematic, precise, and connects to metaphysics and epistemology cleanly.
3. **Philosophical Indispensability**: Discussions of causation, counterfactuals, essence, natural law, and knowledge all invoke necessity and possibility. Modal logic is required to formalize these core philosophical concepts.

**Key Objections**
1. **Metaphysical Excess**: Possible worlds semantics seems to populate reality with infinitely many abstract entities. Do all logically possible worlds exist? This is extravagant ontologically.
2. **Accessibility Underdetermined**: Which possible world logic (K, T, S4, S5) is correct? The choice seems underdetermined by linguistic meaning or observable facts, turning logic into metaphysical speculation.
3. **De Re/De Dicto Ambiguity**: Modal logic does not cleanly resolve whether modal claims are about how things actually are (de re) or about how our language describes them (de dicto). This leaves foundational ambiguities unresolved.

**Diagnostic Markers**
- "Something can be true without being necessarily true; modal facts are irreducible to truth-values." → strength: strong
- "To understand causation or counterfactuals, we need to reason about what could or must be the case." → strength: strong
- "Different possible worlds make true different propositions; logic must account for this." → strength: moderate
- "Necessarily, 2+2=4, but this isn't just a fact about our world—it holds in all possible worlds." → strength: moderate
- "Natural laws express what must be, not just what is; modal operators are essential." → strength: strong

**Common Confusions**
Confused with contingency (the fact that some truths might have been false), though modality underpins contingency. Also confused with epistemic operators like "I believe" or "it's known that"—modal logic is typically metaphysical, not epistemic (though epistemic logics exist). Sometimes conflated with predicate logic (modal logic adds operators to classical logic; predicate logic adds quantifiers).

**Cross-Branch Correlations**
- **Metaphysics**: Commits to possible worlds; supports essentialism or modal realism depending on the variant.
- **Epistemology**: Pairs with externalism (knowledge involves relations to non-actual possibilities).
- **Philosophy of Mind**: Necessary truths about mental properties vs. contingent facts about which minds exist.
- **Metaphysics of Laws**: Explains natural laws as necessary or nomologically necessary, not just universal regularities.

**Internal Variants**
- **Alethic Modality**: Concerned with metaphysical necessity and possibility (what must be, what could be).
- **Epistemic Modality**: Covers knowledge and justification (what can be inferred, what must be true given evidence).
- **Deontic Logic**: Studies obligation and permission (what ought to be done).
- **Temporal Logic**: Formalizes facts about the past, present, and future.

### 9.6 Dialetheism

**SEP Source**: plato.stanford.edu/entries/dialetheism/

**Core Thesis**
Dialetheism is the metaphysical thesis that some propositions are both true and false—that true contradictions exist. This is typically motivated by paradoxes (the Liar Paradox, Russell's Paradox, the Curry Paradox) which seem to generate contradictory truths if one takes intuitive semantic principles seriously. Dialetheists adopt paraconsistent logic to avoid explosion and typically hold that contradictions are localized to paradoxical or self-referential domains, not pervasive.

**Key Thinkers**
Graham Priest (primary advocate), Jc Beall, Richard Routley, Jean-Yves Béziau

**Main Arguments**
1. **Semantic Paradoxes Demand It**: The Liar Sentence ("This sentence is false") is true iff it's false. The most natural reading is that it is both true and false. Classical logic's rejection of this seems evasive; accepting it is honest about the data.
2. **Principled Semantics**: If we want a semantic theory that respects intuitive principles (truth is defined by satisfaction of truth conditions; the Liar sentence's truth condition is its own falsity), we are forced to accept true contradictions.
3. **Metaphysical Consistency**: The world is arguably determinate and complete—gaps don't help. A proposition and its negation both being true is less metaphysically problematic than introducing truth-value gaps.

**Key Objections**
1. **Threatens Rational Discourse**: If contradictions can be true, how do we distinguish good reasoning from bad? The dialetheist must say "some contradictions are good," but this seems to undermine the very notion of rational standards.
2. **Alternatives Exist**: Classical logic has responses to paradoxes without accepting true contradictions (e.g., hierarchical truth, contextualism, restriction of the T-schema). Why embrace dialetheism when other solutions are available?
3. **Pragmatic Absurdity**: Even if the Liar Sentence is both true and false, we don't *act* as though contradictions are acceptable in practical reasoning. Dialetheism is philosophically radical in a way that misaligns with practice.

**Diagnostic Markers**
- "The Liar Paradox shows that some sentences are genuinely both true and false; we should accept this." → strength: strong
- "If we're serious about semantics and the T-schema, true contradictions are inevitable." → strength: strong
- "Rejecting true contradictions forces us into artificial doctrines like truth-value gaps." → strength: moderate
- "Maybe only paradoxical sentences are true contradictions, not the entire world." → strength: moderate
- "Classical logic's refusal to accept what the paradoxes clearly show is evasive." → strength: strong

**Common Confusions**
Confused with simple error or confused reasoning (dialetheism is a deliberate metaphysical stance, not confused). Also confused with paraconsistency, which is compatible with dialetheism but does not entail it—one might be paraconsistent and reject true contradictions (using truth-value gaps instead). Sometimes conflated with relativism or the idea that contradictions are "true for someone."

**Cross-Branch Correlations**
- **Metaphysics of Truth**: Requires a non-standard conception of truth; classical theories (correspondence, coherence) assume bivalence.
- **Semantics**: Rejects hierarchical truth theories; embraces transparent, unified semantics that allows reflexivity.
- **Ontology**: May support disjunctive properties or allow objects to be and not be in given respects.
- **Philosophy of Mathematics**: If accepted, applies to naive set theory; Russell's Paradox generates true contradictions.

**Internal Variants**
- **Metaphysical Dialetheism**: Contradictions are genuine features of the world, especially in self-referential domains.
- **Semantic Dialetheism**: Contradictions arise in language/semantics but don't reflect metaphysical indeterminacy.
- **Trivialism (Extreme)**: Every proposition is both true and false (rarely endorsed; Priest rejects this).
- **Restricted Dialetheism**: Only paradoxical sentences are true contradictions; ordinary domains are classical.

## Spectrum Map

Classical Logic ←→ Non-Classical Variations

**Bivalence/Excluded Middle**
- **Classical Logic**: Strict bivalence; every proposition has exactly one truth value (T or F)
- **Fuzzy Logic**: Continuum of truth values; bivalence abandoned
- **Intuitionistic Logic**: Rejects Excluded Middle; truth indexed to constructive proof
- **Paraconsistent Logic**: Preserves bivalence (or multi-valued truth) but rejects Explosion
- **Modal Logic**: Classical core + modal operators; bivalence maintained within possible worlds
- **Dialetheism**: Rejects bivalence; accepts truth-value gluts (both T and F)

**Contradiction Tolerance**
- **Classical Logic**: Zero tolerance; Explosion ensures inconsistency → triviality
- **Paraconsistent Logic**: Localized contradiction permitted; Explosion blocked
- **Dialetheism**: True contradictions exist (metaphysical stance); paraconsistent logic required
- **Intuitionistic Logic**: Avoids contradictions through constructive proof standards
- **Fuzzy Logic**: No contradictions; indeterminacy is distinct from contradiction
- **Modal Logic**: Contradictions avoided at each world; modal operators navigate contradictions across worlds

**Metaphysical Commitment**
- **Classical Logic**: Determinate, bivalent reality; Law of Non-Contradiction holds universally
- **Intuitionistic Logic**: Anti-Platonic; constructibility is fundamental; mathematical objects are constructions
- **Fuzzy Logic**: Genuine indeterminacy in reality; boundaries are ontologically vague
- **Paraconsistent Logic**: Inconsistent theories can be true; localized inconsistency is metaphysically permissible
- **Modal Logic**: Possible worlds are real (or well-understood as abstract structures); necessity is irreducible
- **Dialetheism**: True contradictions exist; some propositions are both true and false

## Probing Questions

Use these to differentiate between schools in conversation:

1. **On the Liar Paradox** ("This sentence is false"):
   - *Classical Logician*: "It has no truth value or the English language fails; we should revise our semantics."
   - *Dialetheist*: "It's both true and false—that's the honest answer."
   - *Paraconsistent*: "It's both true and false, but that doesn't make classical logic applicable; we use paraconsistent logic."
   - *Intuitionistic*: "It's not constructively derivable; proof-theoretic semantics avoids the problem."

2. **On vague predicates (tallness, baldness)**:
   - *Classical Logician*: "There's a sharp boundary we haven't found; vagueness is epistemic, not real."
   - *Fuzzy Logician*: "Tallness admits degrees; a person can be 0.6 tall."
   - *Intuitionistic*: "We can't construct a proof of tallness for borderline cases, so we suspend judgment."
   - *Dialetheist*: "Some people are both tall and not-tall—there are true contradictions about boundaries."

3. **On the Law of Excluded Middle ("Either P or not-P")**:
   - *Classical Logician*: "Always true; it's a fundamental law of thought."
   - *Intuitionistic*: "Only assertable if we can prove P or prove not-P; undecidable propositions don't satisfy it."
   - *Fuzzy Logician*: "It's true, but P and not-P can each be partially true (e.g., both 0.5)."
   - *Modal Logician*: "Necessarily, P or not-P is true in any world, but P might be contingent."

4. **On inconsistent theories**:
   - *Classical Logician*: "Inconsistent theories are worthless; they imply everything."
   - *Paraconsistent*: "An inconsistent theory can be non-trivial and informationally valuable; it depends on how we structure logical consequence."
   - *Dialetheist*: "True inconsistencies exist; we need paraconsistent logic, but the theory can be about reality."
   - *Fuzzy Logician*: "Fuzziness isn't inconsistency; if the theory has indeterminacy, that's different."

5. **On necessity and possibility**:
   - *Classical Logician*: "Modal concepts are useful shorthand, but logic itself is classical; modality isn't primitive."
   - *Modal Logician*: "Necessity and possibility are irreducible; modal logic is foundational. Different logical systems (K, S4, S5) capture different modal structures."
   - *Intuitionistic*: "We should care about what's constructible, not metaphysical possibility. Modal talk is secondary to proof."
   - *Metaphysical Realist*: "Possible worlds are real or useful; modality is grounded in them."

6. **On mathematical existence**:
   - *Classical Logician*: "Mathematical objects exist in an abstract realm; classical logic captures their structure perfectly."
   - *Intuitionistic*: "Mathematics is a human constructive activity. Numbers and sets exist insofar as we can construct them."
   - *Platonist/Modal*: "Mathematical truths are necessary; they hold in all possible worlds."
   - *Fuzzy*: "Some mathematical properties (e.g., in fuzzy set theory) admit degrees."

7. **On what makes logic correct**:
   - *Classical Logician*: "Logic mirrors universal principles of reason; it's correct by being foundational to all thought."
   - *Intuitionistic*: "Logic must align with our constructive and epistemic capacities; it's correct when it respects proof."
   - *Pragmatist*: "Logic is correct when it's useful for reasoning in a domain; different logics suit different problems."
   - *Paraconsistent*: "Correctness is domain-specific; in domains with inconsistency, classical logic misfires."

## Scoring Rubric

### How to Score Signals

When a person makes a statement, map it to a diagnostic marker and assess strength:

**Strong Signal** (High Confidence)
- Direct assertion of a core principle (e.g., "Contradictions can't both be true" = Classical)
- Explicit rejection of an alternative school's key claim (e.g., "Excluded Middle must hold" = Classical)
- Commitment to a characteristic argument (e.g., "Paradoxes show true contradictions exist" = Dialetheism)

**Moderate Signal** (Medium Confidence)
- Endorsement of a characteristic concern (e.g., "Constructive proof matters" = Intuitionistic tendency)
- Pragmatic reasoning that aligns with a school's commitments (e.g., "We should use whichever logic works best" = Pluralist, possibly paraconsistent)
- Engagement with sophisticated formulations (e.g., "Different possible world models matter" = Modal sophistication)

**Weak Signal** (Low Confidence)
- Vague or inchoate commitment (e.g., "Maybe logic changes sometimes" = could be many things)
- Confusion about terminology (e.g., "Fuzzy logic is when I'm not sure" = folk probability, not necessarily fuzzy logic)
- Context-dependent reasoning that doesn't reveal systematic allegiance

### Integration Notes

- **Multiple Signals**: If a person shows strong signals in two schools (e.g., Modal + Classical), they likely hold a hybrid view—modal extensions of classical logic, or possible-world semantics with classical inference.
- **Context-Dependence**: Some thinkers are classical in ordinary domains but use non-classical logic for specific problems (paradoxes, vagueness, modality). Note this as "domain-specific" or "pluralist" rather than monolithic.
- **Confidence Calibration**: Strong signals in three or more different schools suggest either genuine pluralism or conceptual confusion. Probe further to distinguish.
- **Red Flags for Confusion**:
  - Conflating probability with fuzziness
  - Conflating uncertainty with truth-value gaps
  - Using "modal logic" to mean "maybe it's true"
  - Calling all non-classical logic "fuzzy"
